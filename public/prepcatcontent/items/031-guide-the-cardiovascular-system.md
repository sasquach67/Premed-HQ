## Heart Anatomy and the Cardiac Cycle

The human heart drives a double circulation. The right side of the heart receives deoxygenated blood from the body and pumps it to the lungs (the pulmonary circuit), while the left side receives oxygenated blood from the lungs and pumps it to the rest of the body (the systemic circuit). Because the systemic circuit is much larger and operates at higher pressure, the left ventricle has a far thicker, more muscular wall than the right ventricle, even though both ventricles eject the same stroke volume each beat.

Trace the path of a red blood cell: it enters the right atrium from the superior and inferior vena cavae, passes through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle, is pumped through the pulmonary (semilunar) valve into the pulmonary arteries to the lungs, returns oxygenated via the pulmonary veins into the left atrium, crosses the mitral (bicuspid) valve into the left ventricle, and is ejected through the aortic (semilunar) valve into the aorta. The atrioventricular (AV) valves are anchored by chordae tendineae and papillary muscles, which prevent the valve leaflets from prolapsing backward into the atria during the high pressure of ventricular contraction.

Tricuspid	Atrioventricular	Right atrium and right ventricle	S1 (with mitral)
Mitral (bicuspid)	Atrioventricular	Left atrium and left ventricle	S1 (with tricuspid)
Pulmonary	Semilunar	Right ventricle and pulmonary artery	S2 (with aortic)
Aortic	Semilunar	Left ventricle and aorta	S2 (with pulmonary)

## TRAP

Arteries carry blood away from the heart and veins carry blood toward it. This is defined by direction of flow, not oxygen content. The pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood and the pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood, the reverse of the usual pattern. The umbilical vessels in a fetus are the other exception: the umbilical vein carries oxygenated blood to the fetus and the umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood back to the placenta.

The cardiac cycle alternates between systole (ventricular contraction and ejection) and diastole (ventricular relaxation and filling). At the end of filling, the ventricle holds its end-diastolic volume (EDV); after ejection it retains its end-systolic volume (ESV). The volume actually pumped out per beat is the stroke volume. Most ventricular filling is passive, occurring early in diastole; the atrial contraction (the 'atrial kick') tops off the ventricle just before systole.

SV = EDV - ESV and EF = SV / EDV

Stroke volume is the difference between filled and residual volume; ejection fraction is the fraction of EDV ejected, normally about 55 to 70 percent at rest.

The two normal heart sounds come from valve closure, not from contraction itself. S1 ('lub') is the closure of the AV valves at the start of systole, and S2 ('dub') is the closure of the semilunar valves at the start of diastole. The coronary arteries, which supply the heart muscle itself, branch off the aorta just above the aortic valve. Because contracting ventricular muscle squeezes these vessels shut, the heart receives most of its own blood supply during diastole, which is one reason a very fast heart rate (shortening diastole) can compromise cardiac perfusion.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient has an end-diastolic volume of 130 mL and an end-systolic volume of 60 mL. Calculate the stroke volume and ejection fraction.

Stroke volume = EDV minus ESV = 130 minus 60 = 70 mL. Ejection fraction = SV divided by EDV = 70 / 130 = 0.54, or about 54 percent. This sits at the low end of the normal resting range (roughly 55 to 70 percent).

## Cardiac Conduction and Electrophysiology

Cardiac muscle is myogenic: it generates its own rhythm independent of nervous input. A specialized population of autorhythmic cells spontaneously depolarizes and sets the pace, while the bulk of contractile cardiac myocytes are electrically coupled through gap junctions at the intercalated discs. This coupling lets a wave of depolarization spread cell to cell so that the atria contract as one unit and the ventricles contract as one unit, a property called a functional syncytium.

The signal follows a fixed route. It begins at the sinoatrial (SA) node in the wall of the right atrium, spreads across both atria causing atrial contraction, and converges on the atrioventricular (AV) node. The AV node introduces a deliberate delay, the only normal electrical connection between atria and ventricles, which gives the atria time to finish emptying into the ventricles before the ventricles contract. From there the impulse races down the bundle of His, splits into the right and left bundle branches, and reaches the Purkinje fibers, which drive a rapid, coordinated ventricular contraction starting at the apex and moving upward to eject blood toward the outflow valves.

## KEY

The SA node is the heart's pacemaker because it depolarizes fastest, at an intrinsic rate near 100 beats per minute that resting vagal tone slows to the usual 60 to 100. If it fails, slower backup pacemakers take over: the AV node fires at roughly 40 to 60 per minute and ventricular (Purkinje) tissue at about 20 to 40 per minute. The fastest pacemaker always sets the heart rate and suppresses the slower ones.

Pacemaker cells do not have a stable resting potential. Their membrane slowly drifts upward during phase 4 because of the 'funny' current (a slow Na+ influx through HCN channels). When it reaches threshold, the upstroke is driven by Ca2+ influx, not the fast Na+ channels seen in nerve and skeletal muscle, and repolarization is driven by K+ efflux. The steepness of that phase 4 drift is what autonomic input changes to speed up or slow down the heart.

Ventricular contractile cell action potential

Phase	Name	Dominant ion movement
0	Upstroke	Rapid Na+ influx through fast voltage-gated Na+ channels
1	Early repolarization	Na+ channels inactivate; brief K+ efflux
2	Plateau	Ca2+ influx (L-type channels) balances K+ efflux
3	Repolarization	K+ efflux as Ca2+ channels close
4	Resting potential	Stable, set mainly by K+ (near -90 mV)

The phase 2 plateau is the signature feature of ventricular myocytes and the reason cardiac muscle cannot be tetanized. Sustained Ca2+ entry holds the membrane depolarized for a few hundred milliseconds, producing a long absolute refractory period that lasts nearly as long as the contraction itself. Because the cell cannot be re-excited until it has largely relaxed, the heart is forced to alternate contraction and relaxation, which it must do in order to refill. This is a key contrast with skeletal muscle, where rapid repeated stimulation can summate into sustained tetanus.

NORMAL ECG TRACING

Voltage versus time showing one cardiac cycle. The P wave reflects atrial depolarization; the QRS complex reflects ventricular depolarization and also hides atrial repolarization within it; the T wave reflects ventricular repolarization. The PR interval corresponds largely to the AV nodal delay, and the QT interval spans ventricular depolarization through repolarization.

Although the heart sets its own baseline rhythm, the autonomic nervous system tunes it. Sympathetic fibers release norepinephrine onto beta-1 receptors, steepening the pacemaker drift to raise heart rate (positive chronotropy) and increasing contractility (positive inotropy). Parasympathetic fibers in the vagus nerve release acetylcholine onto M2 muscarinic receptors of the SA and AV nodes, slowing the rate. At rest the heart is under continuous vagal (parasympathetic) tone, which is why the resting rate near 70 sits below the SA node's intrinsic rate.

## Hemodynamics: Cardiac Output, Pressure, and Resistance

Cardiac output is the volume of blood the heart pumps per minute and is the central measure of cardiac performance. At rest it averages about 5 liters per minute, which happens to equal the entire blood volume, so the whole blood supply circulates roughly once per minute. During heavy exercise cardiac output can rise several-fold through increases in both heart rate and stroke volume.

CO = HR x SV

Cardiac output (mL/min) equals heart rate (beats/min) times stroke volume (mL/beat).

Stroke volume is governed by three factors. Preload is the degree of ventricular stretch at the end of filling (essentially EDV) and is set by venous return. Afterload is the pressure the ventricle must overcome to eject blood, essentially aortic pressure. Contractility is the intrinsic strength of contraction at a given preload, increased by sympathetic stimulation. The Frank-Starling relationship links preload to output: greater filling stretches the cardiac muscle, which contracts more forcefully and ejects a larger stroke volume. This is what automatically matches the output of the right and left ventricles, so that whatever returns to the heart is pumped onward.

Blood pressure is usually reported as systolic over diastolic (a typical value is 120/80 mmHg). The difference between them is the pulse pressure. Because the body's organs are perfused throughout the whole cardiac cycle, the physiologically important average is the mean arterial pressure (MAP), not the simple average of systolic and diastolic. Diastole lasts longer than systole, so MAP is weighted toward the diastolic value.

MAP = DBP + (1/3)(SBP - DBP) = CO x TPR

MAP is closer to diastolic pressure because diastole occupies more of the cycle. The relation MAP = CO x TPR is the circulatory analog of Ohm's law, with pressure difference as voltage, flow as current, and total peripheral resistance as resistance.

Flow through a vessel is described by Poiseuille's law. The single most important term is the vessel radius raised to the fourth power: a small change in radius produces a huge change in flow and resistance. This is why arterioles, whose smooth muscle can constrict or dilate, are the body's main resistance vessels and the primary controllers of both regional blood flow and overall blood pressure. Viscosity also matters and rises with hematocrit, so an abnormally high red cell count increases resistance.

Q = (pi x deltaP x r^4) / (8 x eta x L)

Flow Q increases with pressure difference deltaP and the fourth power of radius r, and decreases with blood viscosity eta and vessel length L. Halving the radius cuts flow to 1/16 of its original value.

TIP

Do not confuse flow rate with flow velocity. By continuity, the total volume flow is constant across the system, so velocity is inversely related to total cross-sectional area. Capillaries have the largest combined cross-sectional area of any vessel type, so blood moves slowest there, which maximizes the time available for exchange. The aorta, with the smallest cross-sectional area, has the fastest velocity.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Heart rate is 80 beats per minute and stroke volume is 75 mL. Find the cardiac output. If total peripheral resistance then doubles while cardiac output is held constant, what happens to mean arterial pressure?

CO = HR x SV = 80 x 75 = 6000 mL/min = 6 L/min. Because MAP = CO x TPR, doubling TPR while CO is unchanged doubles the mean arterial pressure. This shows why vasoconstriction of the arterioles is such a powerful way to raise blood pressure.

## Blood Vessels and the Microcirculation

Blood vessels are specialized for their position in the circuit. Arteries leave the heart at high, pulsatile pressure and have thick walls rich in elastin and smooth muscle. The largest elastic arteries, such as the aorta, stretch during systole and recoil during diastole, smoothing the pulsatile output into more continuous flow (the Windkessel effect). Downstream, muscular arteries distribute blood to organs, and arterioles fine-tune how much reaches each tissue.

Vessel types compared

Vessel	Key wall feature	Pressure	Primary role
Elastic arteries (aorta)	Thick elastin; recoils	High, pulsatile	Dampen the pulse; maintain diastolic flow
Muscular arteries	Thick smooth muscle	High	Distribute blood to organs
Arterioles	Smooth muscle, small radius	Large pressure drop occurs here	Main resistance vessels; regulate flow and BP
Capillaries	Single endothelial cell layer	Low	Site of exchange; largest total cross-section, slowest flow
Veins and venules	Thin walls; one-way valves	Low	Capacitance vessels holding most of the blood volume

Veins are thin-walled, low-pressure capacitance vessels that hold roughly two-thirds of the body's blood volume at any moment, acting as a reservoir. Because venous pressure is low, returning blood to the heart depends on accessory mechanisms: one-way valves prevent backflow, the skeletal muscle pump squeezes veins during movement, and the respiratory pump uses pressure changes in the chest during breathing to draw blood toward the heart. Anything that reduces venous return lowers preload and, through Frank-Starling, reduces cardiac output.

Exchange between blood and tissue happens across capillary walls and is governed by Starling forces, a balance of two opposing pressures. Capillary hydrostatic pressure pushes fluid out of the capillary into the interstitium. Plasma colloid osmotic pressure (oncotic pressure), generated mainly by large plasma proteins such as albumin that cannot easily cross the wall, pulls fluid back in. The net direction of fluid movement is the difference between these forces.

## KEY

Along a capillary, hydrostatic pressure falls from the arterial end to the venous end, while oncotic pressure stays roughly constant. At the arterial end hydrostatic pressure dominates, so fluid filters out; at the venous end oncotic pressure dominates, so fluid is reabsorbed. Filtration slightly exceeds reabsorption overall, and the lymphatic system collects the leftover interstitial fluid and returns it to the bloodstream.

Edema, the accumulation of excess interstitial fluid, results whenever this balance tips toward net filtration or drainage fails. The classic causes are increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (for example, in heart failure or venous obstruction), decreased plasma oncotic pressure (from low protein states such as liver disease or malnutrition), increased capillary permeability (as in inflammation, which lets protein leak out), and blocked lymphatic drainage.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient with advanced liver disease synthesizes much less albumin than normal. Predict the effect on capillary fluid balance and explain the mechanism.

Albumin is the dominant contributor to plasma colloid osmotic (oncotic) pressure. With less albumin, oncotic pressure falls, so the inward pull at the venous end weakens and reabsorption decreases. Hydrostatic filtration now outpaces reabsorption, fluid accumulates in the interstitium, and edema develops. This is why low-protein states characteristically cause swelling.

## Blood Composition and Gas Transport

Whole blood separates into plasma, about 55 percent by volume, and formed elements, about 45 percent. The fraction of blood volume made up of red blood cells is the hematocrit, typically around 40 to 45 percent. Plasma is mostly water carrying dissolved electrolytes, nutrients, hormones, wastes, and proteins. The major plasma proteins are albumin (which maintains oncotic pressure and transports many molecules), globulins (including antibodies), and fibrinogen (a clotting precursor).

Red blood cells (erythrocytes) are biconcave discs that, in mammals, lack a nucleus and other organelles, leaving maximum room for hemoglobin and a large surface area for gas exchange. They live about 120 days and are produced in the bone marrow by erythropoiesis, which is stimulated by erythropoietin (EPO), a hormone the kidneys release when they sense low oxygen. White blood cells (leukocytes) provide immune defense, and platelets (cell fragments from megakaryocytes) initiate clotting.

Hemoglobin is the oxygen carrier. Each adult hemoglobin molecule has four globin subunits (two alpha and two beta), and each subunit holds one heme group with a central iron (Fe2+) that binds one O2. So one hemoglobin carries up to four O2 molecules. Binding is cooperative: as each O2 binds, it changes the protein's shape and raises the affinity of the remaining sites. This cooperativity is what gives the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve its characteristic S shape.

OXYGEN-HEMOGLOBIN DISSOCIATION CURVE

Percent hemoglobin saturation plotted against partial pressure of oxygen. The curve is sigmoidal: it is steep in the middle and plateaus at high PO2. In the lungs (PO2 near 100 mmHg) hemoglobin is roughly 98 percent saturated and loads O2; in active tissue (PO2 near 40 mmHg) saturation drops onto the steep part and O2 is unloaded. A right shift moves the curve down and to the right, indicating decreased affinity and greater unloading.

## TRAP

A right shift of the curve means lower oxygen affinity, so hemoglobin releases more O2 at a given PO2, which is good for delivery to tissues. The factors that cause a right shift all signal active metabolism: increased CO2, decreased pH (more acidic), increased temperature, and increased 2,3-BPG. This is the Bohr effect. A left shift means higher affinity (better loading, less unloading). Fetal hemoglobin and carbon monoxide both left-shift the curve; do not assume a right shift is always harmful, it is exactly what working muscle needs.

Carbon dioxide travels in the blood three ways, and the dominant route involves red blood cells. Inside the RBC, the enzyme carbonic anhydrase rapidly converts CO2 and water into carbonic acid, which dissociates into bicarbonate and a proton. The bicarbonate is exported into the plasma in exchange for chloride entering the cell, an electroneutral swap called the chloride shift. This system also makes bicarbonate the body's main blood buffer, linking gas transport directly to acid-base balance.

Forms of CO2 transport in blood

Form	Approximate fraction	Notes
Bicarbonate (HCO3-)	about 70 percent	Made by carbonic anhydrase in RBCs; involves the chloride shift
Carbaminohemoglobin	about 23 percent	CO2 bound to the globin protein amino groups, not to the heme iron
Dissolved CO2	about 7 percent	Physically dissolved in plasma; sets the PCO2

CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-

Catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase. In tissues the reaction runs to the right (loading CO2); in the lungs it runs to the left so CO2 can be exhaled. The reverse reaction is the Haldane effect counterpart: deoxygenated hemoglobin carries CO2 more readily.

WORKED EXAMPLE

During intense exercise a muscle becomes warmer, more acidic, and rich in CO2. How does this local environment affect oxygen delivery to that muscle?

Higher temperature, lower pH, and higher CO2 all right-shift the oxygen-hemoglobin curve (the Bohr effect), lowering hemoglobin's O2 affinity. At the same tissue PO2, hemoglobin now releases more oxygen, so unloading is enhanced precisely in the tissue with the greatest metabolic demand. The system delivers more O2 exactly where it is needed.

## Regulation of Blood Pressure

Blood pressure must be held within a narrow range: too low and tissues are underperfused, too high and vessels are damaged. The body controls it on two timescales. Fast, second-to-second control comes from neural reflexes that adjust heart rate and vessel diameter. Slower, longer-term control comes from hormones that regulate blood volume, mainly by changing how much salt and water the kidneys retain. Because MAP equals cardiac output times total peripheral resistance, every regulator works by changing CO, TPR, or blood volume.

The fastest mechanism is the baroreceptor reflex. Baroreceptors are stretch-sensitive nerve endings in the walls of the carotid sinus and the aortic arch. When blood pressure rises, the vessel walls stretch more and the receptors fire faster, signaling the medulla to dial down sympathetic output and increase parasympathetic (vagal) output. The result is a slower heart rate, reduced contractility, and vasodilation, all of which bring pressure back down. A fall in pressure does the opposite.

## KEY

The baroreceptor reflex is a negative feedback loop and the reason blood pressure does not swing wildly when you stand up. Standing causes blood to pool in the legs, dropping pressure; baroreceptors sense the drop and trigger increased sympathetic tone to raise heart rate and constrict vessels, restoring perfusion to the brain. When this reflex is sluggish, people feel lightheaded on standing (orthostatic hypotension).

For longer-term, volume-based control the key system is the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). When the kidneys detect low blood pressure or low blood flow, they release renin. Renin converts angiotensinogen into angiotensin I, and angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), found largely in the lung capillaries, converts it into angiotensin II. Angiotensin II is a potent vasoconstrictor that also stimulates the adrenal cortex to release aldosterone, triggers ADH release, and stimulates thirst. Aldosterone makes the kidneys reabsorb sodium, and water follows, expanding blood volume and raising pressure.

Two other hormones fine-tune volume. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH, or vasopressin), released from the posterior pituitary in response to high plasma osmolarity or low blood volume, makes the kidneys reabsorb more water and also constricts vessels, both raising pressure. Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) does the opposite: when the atria are stretched by high blood volume, they release ANP, which promotes sodium and water excretion and causes vasodilation, lowering pressure. ANP therefore acts as a natural counterbalance to the RAAS.

Major blood pressure regulators

System	Trigger	Main action	Net effect on BP	Speed
Baroreceptor reflex	Change in arterial wall stretch	Adjusts sympathetic and parasympathetic output	Restores BP toward set point	Seconds (fast)
RAAS	Low renal perfusion or low Na+	Angiotensin II vasoconstriction plus aldosterone Na+ and water retention	Raises BP and volume	Minutes to hours
ADH (vasopressin)	High plasma osmolarity or low volume	Renal water reabsorption plus vasoconstriction	Raises BP and volume	Minutes to hours
ANP	Atrial stretch from high volume	Na+ and water excretion plus vasodilation	Lowers BP and volume	Minutes

Layered on top of these system-wide controls is local autoregulation, by which individual tissues set their own blood flow to match their needs. Metabolically active tissue accumulates signals such as low O2, high CO2, low pH, adenosine, and nitric oxide, which relax the local arteriolar smooth muscle and increase flow to that region. Meanwhile, generalized sympathetic activation constricts most vessels through alpha-1 receptors but can dilate skeletal-muscle vessels through beta-2 receptors, redirecting blood toward muscle during a fight-or-flight response.

TIP

On exam questions, classify each regulator before reasoning. Ask whether it acts fast (neural reflexes, sympathetic and parasympathetic) or slow (hormonal volume control), and whether it raises pressure (sympathetic activation, RAAS, ADH) or lowers it (parasympathetic activation, ANP). Then trace the effect through MAP = CO x TPR to predict the outcome.
