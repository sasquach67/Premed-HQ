# Mendelian Genetics & Inheritance

Mendelian genetics describes how discrete units of heredity (genes) pass from parents to offspring through the laws of segregation and independent assortment, and how we predict trait ratios using Punnett squares, pedigrees, linkage maps, and the Hardy-Weinberg equation. The MCAT tests it heavily because it integrates probability, meiosis, biochemistry (alleles to proteins to phenotypes), and population genetics into quantitative problems you must solve quickly and accurately.

## Foundations: Genes, Alleles, and Mendel's Two Laws

Mendelian genetics is the framework for predicting how traits pass from one generation to the next. Before doing any cross, you must nail the vocabulary, because the MCAT writes questions assuming you can translate fluently between these terms. A gene is a unit of heredity that codes for a product (usually a protein, sometimes a functional RNA). The specific position of a gene on a chromosome is its locus. Different versions of the same gene are alleles, they arise from differences in DNA sequence at that locus.

Because humans (and Mendel's pea plants) are diploid, every individual carries two alleles for each autosomal gene, one inherited from each parent. If the two alleles are identical, the individual is homozygous (AA or aa); if they differ, the individual is heterozygous (Aa). The genotype is the actual allele combination an organism carries; the phenotype is the observable trait that results. A crucial MCAT point: identical phenotypes can hide different genotypes, a dominant phenotype could be AA or Aa. The only way to distinguish them is the cross history (a pedigree) or a test cross.

Mendel deduced two laws from carefully tracking pea traits across generations, and both map directly onto meiosis. The Law of Segregation states that the two alleles for a gene separate (segregate) from each other during gamete formation, so each gamete receives only ONE allele. Physically, this happens when homologous chromosomes, each carrying one allele, are pulled to opposite poles at anaphase I of meiosis. The Law of Independent Assortment states that alleles of different genes are distributed to gametes independently of one another. This corresponds to the random orientation of the homologous pairs on the metaphase I plate. Critically, independent assortment holds ONLY for genes on different chromosomes (or genes very far apart on the same chromosome); genes close together on the same chromosome are linked and tend to be inherited together, a theme we return to in the linkage section.

## KEY

Segregation traces to anaphase I (homologs separate → one allele per gamete). Independent assortment traces to metaphase I (random orientation of the different homolog pairs). Tying each law to a meiotic stage is a favorite MCAT integration point.

Allele	Alternative version of a gene	A, B, or O (i) allele

## TRAP

Do not say a heterozygote 'has the recessive allele blocked.' In complete dominance, the dominant allele typically makes enough functional product on its own (haplosufficiency), so the recessive allele's reduced or absent product simply does not change the phenotype. The recessive allele is still present and usually still transcribed, it just isn't seen. Recessive does NOT mean inactive or rare.

## Punnett Squares: Monohybrid and Dihybrid Crosses

A Punnett square is a bookkeeping grid for combining gametes. You list the possible gametes from each parent along the two axes, then fill each cell with the offspring genotype formed by combining that row's and column's allele. The power of the tool is that it converts a question about meiosis and fertilization into simple counting of cells. By convention, the dominant allele is written with a capital letter and listed first.

The classic monohybrid cross is between two heterozygotes: Aa × Aa. Each parent produces two gamete types (A and a) in equal proportion. Filling the 2×2 square yields 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa, the 1:2:1 genotypic ratio. Because A is completely dominant, AA and Aa look the same, collapsing the phenotype ratio to 3 dominant : 1 recessive (the famous 3:1). Memorize that pairing: 3:1 phenotype with 1:2:1 genotype is the signature of a single-gene cross between two heterozygotes.

### FIGURE: MONOHYBRID PUNNETT SQUARE (AA × AA)

A 2×2 grid. The top is labeled with one parent's gametes A and a; the left side with the other parent's gametes A and a. The four cells read AA (top-left), Aa (top-right), Aa (bottom-left), aa (bottom-right). The figure visually shows why the genotype ratio is 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa and the phenotype ratio is 3 dominant : 1 recessive, three of the four cells contain at least one dominant A allele, and only the bottom-right aa cell shows the recessive phenotype.

A dihybrid cross tracks two genes at once, e.g., AaBb × AaBb. When the two genes assort independently, you CAN draw a 16-cell (4×4) grid, but you should not need to, use the rule of multiplication instead. Treat each gene as its own monohybrid cross (each gives a 3:1 phenotype ratio) and multiply the per-gene probabilities. P(A_) = 3/4 and P(aa) = 1/4; same for B. So P(A_B_) = 3/4 × 3/4 = 9/16, P(A_bb) = 3/4 × 1/4 = 3/16, P(aaB_) = 3/16, and P(aabb) = 1/4 × 1/4 = 1/16, the signature 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio. This multiplicative shortcut is far faster than drawing and tallying a 4×4 square and is exactly how the MCAT expects you to reason under time pressure.

P(both events) = P(event 1) × P(event 2) [multiplication / 'AND' rule, independent events]

Use when you need two independent outcomes simultaneously, e.g., genotype aa AND genotype bb. Multiply the separate probabilities.

P(either event) = P(event 1) + P(event 2) [addition / 'OR' rule, mutually exclusive events]

Use when an outcome can arise in multiple mutually exclusive ways, e.g., a child is AA OR Aa. Add the probabilities (valid only when the events cannot co-occur).

WORKED EXAMPLE

Two parents are both heterozygous for two independently assorting genes: AaBb × AaBb. What is the probability of an offspring that is homozygous recessive for the first gene AND shows the dominant phenotype for the second gene (i.e., genotype aaB_)?

Break it into two independent single-gene problems and multiply.

First gene: Aa × Aa → P(aa) = 1/4.
Second gene: Bb × Bb → P(B_, the dominant phenotype) = 3/4.

Because the two genes assort independently, combine with the AND (multiplication) rule:
P(aaB_) = 1/4 × 3/4 = 3/16.

This is exactly one of the '3' classes in the 9:3:3:1 ratio. Notice you never had to draw the 16-box square, recognizing independence and applying the product rule is the intended, time-efficient method.

TIP

When a question asks 'at least one' (e.g., at least one recessive child among several), compute 1 − P(none). Direct enumeration of every 'at least one' case wastes time; the complement is almost always faster and less error-prone.

A test cross is the experimental complement to the Punnett square: to determine whether an organism showing the dominant phenotype is AA or Aa, cross it with a homozygous recessive (aa). If ANY recessive offspring appear, the unknown parent must have carried an a allele (genotype Aa). If all offspring show the dominant phenotype, the parent is probably AA, though, especially with few offspring, you can never fully exclude Aa, since a heterozygote could by chance produce no recessive offspring. More offspring give more confidence. This is the standard way to unmask a hidden heterozygote.

## Beyond Simple Dominance: Incomplete, Codominance, Multiple Alleles & Gene Interactions

Real inheritance is often messier than dominant/recessive, and the MCAT loves the patterns that break Mendel's clean 3:1. The key is to recognize each pattern by its phenotypic signature and its molecular basis.

In incomplete dominance, the heterozygote shows an intermediate, blended phenotype. The classic example is a red-flowered (RR) plant crossed with a white-flowered (rr) plant producing pink (Rr) offspring. Molecularly, the single functional allele makes only about half the pigment, not enough for full red. A defining consequence: the genotype and phenotype ratios become IDENTICAL. A cross of two pink plants (Rr × Rr) gives 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white, a 1:2:1 phenotype ratio rather than 3:1.

In codominance, the heterozygote expresses BOTH alleles fully and separately, there is no blending into an intermediate. The human ABO blood group illustrates this: the Iᴬ and Iᴮ alleles are codominant, so genotype Iᴬ Iᴮ produces type AB blood with both A and B surface antigens displayed side by side. Sickle-cell is the other classic example, but its classification depends on the level of analysis: at the protein level it is codominant, because heterozygotes (HbA/HbS) make both normal and sickle hemoglobin and both are directly detectable by electrophoresis, whereas at the level of clinical disease the sickle allele behaves recessively (you need two HbS alleles for sickle-cell anemia, though heterozygotes can sickle under extreme low-oxygen stress). The MCAT most often cites sickle cell to make the point that the SAME allele can look dominant, recessive, or codominant depending on which phenotype you measure.

Distinguishing the Dominance Patterns

Pattern	Heterozygote phenotype	Het × het ratio	Example
Complete dominance	Looks like the dominant homozygote	3:1 phenotype	Pea seed shape (round vs wrinkled)
Incomplete dominance	Intermediate / blended	1:2:1 phenotype	Snapdragon color (red/pink/white)
Codominance	Both alleles fully and separately expressed	1:2:1 phenotype	ABO blood (type AB)

The ABO system also illustrates multiple alleles: a single gene with more than two possible alleles in the population (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, and i). Any one diploid person still carries only two of them, but the population pool has three. Iᴬ and Iᴮ are codominant with each other and both are dominant over the recessive i. This produces four phenotypes (A, B, AB, O) from six genotypes, a frequent MCAT problem (see the worked example below).

Several gene-interaction phenomena extend Mendel further. Pleiotropy is one gene affecting multiple, seemingly unrelated traits, the sickle-cell mutation affects RBC shape, causes anemia and painful vaso-occlusive crises, and confers malaria resistance, all from one nucleotide change. Polygenic inheritance is one trait controlled by many genes, producing continuous variation (height, skin color); these show bell-curve distributions rather than discrete categories. Epistasis is when one gene masks or modifies the expression of another, DIFFERENT gene (e.g., a gene controlling whether any pigment is deposited can mask the gene that specifies which color, an albino animal's color genotype is present but hidden). Distinguish epistasis (interaction BETWEEN two different genes) from dominance (interaction between two ALLELES of the SAME gene), the MCAT deliberately blurs these.

## TRAP

Codominance vs incomplete dominance: both give a 1:2:1 phenotype ratio, so the RATIO alone cannot distinguish them. The difference is mechanistic, incomplete dominance produces a NEW, intermediate phenotype (pink), while codominance shows BOTH parental phenotypes at once (type AB blood has distinct A and B antigens, not a blended 'AB' antigen). If the heterozygote is a brand-new in-between trait, it's incomplete dominance; if both original traits appear together, it's codominance.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A woman with type A blood and a man with type B blood have a child with type O blood. What are the parents' genotypes, and what is the probability their next child has type O blood?

Type O blood is genotype ii (homozygous recessive). The child is ii, so the child received one i allele from each parent. Therefore each parent must carry an i allele.

The mother has type A blood, so she must be Iᴬ i (not Iᴬ Iᴬ, since she passed an i). The father has type B blood, so he must be Iᴮ i.

Cross Iᴬ i × Iᴮ i. The four equally likely offspring genotypes are:
Iᴬ Iᴮ (type AB), Iᴬ i (type A), Iᴮ i (type B), and i i (type O).

Each occurs with probability 1/4, so the probability of a type O (ii) child is 1/4. Note that this single cross can produce all four blood types, a detail the MCAT likes to test.

## Sex-Linked Inheritance, Penetrance, and Expressivity

Genes on the sex chromosomes follow distinctive inheritance patterns because males (XY) and females (XX) carry different numbers of them. Almost all medically important sex-linked genes are on the X chromosome (the Y is gene-poor and mostly carries male-determining genes). Males are hemizygous for X linked genes, they have only one X, so a single recessive allele is fully expressed with no second allele to mask it. This is why X linked recessive disorders (hemophilia A/B, red-green color blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, G6PD deficiency) are far more common in males.

The transmission pattern is the diagnostic fingerprint. For X linked recessive traits: (1) affected males vastly outnumber affected females; (2) there is NO male-to-male (father-to-son) transmission of the trait, because a father gives his son a Y, not an X; (3) affected sons typically inherit the allele from an unaffected carrier mother; (4) for a daughter to be affected, her father must be affected AND her mother must be a carrier or affected. An affected father passes his single X (carrying the trait allele) to ALL of his daughters, making every daughter an obligate carrier, another commonly tested point.

### FIGURE: X LINKED RECESSIVE CROSS (CARRIER MOTHER × UNAFFECTED FATHER)

A Punnett square with the carrier mother's gametes Xᴬ and Xᵃ across the top and the unaffected father's gametes Xᴬ and Y down the side. The cells produce: Xᴬ Xᴬ (unaffected daughter), Xᴬ Xᵃ (carrier daughter), Xᴬ Y (unaffected son), Xᵃ Y (AFFECTED son). The figure shows the hallmark outcome: among sons, half are affected and half unaffected; among daughters, none are affected but half are carriers. This is why X linked recessive disease appears to 'skip' through carrier females while striking males.

## KEY

X linked recessive shortcut: a carrier mother (Xᴬ Xᵃ) and an unaffected father give 1/2 of SONS affected and 0 daughters affected (but 1/2 of daughters carriers). 'No father-to-son transmission' is the single fastest way to flag X-linkage on a pedigree, sons get only the father's Y.

Two related concepts describe how reliably a genotype produces its phenotype. Penetrance is the proportion of individuals with a given genotype who actually show the expected phenotype (an all-or-nothing, population-level measure). If only 80% of people with a disease genotype develop symptoms, the trait is incompletely (80%) penetrant, this is one way a pedigree can appear to 'skip' a generation even without sex linkage. Expressivity is the DEGREE or range of the phenotype among individuals who do express it (a variable-intensity, per-individual measure). Polydactyly, for example, varies in how many extra digits appear and on which limbs, variable expressivity. Keep them straight: penetrance = whether it shows at all; expressivity = how strongly it shows.

## TRAP

An apparent generation 'skip' on a pedigree has multiple explanations: a recessive trait passing silently through heterozygous carriers, incomplete penetrance of a dominant trait, or X linked recessive transmission through carrier females. Don't assume 'skipping' automatically means simple autosomal recessive, read the whole pedigree before committing to a mode.

## Genetic Linkage, Recombination, and Gene Mapping

Independent assortment fails when two genes sit close together on the same chromosome, they tend to be inherited together as a unit. Such genes are linked. The main thing that separates linked alleles is crossing over (recombination) during prophase I of meiosis, when homologous chromosomes exchange segments. The closer two genes are, the less likely a crossover falls between them, and the more tightly they are co-inherited.

We quantify this with recombination frequency. After a cross (typically a test cross, so genotypes are readable directly from offspring phenotypes), classify offspring as parental types (allele combinations identical to a parent) or recombinant types (new combinations produced by crossing over). The recombination frequency is the fraction that are recombinant. Because crossover probability scales with physical distance, recombination frequency serves as a proxy for map distance: 1% recombination = 1 map unit = 1 centimorgan (cM).

Recombination frequency (%) = (number of recombinant offspring / total offspring) × 100 = map distance in cM

Recombinant offspring carry allele combinations NOT present in either parent. The two parental (non-recombinant) classes are always the most numerous; the two recombinant classes are the least numerous. 1% = 1 cM = 1 map unit.

Recombination frequency has a maximum of 50%. At 50%, the genes behave as if unlinked, either they sit on different chromosomes, or they are so far apart on the same chromosome that at least one crossover between them is essentially guaranteed, randomizing the combinations. So 50% recombination = independent assortment, and you cannot distinguish 'far apart on the same chromosome' from 'on different chromosomes' by recombination frequency alone.

Recombination frequencies are approximately additive over short distances, which lets you build linear gene maps. If A B = 5 cM and B C = 8 cM, and B lies between A and C, then A C is roughly 13 cM. Additivity breaks down over longer distances because double crossovers between two genes restore the parental arrangement and go undetected, causing measured distance to UNDERESTIMATE true distance. Three-point crosses solve this by using the smallest interval distances to order genes and refine the map. For the MCAT you mainly need to: compute recombination frequency, convert to cM, and order three genes by their pairwise distances.

WORKED EXAMPLE

In a test cross, you score 1000 offspring: 410 of one parental type, 390 of the other parental type, 105 of one recombinant type, and 95 of the other recombinant type. What is the map distance between the two genes?

First identify the classes by frequency. Parental types are the most frequent: 410 and 390 (total 800). Recombinant types are the least frequent: 105 and 95.

Total recombinant offspring = 105 + 95 = 200.
Total offspring = 1000.

Recombination frequency = (200 / 1000) × 100 = 20%.

Since 1% recombination = 1 cM, the genes are 20 cM (20 map units) apart. Sanity check: 20% is well below the 50% maximum, so the genes are indeed linked, not assorting independently.

TIP

Identify parental vs recombinant by counting: the two MOST common offspring classes are parental, the two LEAST common are recombinant. Add BOTH recombinant classes over the total. Forgetting to include both recombinant classes (using only one) is the most common arithmetic slip on these problems, it halves your answer.

## Hardy Weinberg Equilibrium and Population Genetics

Mendel's laws describe individuals and families; Hardy Weinberg (HW) scales the logic up to whole populations. The HW principle states that in an idealized, non-evolving population, allele and genotype frequencies stay constant from generation to generation. It gives you a baseline 'null model', if real frequencies deviate from HW predictions, some evolutionary force must be acting.

For a gene with two alleles, let p = frequency of the dominant allele and q = frequency of the recessive allele. Because every allele in the pool is one or the other, p + q = 1. The genotype frequencies in the next generation follow from randomly pairing gametes, which is the binomial expansion (p + q)² = p² + 2pq + q² = 1.

p + q = 1 and p² + 2pq + q² = 1

p = frequency of dominant allele, q = frequency of recessive allele. p² = frequency of homozygous dominant (AA), 2pq = frequency of heterozygotes/carriers (Aa), q² = frequency of homozygous recessive (aa). Together the two equations let you solve for everything from a single piece of data.

The standard problem-solving entry point: the recessive PHENOTYPE corresponds to genotype aa, whose frequency is q². So if you're told how common the recessive condition is, you have q² directly. Take the square root to get q, subtract from 1 to get p, then compute p² (homozygous dominant) and especially 2pq (carrier frequency), the carrier frequency is the most frequently asked quantity, because carriers are 'hidden' heterozygotes invisible to the phenotype. (One caution: q² = recessive phenotype only when the trait shows complete dominance, so that AA and Aa are indistinguishable; the MCAT virtually always sets up its problems this way.)

The Five Hardy Weinberg Assumptions (all must hold)

Assumption	If violated →	Real-world example of violation
No mutation	Allele frequencies change	New disease allele arises
No gene flow (migration)	Alleles added or removed	Immigration into a population
No natural selection	Favored alleles increase	Antibiotic resistance selected for
Random mating	Genotype frequencies shift	Assortative mating, inbreeding
Large (effectively infinite) population	Genetic drift changes frequencies	Bottleneck or founder effect

## KEY

A population in Hardy Weinberg equilibrium is, by definition, NOT evolving. The five assumptions are exactly the five mechanisms of evolution turned 'off': mutation, gene flow, natural selection, non-random mating, and genetic drift (small populations). 'Name a force that would violate HW' is a guaranteed MCAT question, note that non-random mating shifts genotype frequencies but, by itself, does not change ALLELE frequencies.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A recessive autosomal disease affects 1 in 10,000 people. Assuming Hardy Weinberg equilibrium, what fraction of the population are unaffected carriers?

Affected individuals are homozygous recessive (aa), so q² = 1/10,000 = 0.0001.

Solve for q: q = √0.0001 = 0.01.
Then p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.01 = 0.99.

Carriers are heterozygotes (Aa) with frequency 2pq:
2pq = 2 × 0.99 × 0.01 = 0.0198 ≈ 0.02, or about 2% of the population (roughly 1 in 50).

Key insight: carriers (~1 in 50) are about 198 times more common than affected individuals (1 in 10,000). For a rare recessive allele, heterozygous carriers vastly outnumber affected homozygotes, a heavily tested conceptual point.

## TRAP

For a rare allele, p ≈ 1, so the carrier frequency 2pq ≈ 2q. Students often confuse q (the ALLELE frequency) with q² (the frequency of the affected PHENOTYPE/genotype). The disease prevalence you're given is almost always q², not q, square-root it FIRST. And re-read the question: it usually asks for 2pq (carriers), not q² (affected) or p² (homozygous unaffected).

## Pedigree Analysis: Reading Inheritance Patterns

A pedigree is a family tree annotated with phenotypes, used to deduce a trait's mode of inheritance. Standard symbols: squares = males, circles = females, shaded/filled = affected, unfilled = unaffected; horizontal lines connect mates, vertical lines descend to offspring, and Roman numerals label generations. Your job is to test the trait against the possible inheritance modes, autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X linked recessive, X linked dominant, and eliminate those that contradict the data. A single contradictory individual can rule out an entire mode, so actively hunt for the disqualifying case.

Pedigree Pattern Cheat Sheet

Mode	Telltale signs	Quick disqualifier
Autosomal recessive	Can skip generations; affected child from two unaffected (carrier) parents; M and F equally affected	Two AFFECTED parents with an UNAFFECTED child rules it OUT
Autosomal dominant	Every affected child has ≥1 affected parent; no skipping (full penetrance); M and F equally affected	An affected child from two UNAFFECTED parents rules it OUT
X linked recessive	Mostly males affected; no father→son transmission; carrier mothers	Affected daughter with an UNAFFECTED father rules it OUT
X linked dominant	Affected fathers pass to ALL daughters and NO sons; more females affected	Father→son transmission rules it OUT

A systematic three-question approach handles most pedigrees. First, dominant or recessive? If two unaffected parents have an affected child, the trait must be recessive (the parents were carriers). If two affected parents have an unaffected child, the trait must be dominant (the parents were heterozygous). Second, autosomal or X linked? Look for father-to-son transmission: if an affected father has an affected son for the SAME trait, it cannot be X linked (sons get only the father's Y), pushing you toward autosomal. A strong male bias among the affected suggests X linked recessive. Third, check consistency across the entire pedigree, then assign genotypes to compute any requested probabilities.

WORKED EXAMPLE

In a pedigree, two unaffected parents have four children: two affected sons, one unaffected son, and one unaffected daughter. No affected females appear anywhere in the pedigree, and affected individuals are exclusively male across several generations. What is the most likely mode of inheritance?

Step 1, dominant or recessive? Two UNAFFECTED parents produced AFFECTED children. Affected offspring from unaffected parents means the trait is RECESSIVE (the parents are unaffected carriers). This eliminates both dominant modes.

Step 2, autosomal or X linked? Affected individuals are essentially all male, and the unaffected mother evidently passed the allele to her affected sons. A strong/exclusive male bias points to X-LINKED RECESSIVE: the mother is a carrier (Xᴬ Xᵃ), and each son has a 1/2 chance of inheriting Xᵃ and being affected (hemizygous). Daughters would need an affected father AND a carrier mother to be affected, which explains the absence of affected females here. (Autosomal recessive is not strictly impossible, but it would not predict such a consistent male-only pattern, so X linked recessive is the best fit.)

Step 3, consistency check. The unaffected father (Xᴬ Y) gives sons only his Y, so all affected sons received Xᵃ from their mother, with no father-to-son transmission of the trait. Everything fits.

Conclusion: X linked recessive.

TIP

When a pedigree asks for the probability that a specific individual is a carrier or affected, first assign every possible genotype using the deduced inheritance mode, then apply conditional probability, restrict to the outcomes consistent with what's already known (e.g., 'this person is unaffected') and renormalize. For an unaffected child of two Aa carriers, the possible genotypes are 1 AA : 2 Aa; given 'unaffected,' the aa option is removed, so P(carrier) = 2/3, not 1/2.

Two more inheritance patterns occasionally appear. Mitochondrial (cytoplasmic) inheritance is strictly maternal: because the egg contributes essentially all of the zygote's cytoplasm and mitochondria, mitochondrial-DNA traits pass from an affected mother to ALL of her children, while an affected father transmits to NONE. On a pedigree this looks like transmission only through females, affecting both sexes (expression can vary due to heteroplasmy, the mix of mutant and normal mitochondria). Y linked traits, by contrast, pass strictly father-to-son and affect only males. Recognizing the 'all children of affected mothers, no children of affected fathers' signature of mitochondrial inheritance is the high yield takeaway.
