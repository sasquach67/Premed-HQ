# Cognition, Language, and Intelligence

Cognition spans how thinking develops (Piaget and Vygotsky), how we solve problems and fall into heuristic-driven biases, how intelligence is theorized and measured, and how language is structured, acquired, and localized in the brain. This guide ties those threads together with the high-yield distinctions the MCAT tests most.

## Cognition and Cognitive Development

Cognition refers to all the mental activity associated with thinking, knowing, remembering, and communicating, which is to say how the brain acquires, processes, stores, and uses information. The dominant framework is the information-processing model, which treats the mind like a computer that encodes input, stores it, and retrieves it to guide behavior (the storage and retrieval mechanics themselves are developed in the Memory guide). Unlike a serial computer, however, the brain performs massive parallel processing, handling many streams of information at once. Cognitive psychology studies how these processes develop, how we represent knowledge as concepts and schemas, and how thinking can be both powerfully efficient and systematically error-prone.

Jean Piaget proposed that children are active thinkers who construct understanding through schemas, the mental frameworks that organize knowledge. New experiences are handled by two complementary processes: assimilation, fitting new information into an existing schema (a child calls a zebra a 'horse'), and accommodation, modifying or creating a schema when new information does not fit (the child revises the schema to include 'zebra'). When experiences clash with existing schemas, the resulting cognitive disequilibrium drives the child toward equilibration, motivating a reorganization of thought. Piaget argued that cognition advances through four qualitatively distinct, universal stages that occur in a fixed order.

Piaget's Four Stages of Cognitive Development

Stage	Approx. Age	Hallmark Abilities	Key Limitations
Sensorimotor	Birth to 2 yr	Experiences world through senses and motor action; object permanence develops	Stranger anxiety; early lack of object permanence
Preoperational	2 to 7 yr	Symbolic thought, pretend play, language explosion	Egocentrism, no conservation, centration, animism
Concrete operational	7 to 11 yr	Conservation, reversibility, logical reasoning about concrete events	Cannot reason abstractly or hypothetically
Formal operational	12 yr and up	Abstract, hypothetical, and deductive reasoning; systematic problem solving	Adolescent egocentrism may persist

Several signature tasks mark the transitions between stages. Object permanence, acquired during the sensorimotor stage, is the understanding that objects continue to exist when out of sight; before it develops, 'out of sight' is literally 'out of mind.' Conservation, achieved in the concrete operational stage, is the recognition that quantity (number, volume, mass) stays constant despite changes in shape or arrangement; a preoperational child insists a tall, thin glass holds 'more' water than a short, wide one because they fixate on a single dimension, an error called centration. Egocentrism, characteristic of preoperational thought, is the inability to take another person's visual or mental perspective. Related but distinct is theory of mind, the capacity to attribute beliefs, desires, and intentions to others, which emerges around age four or five and is classically tested with false-belief tasks.

Lev Vygotsky offered a sociocultural alternative that emphasizes social interaction and culture rather than solitary discovery. His central concept is the zone of proximal development (ZPD): the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance from a more knowledgeable other. The temporary support an adult or peer provides, and gradually withdraws, is called scaffolding. For Vygotsky, language is the primary tool of thought, and internalized speech (private speech becoming inner speech) drives cognitive development, making culture and instruction central engines of growth.

## KEY

Memorize Piaget's stages in order with one signature ability each: Sensorimotor = object permanence, Preoperational = symbolic thought but egocentric with no conservation, Concrete operational = conservation and reversibility, Formal operational = abstract and hypothetical reasoning. The MCAT loves to describe a child's behavior and ask for the stage.

## TRAP

Do not confuse object permanence with conservation. Object permanence (objects exist when unseen) is a sensorimotor achievement in infancy. Conservation (quantity is unchanged despite a change in appearance) is a concrete operational achievement around age seven. A child who fails the water-glass task lacks conservation, not object permanence.

## Problem Solving, Heuristics, and Biases

When we face a problem, several strategies are available. Trial and error tries possibilities until one works, which is reliable for small problem spaces but inefficient for large ones. An algorithm is a step-by-step procedure that guarantees a correct solution if followed (like a formula or an exhaustive search), but it can be slow. A heuristic is a mental shortcut, or rule of thumb, that is fast and usually effective but does not guarantee a correct answer. Insight, by contrast, is the sudden 'aha' realization of a solution without conscious step-by-step reasoning. Heuristics make cognition efficient, but they are also the source of predictable biases.

Two heuristics dominate MCAT questions. The availability heuristic judges the likelihood or frequency of an event by how easily examples come to mind; because vivid, recent, or emotional events are more memorable, people overestimate dramatic risks (plane crashes, shark attacks) and underestimate common ones. The representativeness heuristic judges the probability that something belongs to a category by how well it matches the prototype or stereotype of that category, while ignoring actual statistical frequencies. This produces the base-rate fallacy: neglecting the underlying base rate (how common each category actually is) in favor of a superficial resemblance.

Beyond heuristics, several biases systematically distort reasoning. Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that supports existing beliefs while avoiding disconfirming evidence. Belief perseverance is clinging to a belief even after the evidence that formed it has been discredited. Overconfidence is overestimating the accuracy of one's own judgments. The framing effect shows that how a choice is worded (90% survival vs 10% mortality) changes decisions even when the facts are identical. Anchoring is over-relying on the first piece of information encountered, and hindsight bias is the 'I knew it all along' feeling after an outcome is known.

Two obstacles specifically block problem solving. A mental set is the tendency to approach a new problem using a strategy that worked in the past, even when a simpler or different approach would work better. Functional fixedness is a special case: the inability to see an object being used for anything other than its usual function, such as failing to realize a coin could serve as a screwdriver. Recognizing both is the first step to overcoming them through flexible, divergent thinking.

Common Heuristics and Cognitive Biases

Term	Definition	Quick Example
Availability heuristic	Estimate likelihood by ease of recall	Fearing plane crashes after seeing one in the news
Representativeness heuristic	Judge by resemblance to a prototype, ignoring base rates	Assuming a quiet reader is a librarian, not a farmer
Base-rate fallacy	Ignoring actual category frequencies	Overlooking that farmers vastly outnumber librarians
Confirmation bias	Favoring belief-consistent evidence	Reading only news that agrees with you
Belief perseverance	Holding a belief after it is disproven	Keeping a first impression after a retraction
Framing effect	Wording changes the decision	Choosing '90% lean' over '10% fat'

Dual-process theory organizes these phenomena into two modes of thought. System 1 is fast, automatic, effortless, and intuitive; it generates heuristics and gut feelings. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful, and analytical; it applies algorithms and logical rules. Intuition reflects System 1, and most cognitive biases arise when System 1 answers a hard question with an easy substitute and System 2 fails to override it. With practice, effortful System 2 reasoning can shift into fast, reliable System 1 pattern recognition, which is part of what makes someone an expert.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Steve is described by a neighbor as shy, withdrawn, helpful but with little interest in people, and a passion for detail and order. Asked whether Steve is more likely a librarian or a farmer, most people say librarian. Why is this judgment likely an error?

It relies on the representativeness heuristic: Steve resembles the prototype of a librarian, so people assign him to that category. But this ignores the base rate, because there are far more farmers than librarians in the population. Even if shy, orderly people are somewhat more common among librarians, the sheer number of farmers means a randomly described shy person is still statistically more likely to be a farmer. Neglecting that base rate is the base-rate fallacy.

## TRAP

Availability and representativeness are easy to swap. Availability is about ease of recall (how readily examples come to mind). Representativeness is about resemblance to a prototype (how well something fits a stereotype). If the scenario stresses 'memorable, recent, or vivid,' it is availability; if it stresses 'matches the type of person or thing,' it is representativeness.

## Theories of Intelligence

Psychologists disagree about whether intelligence is one thing or many. Charles Spearman, using factor analysis, found that people who score well on one type of mental test tend to score well on others, leading him to propose a general intelligence factor, g, that underlies all cognitive abilities, alongside specific abilities (s) tied to particular tasks. L.L. Thurstone challenged a single g, arguing instead for seven independent primary mental abilities (such as verbal comprehension, numerical ability, and spatial visualization), though his factors still correlated, which partially supported g.

Raymond Cattell distinguished two components of g. Fluid intelligence (Gf) is the capacity to reason quickly and solve novel problems independent of prior knowledge; it tends to peak in early adulthood and decline with age. Crystallized intelligence (Gc) is accumulated knowledge, skills, and vocabulary built through experience and education; it remains stable or even increases into late adulthood. This distinction explains why older adults may struggle with novel puzzles yet outperform younger adults on knowledge-based tasks.

Howard Gardner proposed the theory of multiple intelligences, arguing that intelligence is not a single capacity but a set of relatively independent domains, commonly listed as linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, and naturalist. Evidence comes partly from savant syndrome, in which a person limited in most areas shows an exceptional, isolated skill. Critics note that these 'intelligences' may be better described as talents and that the domains still tend to correlate with one another.

Robert Sternberg's triarchic theory describes three intelligences: analytical intelligence (academic problem solving, the kind tested by traditional IQ), creative intelligence (generating novel ideas and adapting to new situations), and practical intelligence (the 'street smarts' needed for everyday tasks and reading social context). Sternberg emphasized that real-world success draws heavily on the creative and practical abilities that standard tests largely miss.

Emotional intelligence, developed by Peter Salovey and John Mayer and popularized by Daniel Goleman, is the ability to perceive, understand, manage, and use emotions in oneself and others. It includes self-awareness, self-regulation, empathy, and social skill, and high emotional intelligence predicts social and occupational success beyond what IQ alone explains. Critics argue it stretches the definition of 'intelligence' too far, blurring the line between ability and personality.

Theories of Intelligence at a Glance

Theorist	Theory	Core Idea
Spearman	General intelligence (g)	A single underlying factor drives all mental abilities
Thurstone	Primary mental abilities	Seven independent factors, not one g
Cattell	Fluid vs crystallized	Gf = novel reasoning (declines); Gc = knowledge (stable)
Gardner	Multiple intelligences	Eight independent domains (musical, spatial, and so on)
Sternberg	Triarchic theory	Analytical, creative, and practical intelligence
Salovey & Mayer	Emotional intelligence	Perceive, understand, manage, and use emotions

## KEY

Frame the debate as 'one vs many.' Spearman's g says intelligence is essentially one general factor; Gardner and Sternberg say it is several independent abilities; Cattell splits g itself into fluid (novel reasoning, declines with age) and crystallized (accumulated knowledge, stays stable). Knowing which camp a theorist belongs to answers most MCAT questions.

## Intelligence Testing and Heritability

Modern intelligence testing began in 1905 when Alfred Binet and Theodore Simon built a test to identify French schoolchildren needing extra help. They introduced mental age, the chronological age that best matches a child's test performance. Lewis Terman of Stanford revised the test for American use, creating the Stanford-Binet, and championed its broad application. The original aim was practical educational placement, not the ranking of innate worth, a nuance often lost in later misuses of the tests.

IQ = (mental age / chronological age) x 100

Ratio IQ, introduced by William Stern. It works for children but breaks down in adulthood, since mental age plateaus while chronological age keeps rising, which is why modern tests use deviation IQ instead.

David Wechsler developed the most widely used adult test, the WAIS (with the WISC for children), reporting separate verbal and performance scores plus a full-scale IQ. Rather than a ratio, Wechsler tests use a deviation IQ that compares a person to their same-age peers. Scores are set by standardization on a representative sample, producing a normal distribution (bell curve) with a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of 15 for the Wechsler scales (the historical Stanford-Binet used a standard deviation of 16). About 68% of people score within one standard deviation (85 to 115) and about 95% within two (70 to 130).

A useful test must be both reliable and valid. Reliability is consistency: a test is reliable if it yields similar results on retesting (test-retest reliability), across two halves of the test (split-half reliability), or across alternate forms. Validity is accuracy, meaning whether the test measures what it claims to. Content validity covers the relevant domain, while predictive (criterion) validity is how well scores forecast an outcome such as academic performance. A test can be highly reliable yet invalid (consistently measuring the wrong thing), but it cannot be valid without first being reliable.

The Flynn effect is the observation that average IQ scores have risen substantially over the past century, by roughly three points per decade in industrialized nations. Because the gains are too rapid to be genetic, they are attributed to environmental factors such as improved nutrition, education, healthcare, and greater familiarity with abstract, test-like reasoning. The effect is why tests must be periodically re-standardized so the population mean stays at 100.

Twin and adoption studies show that intelligence has a substantial genetic component: identical twins raised apart resemble each other in IQ far more than unrelated individuals do, and heritability estimates for adults commonly fall around 50 to 80%. Crucially, heritability is the proportion of variation in a trait within a population that is attributable to genes; it is not a statement about any single individual and does not mean intelligence is fixed or that differences between groups are genetic. Environment still matters enormously, as the Flynn effect demonstrates. Performance can also be depressed by stereotype threat, the anxiety, shown by Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson, that arises when people fear confirming a negative stereotype about their group, which can lower test scores independent of actual ability.

## TRAP

Heritability is the most misread term in this topic. A heritability of 0.7 means 70% of the variation among people in a population is linked to genetic differences; it does NOT mean any one person's intelligence is '70% genes,' and it does NOT mean differences between groups are genetic. High heritability is fully compatible with large environmental effects, as the Flynn effect shows.

WORKED EXAMPLE

An 8-year-old child performs on an intelligence test at the level of a typical 10-year-old. Using the original ratio formula, what is the child's IQ, and why would this formula fail for a 40-year-old?

Ratio IQ = (mental age / chronological age) x 100 = (10 / 8) x 100 = 125, so the child scores above average. The formula fails in adulthood because mental age levels off after adolescence while chronological age keeps increasing; a 40-year-old with a mental age of 20 would compute to an IQ of 50 despite normal ability. This is exactly why modern tests abandon the ratio and use deviation IQ, comparing each person to same-age peers.

## Language: Components and Development

Language is a system of symbols (spoken, written, or signed) combined by rules to communicate meaning. Psycholinguists analyze it on several levels, from the smallest sound units up to social use. The hierarchy builds from phonemes (units of sound) to morphemes (units of meaning) to whole sentences governed by syntax, all of it deployed appropriately in context through pragmatics.

Building Blocks of Language

Component	Definition	Example
Phoneme	Smallest distinct unit of sound	The /b/ vs /p/ in 'bat' vs 'pat'
Morpheme	Smallest unit of meaning	'cats' = 'cat' + plural '-s' (two morphemes)
Semantics	Meaning of words and sentences	'plant' as a living thing vs a factory
Syntax	Rules for word order and structure	'The dog bit the man' vs 'The man bit the dog'
Grammar	Full rule system (syntax plus semantics)	The rules that make a sentence well-formed
Pragmatics	Practical, social use and context	'Can you pass the salt?' understood as a request

Language development follows a remarkably universal timetable. Newborns cry; around 2 months infants coo, producing vowel-like sounds; around 4 to 6 months they babble, producing consonant-vowel combinations that initially include sounds from all languages and then narrow to those of the household language. Near 12 months children enter the one-word, or holophrastic, stage, using single words to convey whole ideas. Around 18 to 24 months they reach the two-word, telegraphic stage, stringing together content words ('want cookie') while omitting function words, much like a telegram.

As toddlers learn grammar, they show overregularization (also called overgeneralization): applying a rule too broadly, as in 'goed,' 'runned,' or 'foots.' Far from a simple mistake, this signals that the child has internalized a grammatical rule rather than merely imitating adults, who never say 'goed.' Language acquisition also appears to have a critical (or sensitive) period: children who are not exposed to language early in life, or who begin a second language after roughly puberty, rarely attain native-like fluency, suggesting the developing brain is specially primed for language early on.

## KEY

Know the milestone order cold: crying, then cooing (~2 mo), then babbling (~4 to 6 mo), then one-word/holophrastic (~1 yr), then two-word/telegraphic (~2 yr). Overregularization ('goed,' 'foots') is evidence of rule learning rather than imitation and is a favorite MCAT detail.

## TRAP

Phoneme vs morpheme: a phoneme is the smallest unit of SOUND (no meaning by itself), while a morpheme is the smallest unit of MEANING. The word 'dogs' has four phonemes (d-o-g-z) but only two morphemes ('dog' plus plural '-s'). Sound equals phoneme, meaning equals morpheme.

## Theories of Language and the Brain

How do children acquire language so quickly? B.F. Skinner offered a behaviorist (nurture) account: language is learned through operant conditioning, with imitation, reinforcement, and association shaping correct speech. Noam Chomsky countered with a nativist account: humans are born with an innate language acquisition device (LAD) and a universal grammar, a built-in framework of grammatical rules common to all languages. Chomsky's key argument is the poverty of the stimulus: children produce and understand novel sentences they have never heard and master grammar faster than reinforcement could explain, and errors like 'goed' are never modeled by adults.

An interactionist view blends the two, holding that an innate biological readiness interacts with rich social experience (echoing Vygotsky) to drive acquisition. A separate question is whether language shapes thought. Benjamin Whorf's linguistic relativity hypothesis proposed that the language we speak influences how we perceive and think about the world. The strong version, linguistic determinism, in which language fully determines thought, is largely rejected, but a weaker version, that language can influence cognition (for example, the color categories or spatial terms a language provides), has experimental support.

Language is strongly lateralized to the left hemisphere in most people. Broca's area, in the left frontal lobe near the motor cortex, governs speech production. Damage causes Broca's (expressive, nonfluent) aphasia: speech is halting, effortful, and telegraphic, and producing grammatical sentences is difficult, but comprehension remains largely intact. Patients are typically aware of and frustrated by their deficit.

Wernicke's area, in the left temporal lobe, governs language comprehension. Damage causes Wernicke's (receptive, fluent) aphasia: speech is fluent and grammatically smooth but full of invented or incorrect words and empty of meaning ('word salad'), and comprehension is severely impaired, with patients often unaware of their errors. The arcuate fasciculus is the nerve bundle connecting Wernicke's and Broca's areas; damaging it produces conduction aphasia, in which comprehension and fluent speech are preserved but the patient cannot repeat words. Damage to both areas yields global aphasia.

Aphasia Types

Aphasia	Area Damaged	Speech	Comprehension	Repetition
Broca's (expressive)	Left frontal (Broca's)	Nonfluent, effortful	Intact	Impaired
Wernicke's (receptive)	Left temporal (Wernicke's)	Fluent but meaningless	Impaired	Impaired
Conduction	Arcuate fasciculus	Fluent	Intact	Impaired
Global	Both Broca's and Wernicke's	Nonfluent	Impaired	Impaired

WORKED EXAMPLE

A stroke patient speaks in long, fluent, grammatically smooth sentences that are full of made-up words and convey almost no meaning. She does not seem to realize anything is wrong and cannot follow spoken commands. Which aphasia is this, and where is the lesion?

This is Wernicke's (receptive, fluent) aphasia. The hallmarks are fluent but meaningless speech ('word salad'), impaired comprehension, and lack of awareness of the deficit. The lesion is in Wernicke's area in the left temporal lobe. Contrast this with Broca's aphasia, where speech is nonfluent and effortful but comprehension is preserved; a useful mnemonic is that Broca's gives 'Broken' speech.

## TRAP

Broca's vs Wernicke's is a guaranteed MCAT distinction. Broca's is a production problem: nonfluent, effortful, 'Broken' speech, but comprehension is intact and the patient knows it. Wernicke's is a comprehension problem: fluent but meaningless speech and impaired understanding, with the patient unaware. Frontal = produce, Temporal = understand.
