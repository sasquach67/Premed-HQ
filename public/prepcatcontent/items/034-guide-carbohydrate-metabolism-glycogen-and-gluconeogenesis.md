# Carbohydrate Metabolism: Glycogen and Gluconeogenesis

How the body stores and rebuilds glucose: glycogen synthesis and breakdown, their hormonal and allosteric control, and gluconeogenesis from non-carbohydrate precursors. Emphasis on the liver-versus-muscle division of labor and the reciprocal regulation that keeps storage and production from running at once.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Liver glycogen buffers blood glucose for the whole body because the liver has glucose-6-phosphatase; muscle glycogen serves only muscle, which lacks that enzyme.
Glycogen phosphorylase (breakdown) and glycogen synthase (synthesis) are the rate-limiting, reciprocally regulated enzymes; phosphorylation activates phosphorylase and inactivates synthase.
Glycogen phosphorylase uses phosphorolysis (inorganic phosphate, not ATP) to release glucose-1-phosphate and requires pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6).
Gluconeogenesis bypasses the three irreversible glycolytic steps with four enzymes: pyruvate carboxylase, PEP carboxykinase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, and glucose-6-phosphatase, costing six ATP equivalents per glucose.
Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate reciprocally controls the pathways: it activates PFK-1 (glycolysis) and inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (gluconeogenesis); glucagon lowers it, insulin raises it.
Acetyl-CoA from even-chain fatty acids cannot become net glucose; the glucogenic precursors are lactate, glycerol, and glucogenic amino acids such as alanine.
Glucagon and epinephrine raise cAMP and activate PKA to mobilize glycogen; insulin activates protein phosphatase 1 to store it.

## Glucose Homeostasis and the Division of Labor

Beyond burning glucose in glycolysis, the body must hold blood glucose near 4 to 5 mM around the clock. This matters because the brain and red blood cells rely on glucose as their primary or sole fuel: red blood cells have no mitochondria, and the brain cannot use fatty acids. Two strategies meet this demand. Glycogen, a branched polymer of glucose, is a fast-access short-term reserve. Gluconeogenesis, the synthesis of new glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors, is the sustained source when stores run low.

The timeline maps onto these tools. In the fed state, insulin drives glucose into cells and promotes glycogen synthesis (glycogenesis). In early fasting, the liver breaks down glycogen (glycogenolysis), a reserve that lasts roughly 12 to 24 hours. During prolonged fasting, glycogen is exhausted and gluconeogenesis becomes the dominant glucose source, supplemented by ketone bodies that spare glucose for the brain.

The liver acts as the body's glucostat. It both stores glycogen and runs gluconeogenesis, and it can release free glucose into the blood. Skeletal muscle is a selfish consumer: it stores large amounts of glycogen but keeps that glucose for its own contraction. The kidney cortex performs a meaningful minority of gluconeogenesis, especially in prolonged starvation and acidosis.

## KEY

The single most tested fact in this topic: only the liver (and kidney and intestine) express glucose-6-phosphatase, so only they can dephosphorylate glucose-6-phosphate and export free glucose. Muscle lacks this enzyme, so muscle glycogen can never directly raise blood glucose.

## Glycogen Structure and Synthesis (Glycogenesis)

Glycogen is a highly branched polymer of glucose. Straight chains are joined by alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds, and branches sprout from alpha-1,6 bonds roughly every 8 to 12 residues. Branching is not cosmetic: it increases solubility and, critically, creates many non-reducing ends. Because the enzymes that build and dismantle glycogen work only at non-reducing ends, more ends mean faster simultaneous synthesis or mobilization. A glycogen molecule has just one reducing end, anchored to its core protein.

BRANCHED ARCHITECTURE OF GLYCOGEN

A treelike polymer radiating from a central glycogenin protein. Linear runs of glucose are linked alpha-1,4; branch points where a new chain departs are linked alpha-1,6 and recur about every 8 to 12 residues. The many outer tips are the non-reducing ends where synthase and phosphorylase act; the single buried connection to glycogenin is the lone reducing end.

Glycogen synthase cannot start a chain from scratch; it can only extend an existing one. The primer is glycogenin, a protein that autoglucosylates, attaching the first several glucose units to one of its own tyrosine residues. Glycogen synthase then takes over to elongate the chain.

Glycogenesis proceeds in steps. Glucose is first trapped as glucose-6-phosphate by hexokinase (or glucokinase in the liver). Phosphoglucomutase then shifts the phosphate to make glucose-1-phosphate. Glucose-1-phosphate reacts with UTP, catalyzed by UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase, to form UDP-glucose, the activated glucose donor, releasing pyrophosphate (PPi).

glucose-1-phosphate + UTP -> UDP-glucose + PPi ; then PPi + H2O -> 2 Pi

Hydrolysis of pyrophosphate by inorganic pyrophosphatase removes a product and pulls the reaction forward, making UDP-glucose synthesis effectively irreversible.

Glycogen synthase, the rate-limiting enzyme of synthesis, transfers glucose from UDP-glucose onto the non-reducing end of a growing chain, forming a new alpha-1,4 bond. It builds only linear chains. Once a chain is long enough, branching enzyme (amylo-(1,4 to 1,6)-transglycosylase) detaches a block of about 6 to 7 terminal residues and reattaches it through an alpha-1,6 bond, at least four residues away from an existing branch, creating a new branch point.

## KEY

Cost accounting: adding one glucose to glycogen costs about two ATP equivalents, one to phosphorylate glucose (hexokinase) and one to regenerate UTP from UDP. UDP-glucose, not glucose-6-phosphate, is the activated building block for the polymer.

## Glycogen Breakdown (Glycogenolysis)

Breakdown is run by glycogen phosphorylase, the rate-limiting enzyme of glycogenolysis. It cleaves alpha-1,4 bonds from the non-reducing ends by phosphorolysis, using inorganic phosphate to release glucose-1-phosphate one unit at a time. It cannot cut alpha-1,6 branch points and stalls four residues from a branch, leaving a structure called a limit dextrin.

Phosphorolysis is chemically clever. By attacking with phosphate rather than water, the product emerges already phosphorylated as glucose-1-phosphate, so no ATP is spent to phosphorylate it later. Phosphorylation also keeps the glucose charged and trapped inside the cell rather than leaking out through membrane transporters.

## KEY

Glycogen phosphorylase requires pyridoxal phosphate (PLP), the active form of vitamin B6, as a cofactor. This is a favorite vitamin-to-enzyme link on test day.

The branch points are handled by debranching enzyme, a single bifunctional protein. Its 4-alpha-glucanotransferase activity moves a block of three glucose residues from the branch onto a nearby chain end, and its alpha-1,6-glucosidase activity then hydrolyzes the lone remaining alpha-1,6-linked glucose, releasing it as free glucose. As a result, roughly 90 percent of mobilized glucose leaves as glucose-1-phosphate and about 10 percent as free glucose from the branch points.

Phosphoglucomutase converts glucose-1-phosphate to glucose-6-phosphate, the same metabolic crossroad used in synthesis. From here the fate diverges by tissue.

In the liver, glucose-6-phosphatase in the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum strips the phosphate, and the resulting free glucose is exported to the blood to support other tissues. In muscle, which lacks this enzyme, glucose-6-phosphate cannot be dephosphorylated, so it instead feeds directly into glycolysis to make ATP for contraction.

## TRAP

Common mistake: assuming muscle glycogen helps replenish blood sugar. It does not do so directly. Muscle can contribute indirectly only by exporting lactate to the liver (the Cori cycle), where gluconeogenesis remakes glucose.

## Hormonal and Allosteric Regulation

Three hormones set the tone. Glucagon (acting on the liver) and epinephrine (acting on both liver and muscle) signal a need for fuel and turn glycogen breakdown on. Insulin signals abundance and turns storage on. The hormones converge on the phosphorylation state of two enzymes, glycogen phosphorylase and glycogen synthase.

The activating signal is an amplifying cascade. Glucagon or epinephrine binds a G-protein-coupled receptor, the stimulatory G protein (Gs) activates adenylyl cyclase, cAMP rises, and protein kinase A (PKA) is switched on. PKA phosphorylates and activates phosphorylase kinase, which in turn phosphorylates glycogen phosphorylase, converting the less active b form to the active a form. Each step multiplies the signal, so a few hormone molecules mobilize a large amount of glucose.

TIP

Decision shortcut: figure out the phosphorylation state from the hormone, then read off the enzymes. Phosphorylation (glucagon or epinephrine, via PKA) means breakdown ON and synthesis OFF, because PKA also directly phosphorylates and inactivates glycogen synthase. Dephosphorylation (insulin, via protein phosphatase 1) means the reverse.

Active versus inactive enzyme forms

Enzyme	More active form	Less active form	Effect of phosphorylation
Glycogen phosphorylase	a (phosphorylated)	b (dephosphorylated)	Activates
Glycogen synthase	a (dephosphorylated)	b (phosphorylated)	Inactivates
Phosphorylase kinase	Phosphorylated or Ca2+-bound	Dephosphorylated	Activates

Allosteric controls let the cell's own energy state override hormones. In resting muscle, phosphorylase b is allosterically activated by AMP (a low-energy signal) and inhibited by ATP and glucose-6-phosphate (high-energy signals). In the liver, free glucose binds phosphorylase a and inhibits it, so a well-fed liver stops degrading glycogen even before insulin fully acts. Glycogen synthase, conversely, is allosterically activated by glucose-6-phosphate, so abundant substrate favors storage.

Muscle adds a contraction-linked control. Phosphorylase kinase contains a calmodulin subunit, so the same Ca2+ that triggers muscle contraction also activates phosphorylase kinase independently of cAMP. This couples glycogen breakdown directly to the moment of muscle activity, with epinephrine reinforcing it.

Insulin reverses the whole scheme. It activates protein phosphatase 1 (PP1), which dephosphorylates the enzymes: glycogen synthase becomes active and glycogen phosphorylase becomes inactive. Insulin also lowers cAMP by activating phosphodiesterase, draining the signal that drives PKA.

WORKED EXAMPLE

During a sprint, epinephrine binds beta-adrenergic receptors on a muscle cell and intracellular Ca2+ rises. Predict the activity of glycogen phosphorylase and glycogen synthase and name the key signaling steps.

Epinephrine activates Gs, then adenylyl cyclase, raising cAMP and activating PKA. PKA phosphorylates and activates phosphorylase kinase, which converts glycogen phosphorylase b to the active a form, so glycogenolysis turns ON. PKA also phosphorylates glycogen synthase, converting it to the inactive b form, so synthesis turns OFF. Simultaneously, Ca2+ released for contraction binds the calmodulin subunit of phosphorylase kinase and activates it independently of cAMP, tightly coupling breakdown to contraction. Rising AMP from ATP consumption further activates muscle phosphorylase b allosterically. Net result: rapid glucose-6-phosphate production to fuel glycolysis in the working muscle.

## Gluconeogenesis: Building Glucose Anew

Gluconeogenesis makes glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors, chiefly lactate, glycerol, and glucogenic amino acids such as alanine. It runs mostly in the liver, with a smaller contribution from the kidney cortex. It is largely the reverse of glycolysis and shares seven of its enzymes, but it cannot simply run glycolysis backward, because three glycolytic steps are strongly exergonic and irreversible. Four gluconeogenic enzymes bypass them.

The first two bypasses convert pyruvate to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). Pyruvate carboxylase, a mitochondrial, biotin-dependent enzyme, uses ATP to carboxylate pyruvate to oxaloacetate; it is allosterically activated by acetyl-CoA. Oxaloacetate is then shuttled out of the mitochondrion (commonly as malate) and PEP carboxykinase (PEPCK) uses GTP to convert it to PEP.

The remaining two bypasses are simple dephosphorylations. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase removes a phosphate from fructose-1,6-bisphosphate to give fructose-6-phosphate; this is the key regulatory step of the pathway. Finally, glucose-6-phosphatase in the endoplasmic reticulum converts glucose-6-phosphate to free glucose, which only the liver, kidney, and intestine can do.

The four gluconeogenic bypass enzymes

Enzyme	Reaction	Glycolytic step bypassed	Cofactor / location
Pyruvate carboxylase	Pyruvate to oxaloacetate	Pyruvate kinase	Biotin, ATP; mitochondria; activated by acetyl-CoA
PEP carboxykinase (PEPCK)	Oxaloacetate to PEP	Pyruvate kinase	GTP; cytosol and mitochondria
Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase	F1,6BP to F6P	Phosphofructokinase-1	Cytosol; main control point
Glucose-6-phosphatase	G6P to glucose	Hexokinase / glucokinase	ER lumen; liver, kidney, intestine

2 pyruvate + 4 ATP + 2 GTP + 2 NADH + 2 H+ + 6 H2O -> glucose + 4 ADP + 2 GDP + 6 Pi + 2 NAD+

Six high-energy phosphate bonds (4 ATP plus 2 GTP) plus 2 NADH are spent per glucose, whereas glycolysis nets only 2 ATP. That asymmetry is why the two pathways must be reciprocally regulated.

## TRAP

Acetyl-CoA from even-chain fatty acids cannot be made into net glucose, because pyruvate dehydrogenase is irreversible and the two carbons entering the TCA cycle as acetyl-CoA are lost as CO2. Glycerol (from fat lipolysis) and the propionyl-CoA from odd-chain fatty acids are glucogenic, but fatty acids as a class are not.

Regulation mirrors the energy logic. Pyruvate carboxylase is switched on by acetyl-CoA, which accumulates when fatty acid oxidation is high, the hallmark of fasting. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase is inhibited by AMP and by fructose-2,6-bisphosphate and activated by citrate. Cortisol supports the pathway over hours by mobilizing amino acid substrate and inducing gluconeogenic enzymes.

WORKED EXAMPLE

How many high-energy phosphate bonds are consumed to build one glucose from two pyruvate molecules?

Pyruvate carboxylase uses 1 ATP per pyruvate, so 2 ATP. PEPCK uses 1 GTP per oxaloacetate, so 2 GTP. Reversing phosphoglycerate kinase uses 1 ATP per 3-phosphoglycerate, so 2 more ATP. Total: 4 ATP plus 2 GTP equals 6 high-energy phosphate bonds (plus 2 NADH consumed). Since glycolysis yields only 2 ATP, the cell pays a 4 ATP-equivalent premium to run glucose synthesis, which is why glycolysis and gluconeogenesis cannot productively run at the same time.

## Integration: Reciprocal Control, Substrate Cycles, and Disease

The master switch between glycolysis and gluconeogenesis is fructose-2,6-bisphosphate. It is made and destroyed by a single bifunctional enzyme with PFK-2 and FBPase-2 activities. In the liver, glucagon drives PKA to phosphorylate this enzyme, which turns off PFK-2 and turns on FBPase-2, so fructose-2,6-bisphosphate falls. Insulin promotes dephosphorylation, raising it. The molecule itself activates PFK-1 (glycolysis) and inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (gluconeogenesis).

## KEY

Remember fructose-2,6-bisphosphate as the fed-state, insulin-high signal: high levels push glycolysis (activate PFK-1) and brake gluconeogenesis (inhibit FBPase-1). Glucagon lowers it to do the opposite.

The Cori cycle links the two pathways across organs. Muscle and red blood cells running anaerobic glycolysis export lactate to the liver, where gluconeogenesis converts it back to glucose that returns to those tissues. The net effect shifts the metabolic ATP burden of glucose regeneration from muscle to the liver, letting muscle keep contracting under oxygen debt.

The glucose-alanine cycle carries both carbon and nitrogen. In muscle, amino groups from amino acid breakdown are transferred to pyruvate to form alanine, which travels to the liver. There, transamination regenerates pyruvate for gluconeogenesis while the nitrogen is funneled into the urea cycle for safe disposal.

High-yield glycogen storage diseases

Type / name	Deficient enzyme	Key features
I (Von Gierke)	Glucose-6-phosphatase	Severe fasting hypoglycemia, hepatomegaly, lactic acidosis
II (Pompe)	Lysosomal acid alpha-glucosidase (acid maltase)	Cardiomegaly, hypotonia; lysosomal, spares blood glucose
III (Cori)	Debranching enzyme	Milder hypoglycemia, short-branched limit dextrin buildup
V (McArdle)	Muscle glycogen phosphorylase	Exercise intolerance, cramps, no blood lactate rise on exertion

These diseases are pathway logic in clinical form. Von Gierke disease blocks the final exit step shared by glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, so the liver cannot release free glucose at all, producing profound fasting hypoglycemia and a backlog that drives lactic acidosis. McArdle disease disables only muscle glycogen breakdown, so patients tolerate rest but cramp during exercise and, tellingly, show no rise in blood lactate when they exert. Reasoning from which enzyme is missing to the expected symptom is exactly how the MCAT tests this material.
