# Cell membranes & transport

The cell membrane is a selectively permeable phospholipid bilayer that controls what enters and leaves the cell, and the MCAT tests how its structure dictates transport, from passive diffusion and osmosis to ATP-driven pumps that build the ion gradients underlying membrane potential. Mastering tonicity, the difference between channels and carriers, and the Nernst/electrochemical logic of membrane potential pays off across biology, biochemistry, and physiology passages.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Passive transport moves a substance DOWN its electrochemical gradient with NO ATP (simple diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion); active transport moves it UP its gradient and needs energy, ATP directly (primary) or a coupled ion gradient (secondary). Needing a protein does NOT make transport active.
The Na+/K+ ATPase pumps 3 Na+ OUT and 2 K+ IN per ATP, against both gradients. It is electrogenic (net +1 out) and builds the gradients that power secondary active transport and set up the membrane potential.
Tonicity depends ONLY on NON-penetrating solutes: cells shrink in hypertonic, swell/lyse in hypotonic, stay the same in isotonic. Water flows toward the side with more non-penetrating solute, so iso-osmotic is NOT always isotonic (the urea/glycerol trap).
Resting potential (~ -70 mV) arises mainly because the membrane is most permeable to K+; K+ leaks OUT and makes the inside negative. V_rest sits near E_K, and membrane potential always chases the equilibrium potential of the most-permeable ion.
The membrane is a fluid mosaic: an amphipathic phospholipid bilayer (hydrophilic heads out, hydrophobic tails in) with proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates, carbs ONLY on the extracellular face. Self-assembly is driven by the hydrophobic effect (entropy), not tail-tail attraction.
Cholesterol buffers fluidity: LESS fluid at high temperature, MORE fluid at low temperature. Unsaturation (cis kinks) ALWAYS increases fluidity, don't conflate the two rules.

## The Fluid Mosaic Model & Membrane Composition

Every cell is bounded by a plasma membrane, and the same basic architecture wraps the cell's internal organelles. The accepted description is the fluid mosaic model (Singer & Nicolson, 1972): the membrane is a two-dimensional fluid of lipids in which proteins and other molecules are embedded and can drift laterally. 'Fluid' captures that components move within the plane of the membrane; 'mosaic' captures the patchwork of different proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates dotting that fluid sea.

The structural backbone is the phospholipid bilayer. Each phospholipid is amphipathic (also called amphiphilic): it has a hydrophilic ('water-loving') phosphate-containing head and two hydrophobic ('water-fearing') fatty acid tails. In the watery environment inside and outside the cell, the molecules self-assemble so the polar heads face the aqueous surfaces and the nonpolar tails huddle together in the membrane's interior, away from water. This spontaneous arrangement is driven by the hydrophobic effect, an entropy-driven process in which ordered water cages around the nonpolar tails are released, increasing the entropy of the surrounding water. It is NOT driven by attractive bonds between the tails, a frequently tested subtlety.

### FIGURE: PHOSPHOLIPID BILAYER CROSS-SECTION

A cross-section of the bilayer shows two rows of phospholipids arranged tail-to-tail. The outer surfaces are lined with round hydrophilic phosphate heads facing the extracellular fluid (top) and cytoplasm (bottom). Sandwiched between them are the wavy hydrophobic fatty acid tails forming a nonpolar core; the whole bilayer is roughly 5-10 nm thick, with the purely hydrophobic core spanning ~3 nm. Cholesterol molecules are wedged between phospholipids with their small -OH groups oriented up near the polar heads. Integral proteins span the full width; peripheral proteins sit on one surface; chains of sugars (the glycocalyx) project only from the outer (extracellular) face. The key teaching point: the membrane is asymmetric, the two leaflets differ in lipid and carbohydrate composition, and carbohydrates project only outward.

Major membrane components and their roles

Component	Description	Function
Phospholipids	Amphipathic; form the bilayer	Selectively permeable barrier; structural backbone
Cholesterol	Steroid wedged among phospholipids (animal cells)	Buffers fluidity; reduces permeability to small water soluble molecules
Integral proteins	Embedded in/spanning the bilayer (transmembrane ones cross fully)	Transport channels, carriers, receptors, anchors
Peripheral proteins	Loosely bound to membrane surface or to integral proteins	Signaling, cytoskeletal attachment, enzyme activity
Carbohydrates (glycocalyx)	Sugars on outer face (glycolipids/glycoproteins)	Cell recognition, adhesion, immune ID, protection

## KEY

Phospholipids are AMPHIPATHIC, one molecule has both a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic region. This dual nature is the entire reason a bilayer forms spontaneously in water. If a question asks why the membrane self-assembles, the answer is the hydrophobic effect: nonpolar tails sequester away from water, increasing the entropy of the surrounding water.

## TRAP

Membrane carbohydrates are found ONLY on the extracellular surface, never on the cytoplasmic face. The MCAT uses membrane asymmetry to test whether you really understand orientation, glycoproteins and glycolipids face out, and this is how the immune system distinguishes self from non-self.

## Membrane Fluidity & Membrane Proteins

Because the membrane is fluid, its viscosity matters for protein function, vesicle fusion, and transport. Three factors control fluidity. (1) Temperature: higher temperature increases fluidity (molecules have more kinetic energy and move faster); cooling can cause the membrane to transition into a rigid, tightly packed gel phase. (2) Fatty-acid saturation: unsaturated fatty acids contain cis double bonds that introduce kinks, preventing the tails from packing tightly, these INCREASE fluidity. Saturated tails are straight, pack tightly, and DECREASE fluidity. (3) Tail length: longer tails have more surface contact and more van der Waals interactions between neighbors, which reduces fluidity.

Cholesterol is the membrane's 'fluidity buffer' and acts bidirectionally depending on temperature. At high (body) temperature, cholesterol restrains phospholipid movement and DECREASES fluidity, holding the membrane together. At low temperature, cholesterol inserts between phospholipids and prevents them from packing into a tight, ordered gel, thereby INCREASING fluidity (preventing solidification). The net effect is to keep fluidity within a workable range across temperatures. Cholesterol also lowers the membrane's permeability to small water soluble molecules and ions by filling gaps between phospholipid tails.

Proteins are classified by how tightly they associate with the bilayer. Integral (intrinsic) proteins are embedded within the hydrophobic core; the subset that crosses the entire membrane are transmembrane proteins, whose membrane spanning regions are often alpha-helices (or beta-barrels) rich in hydrophobic amino acids that face the lipid tails. Integral proteins can only be removed by disrupting the membrane itself (e.g., with detergents). Peripheral (extrinsic) proteins bind loosely to the surface or to integral proteins via electrostatic and hydrogen bond interactions and can be removed by milder treatments (e.g., changes in pH or salt concentration). Functionally, membrane proteins act as transporters, enzymes, receptors, cell-recognition markers, and attachment points for the cytoskeleton and extracellular matrix.

TIP

Mnemonic for cholesterol: it OPPOSES temperature extremes. Hot membrane too fluid → cholesterol stiffens it. Cold membrane too rigid → cholesterol loosens it. It is a buffer, so it always pushes fluidity back toward the middle.

## TRAP

Don't tangle the saturation rule with the cholesterol rule. Increasing unsaturation ALWAYS increases fluidity (more kinks, looser packing). Cholesterol's effect is conditional on temperature (stiffens when hot, loosens when cold). Mixing these two up is a classic distractor.

## Passive vs. Active Transport

Substances cross the membrane by two fundamentally different strategies. Passive transport moves a substance DOWN its electrochemical gradient (from high to low concentration/electrochemical potential), requires NO metabolic energy, and is driven by the substance's own gradient, increasing the entropy of the system. Active transport moves a substance UP its gradient (low to high electrochemical potential), is thermodynamically unfavorable, and therefore REQUIRES coupling to an energy source.

Passive transport has three forms. (1) Simple diffusion: small nonpolar or uncharged molecules (O2, CO2, N2, steroid hormones, small lipids) dissolve directly through the bilayer. Small uncharged polar molecules like water and urea can also cross slowly by simple diffusion. (2) Osmosis: the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane (covered in detail next). (3) Facilitated diffusion: polar molecules and ions (glucose, amino acids, Na+, K+, Cl-) that cannot readily cross the hydrophobic core move down their gradient with the help of transport proteins. It is still passive, no ATP, but it needs a protein because the solute can't efficiently dissolve through the lipid core.

Facilitated diffusion uses two protein types. Channel proteins form a hydrophilic pore through which solute flows; they are fast, and many are gated (opened/closed by voltage, ligands, or mechanical stress). Aquaporins are water-specific channels that dramatically speed osmosis. Carrier proteins bind the solute on one side, undergo a conformational change, and release it on the other side; they are slower and become saturated at high solute concentration (showing Michaelis-Menten-like kinetics, like an enzyme).

Channels vs. carriers vs. simple diffusion

Feature	Simple diffusion	Channel protein	Carrier protein
Protein needed?	No	Yes (pore)	Yes (binding site)
Substances	Small nonpolar (O2, CO2, lipids); water/urea slowly	Ions, water (aquaporins)	Glucose, amino acids, some ions
Speed	Slow, gradient-limited	Very fast	Slower (rate-limited by conformational change)
Saturable (Michaelis Menten)?	No	No (flux scales with gradient)	Yes (binding site saturates)
Conformational change?	No	No (open/closed gating only)	Yes (alternating-access)

Active transport is subdivided by its energy source. Primary active transport hydrolyzes ATP directly to power a pump (e.g., the Na+/K+ ATPase, the sarcoplasmic/endoplasmic-reticulum Ca2+ ATPase, the H+/K+ ATPase of stomach parietal cells). Secondary active transport (coupled transport) uses the energy stored in an existing ion gradient, usually the Na+ gradient built by the Na+/K+ ATPase, to drive a second solute against its gradient. Because that ion gradient was itself made with ATP, secondary transport depends on ATP indirectly. In a symporter (cotransporter) both solutes move the same direction (e.g., the SGLT Na+/glucose transporter in the gut and kidney); in an antiporter (exchanger) the two solutes move in opposite directions (e.g., the Na+/Ca2+ exchanger).

## KEY

The litmus test: does the substance move DOWN its electrochemical gradient (passive, no energy) or UP it (active, needs energy)? Facilitated diffusion uses a protein but is STILL passive because the solute moves down its gradient. Needing a protein does not make transport active.

## TRAP

Secondary active transport does NOT consume ATP at the transporter itself, yet it still depends on ATP. The transporter exploits a Na+ gradient that was created by ATP-driven pumps. So if you block ATP synthesis, secondary active transport eventually fails because the Na+ gradient collapses. Distinguish 'uses ATP directly' (primary) from 'depends on ATP indirectly' (secondary).

## Osmosis & Tonicity

Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of LOWER solute concentration (higher water concentration) to a region of HIGHER solute concentration (lower water concentration). In effect, water moves to dilute the more concentrated side. The tendency of a solution to draw water in is quantified by osmotic pressure, the pressure that must be applied to a solution to exactly stop net water flow into it across a semipermeable membrane.

Π = iMRT

Π = osmotic pressure; i = van't Hoff factor (number of dissociated particles per formula unit, e.g., NaCl ≈ 2, glucose = 1, CaCl2 ≈ 3); M = molarity of the solute; R = ideal gas constant (0.0821 L·atm/mol·K); T = absolute temperature in kelvin. A higher osmotic pressure means a stronger osmotic pull on water, water flows toward the solution with the higher Π.

It is essential to separate two related but distinct ideas: osmolarity and tonicity. Osmolarity counts the TOTAL concentration of all solute particles in a solution (osmoles per liter), regardless of whether those particles can cross the membrane. Tonicity describes the effect a solution has on cell VOLUME, and it depends ONLY on the concentration of NON-penetrating (impermeant) solutes, solutes trapped on one side that osmotically pull water. A solute that freely crosses the membrane equilibrates to equal concentrations on both sides and exerts no lasting osmotic effect, so it contributes to osmolarity but not to tonicity.

Tonicity and its effect on a cell

Solution (relative to cell)	Non-penetrating solute outside	Net water movement	Effect on cell
Hypertonic	Higher than inside	Water leaves cell	Cell shrinks (crenation in RBCs; plasmolysis in plant cells)
Hypotonic	Lower than inside	Water enters cell	Cell swells; may lyse (animal cell) / becomes turgid (plant cell, protected by wall)
Isotonic	Equal to inside	No NET movement	No net volume change (normal)

### FIGURE: RED BLOOD CELLS IN THREE SOLUTIONS

Three panels show a red blood cell (RBC). In the HYPERTONIC panel, arrows point outward as water leaves; the cell is shriveled and spiky (crenated). In the ISOTONIC panel, equal arrows in and out leave the cell its normal biconcave disc shape. In the HYPOTONIC panel, arrows point inward as water rushes in; the cell is swollen, round, and one is bursting (hemolysis). The teaching point: water always flows toward the side with more NON-penetrating solute, and animal cells (no cell wall) are vulnerable to both shrinking and bursting, whereas walled plant cells resist lysis.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A red blood cell with an internal osmolarity of 300 mOsm (all non-penetrating solutes) is placed in a beaker of 300 mOsm urea solution. Urea freely crosses the cell membrane. What happens to the cell?

Step 1, Compare osmolarity: both are 300 mOsm, so the solutions are iso-OSMOTIC. A student who stops here would wrongly predict no change. Step 2, Apply tonicity, which depends ONLY on non-penetrating solutes. Urea is penetrating, so it crosses the membrane and equilibrates to equal concentrations inside and out, contributing NOTHING to tonicity. The effective non-penetrating solute concentration outside is therefore ~0 mOsm, while inside it is 300 mOsm of trapped solutes. Step 3, With respect to impermeant solutes, the cell's interior is hypertonic relative to the bath, so water flows IN. Result: the cell swells and ultimately lyses. Key lesson: a solution can be iso-OSMOTIC yet HYPOtonic if its solutes can penetrate the membrane.

## TRAP

Iso-osmotic does NOT always mean isotonic. The MCAT loves urea or glycerol: a penetrating solute counts toward osmolarity but NOT toward tonicity. Always ask 'can this solute cross the membrane?' before predicting water flow.

TIP

Remember 'water follows (non-penetrating) solute.' Water moves toward the compartment with MORE impermeant solute. A cell in a HYPERtonic solution loses water (think 'hyper = high outside solute → water out'). Hypo = low outside solute → water in.

## The Na+/K+ ATPase & Electrochemical Gradients

The single most important pump for the MCAT is the sodium-potassium ATPase (Na+/K+ pump), a primary active transporter found in the plasma membrane of essentially all animal cells. With each catalytic cycle it hydrolyzes one ATP to pump 3 Na+ OUT of the cell and 2 K+ INTO the cell, both ions moving against their concentration gradients. The result is the characteristic ion distribution: high extracellular Na+ / low intracellular Na+, and high intracellular K+ / low extracellular K+.

### FIGURE: NA+/K+ ATPASE CYCLE

A transmembrane protein spans the membrane. Inside the cell, 3 Na+ ions bind the pump, which is open to the cytoplasm. ATP is hydrolyzed to ADP + Pi, and the phosphate group attaches to the pump (phosphorylation). This drives a conformational change so the pump opens to the outside and releases the 3 Na+. Two extracellular K+ then bind; dephosphorylation (loss of Pi) flips the pump back to its inward-open shape, releasing the 2 K+ inside. The teaching point: the pump is an enzyme that couples ATP hydrolysis to conformational changes, moving 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per ATP, an unequal exchange that nets one positive charge out per cycle, making the pump electrogenic.

The pump accomplishes three things tested heavily by the MCAT. First, it establishes the Na+ and K+ concentration gradients that power secondary active transport (e.g., glucose absorption in the gut uses the inward Na+ gradient via SGLT). Second, because it exports 3 positive charges while importing only 2, it is electrogenic and contributes (modestly, a few mV) to the negative resting membrane potential. Third, it helps maintain cell volume: by keeping intracellular Na+ low, it offsets the osmotic pull of the cell's trapped proteins and other impermeant solutes, preventing the cell from swelling and lysing.

An electrochemical gradient combines two forces acting on an ion: the chemical (concentration) gradient and the electrical gradient (the membrane potential acting on the ion's charge). The NET direction an ion moves passively is the sum of both. For example, the inside of a resting cell is negative, which electrically ATTRACTS Na+ inward, and Na+ is also far more concentrated outside, which chemically pushes it inward, both forces drive Na+ in, so opening a Na+ channel produces a strong inward Na+ rush (the basis of the action potential's depolarization).

## KEY

Memorize the Na+/K+ ATPase stoichiometry cold: 3 Na+ OUT, 2 K+ IN, per 1 ATP. The 3-out/2-in mismatch (net +1 charge out per cycle) is why it's electrogenic. The gradients it builds are the 'battery' powering secondary active transport and membrane excitability.

## TRAP

Cardiac glycosides (digoxin, ouabain) inhibit the Na+/K+ ATPase. When the pump stops, intracellular Na+ rises, which reduces the gradient driving the Na+/Ca2+ antiporter, so the cell exports less Ca2+ and intracellular Ca2+ rises, increasing cardiac contractility. If a passage describes such a drug, trace the chain: pump blocked → intracellular Na+ up → Na+/Ca2+ exchange slows → intracellular Ca2+ up → stronger contraction.

## Membrane Potential & the Nernst Equation

Most cells maintain a resting membrane potential, a voltage difference across the membrane with the inside NEGATIVE relative to the outside, typically about -70 mV in neurons. Two ingredients create it: (1) ion concentration gradients (set up by the Na+/K+ pump), and (2) selective membrane permeability. At rest the membrane is far more permeable to K+ than to Na+ because of open K+ 'leak' channels. K+ therefore flows down its concentration gradient OUT of the cell, carrying positive charge out and leaving the interior negative. The exit slows as the growing negative interior electrically pulls K+ back; the potential settles near K+'s equilibrium value (~ -90 mV), but a small resting Na+ permeability lets some Na+ leak in and pulls the actual resting potential up to about -70 mV.

E_ion = (61 mV / z) × log([ion]_outside / [ion]_inside) (at 37 °C)

E_ion = equilibrium (Nernst) potential for that single ion, the membrane voltage at which the electrical force exactly balances the concentration force, so the net flux of that ion is zero. z = charge of the ion (+1 for K+/Na+, +2 for Ca2+, −1 for Cl−). The ~61 mV constant bundles 2.303·RT/F at body temperature (≈ 59 62 mV; use them interchangeably). The log term compares outside to inside concentrations.

The Nernst equation gives the equilibrium potential for ONE ion in isolation, the voltage that would exactly oppose that ion's concentration gradient so it has no net movement. For typical mammalian cells, E_K ≈ -90 mV and E_Na ≈ +60 mV. The actual resting potential lies between these, much closer to E_K because the resting membrane is mostly K+-permeable. (When several ions matter at once, the Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (GHK) equation weights each ion by its permeability, but for the MCAT, applying Nernst to a single ion plus the qualitative GHK idea is enough.)

WORKED EXAMPLE

A neuron has [K+]outside = 5 mM and [K+]inside = 140 mM. Using E = 61 × log([out]/[in]), estimate the equilibrium potential for K+. Is this above or below the typical resting potential of -70 mV, and what does that imply about K+ movement?

Step 1, Set up the ratio: [out]/[in] = 5/140 ≈ 0.036. Step 2, Take the log: log(0.036) ≈ -1.45. Step 3, Multiply by 61 (z = +1, so divide by 1): E_K = 61 × (-1.45) ≈ -88 mV (about -90 mV). Step 4, Interpret: K+'s equilibrium potential (-88 mV) is MORE negative than the resting potential (-70 mV). Because the cell sits ABOVE (less negative than) E_K, there is a net electrochemical driving force pushing K+ OUT of the cell, the membrane is not negative enough to electrically hold all the K+ in against its concentration gradient. This continuous, slow outward K+ leak is exactly what holds the inside negative and keeps the resting potential near E_K. Takeaway: the resting potential sits close to the equilibrium potential of the ion the membrane is most permeable to (K+), but slightly positive of it because of the small Na+ leak.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Why does opening voltage-gated Na+ channels cause the membrane potential to shoot toward +60 mV during an action potential?

Step 1, Recall E_Na ≈ +60 mV: Na+ is concentrated outside (~145 mM) and low inside (~12 mM), and at rest the inside is negative, so BOTH the chemical gradient (high outside drives Na+ in) and the electrical gradient (negative inside attracts the positive ion) push Na+ inward, a large inward electrochemical driving force. Step 2, Opening voltage-gated Na+ channels dramatically increases Na+ permeability. By the GHK logic, the membrane potential moves toward the equilibrium potential of whichever ion is now most permeable. Step 3, With Na+ permeability suddenly dominating, the potential races from about -70 mV up toward E_Na (+60 mV): this is depolarization, the rising phase of the action potential. Step 4, The Na+ channels quickly inactivate and voltage-gated K+ channels open, so the potential never quite reaches +60 mV before repolarizing back toward E_K. Takeaway: membrane potential always heads toward the equilibrium potential of whatever ion the membrane is currently most permeable to.

TIP

The membrane potential always chases the equilibrium potential of the MOST permeable ion. At rest that's K+ (so V_rest ≈ E_K, near -70 to -90 mV). When Na+ channels open, Na+ becomes most permeable, so V races toward E_Na ≈ +60 mV. This one principle explains every phase of the action potential.

## TRAP

Don't assume the Na+/K+ pump is the DIRECT cause of the resting potential, it contributes only a few millivolts directly. The resting potential is generated mainly by K+ leaking out through leak channels, down the gradient the pump built. The pump's role is to maintain the gradients, not to set the moment-to-moment voltage. (Even so, blocking the pump eventually abolishes the potential because the gradients run down.)

## Bulk Transport & Cell Junctions

Large molecules and bulk material that cannot use channels or carriers are moved by vesicle-based transport, which always requires energy (ATP for vesicle formation, movement, and fusion). Endocytosis brings material INTO the cell by engulfing it in a membrane vesicle. It has three flavors: phagocytosis ('cell eating', engulfing large particles or whole cells, as macrophages do to bacteria), pinocytosis ('cell drinking', nonspecific bulk uptake of extracellular fluid and dissolved solutes), and receptor-mediated endocytosis (specific uptake triggered when ligands bind surface receptors that cluster in clathrin-coated pits, how cells take up LDL cholesterol). Exocytosis is the reverse: vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane to release their contents (e.g., neurotransmitters at a synapse, secreted proteins, hormones) and simultaneously add new lipid and protein to the cell surface.

In tissues, cells are held together and communicate through specialized cell junctions. The MCAT expects you to distinguish three in animal cells. Tight junctions form a continuous, watertight seal between adjacent cells, blocking molecules from leaking through the space between cells (the paracellular path), critical in the intestinal lining and the blood-brain barrier. Desmosomes are 'spot welds' (anchoring junctions) that bind cells together mechanically via intermediate filaments (anchored at structures called dense plaques), giving tissues strength against stretching (e.g., skin, cardiac muscle). Gap junctions are clusters of protein channels (connexons, each built from connexin subunits) that directly connect the cytoplasm of neighboring cells, allowing ions and small molecules to pass, enabling rapid electrical and chemical coupling, as in cardiac and smooth muscle. (Plant cells use analogous channels called plasmodesmata.)

Animal cell junctions

Junction	Function	Analogy / Example
Tight junction	Seals the space between cells; prevents paracellular leakage	Watertight zipper; intestinal lining & blood-brain barrier
Desmosome	Mechanical anchoring of cells together (via intermediate filaments)	Spot welds; skin, cardiac muscle
Gap junction	Cytoplasmic channel for ions/small molecules; cell-to-cell communication	Tunnel; cardiac & smooth muscle electrical coupling

## KEY

In an epithelial cell, the junctions are arranged apical-to-basal: tight junction (seal) near the apex, then desmosomes (anchor), with gap junctions (communication) along the lateral surface. For function, link tight = barrier, desmosome = strength, gap = communication.

## TRAP

All vesicular (bulk) transport, phagocytosis, pinocytosis, receptor-mediated endocytosis, and exocytosis, requires ATP, even though it isn't 'pumping' a single solute against a concentration gradient. Don't misclassify endocytosis as passive just because no specific transport protein is moving a single ion.
