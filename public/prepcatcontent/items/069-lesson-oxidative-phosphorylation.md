# Oxidative phosphorylation

Where glucose's energy actually gets cashed in, the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis.

WHAT YOU'LL LEARN

Trace electrons from NADH to O₂
Know which complexes pump protons
Tally the real ATP yield

CONCEPTS

The electron path

NADH drops electrons at Complex I; they ride coenzyme Q to Complex III, then cytochrome c to Complex IV, which hands them to O₂. FADH₂ (and succinate) enters one step later at Complex II → CoQ, bypassing Complex I, which is why it yields less ATP.

Pumping builds the gradient

Complexes I, III, and IV pump H⁺ from the matrix into the intermembrane space. Complex II does NOT pump. The resulting proton-motive force (a pH gradient + membrane potential) is the stored energy, not the electrons themselves.

Chemiosmosis makes the ATP

Protons flow back into the matrix through ATP synthase (Complex V), and that flow drives ADP + Pi → ATP. ATP synthase is not part of electron transport, it's the downstream turbine the gradient spins.

The ATP ledger

Each NADH yields ≈ 2.5 ATP, each FADH₂ ≈ 1.5 ATP. Full oxidation of one glucose nets ≈ 30 32 ATP (the modern figure; older texts say 36 38).

THE RULE

O₂ is the final electron acceptor at Complex IV. No O₂ → the chain backs up, NADH can't be reoxidized, and oxidative phosphorylation stops.

CHEAT SHEET

Path: I → CoQ → III → cyt c → IV → O₂
FADH₂/succinate enters at II → CoQ (bypasses I)
Proton pumps: I, III, IV (NOT II)
ATP synthase = Complex V, runs on the H⁺ gradient
NADH ≈ 2.5 ATP · FADH₂ ≈ 1.5 ATP
≈ 30 32 ATP per glucose · inner mitochondrial membrane

COMMON TRAPS

·
Thinking Complex II pumps protons, it doesn't.
·
Swapping the carriers: CoQ rides in the membrane, cytochrome c in the intermembrane space.
·
Calling ATP synthase part of the ETC (it's chemiosmosis, downstream).
·
Quoting the old 36 38 ATP figure instead of ≈ 30 32.
