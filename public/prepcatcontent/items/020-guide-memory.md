# Memory

Memory is the set of processes by which information is encoded, stored, and retrieved, and the MCAT tests it through the multi-store model, memory types, forgetting mechanisms, and the neurobiology of memory. Expect questions that link experimental findings (the forgetting curve, the serial position effect, interference) to underlying processes and brain structures.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Three processes: ENCODING (in) -> STORAGE (hold) -> RETRIEVAL (out); a memory failure can occur at any stage, and the MCAT often asks you to localize which one. Encoding depth matters: semantic > acoustic > visual, and elaborative rehearsal beats maintenance rehearsal.
Atkinson-Shiffrin three-box model: sensory (huge capacity, sub-second) -> STM/working (~7 plus or minus 2 items, ~15-30 s) -> LTM (unlimited, durable). Don't swap capacity vs duration, and remember sensory has the LARGEST capacity but the SHORTEST duration.
LTM split: explicit/declarative (episodic + semantic; conscious; HIPPOCAMPUS/medial temporal lobe) vs implicit/nondeclarative (procedural + priming + conditioning; unconscious; cerebellum, basal ganglia, amygdala, NOT hippocampus-dependent). H.M. could still learn motor skills.
Proactive interference = OLD blocks NEW; retroactive = NEW blocks OLD. Interference is a RETRIEVAL competition problem, not erasure of the trace.
Serial position effect: primacy (rehearsal -> LTM) + recency (still in STM); a delay/distractor wipes out recency but spares primacy, clean evidence for separate stores. Ebbinghaus curve drops steeply then plateaus; spacing and testing effects flatten it.
Hippocampus consolidates new explicit memories; damage causes ANTEROGRADE amnesia (no new explicit memories; implicit spared) while RETROGRADE amnesia loses old memories. LTP (NMDA/glutamate) is the leading cellular model of learning.

## The Three Core Processes: Encoding, Storage, Retrieval

Memory is not a single thing you 'have', it is a sequence of processes you perform on information. The MCAT frames nearly every memory question around three stages, and a large fraction of questions reduce to 'which stage failed?' First is ENCODING: transforming sensory input into a form the brain can store. Second is STORAGE: retaining the encoded information over time. Third is RETRIEVAL: accessing stored information and bringing it back into conscious awareness. If you study a list and later cannot remember it, the failure could be at any of the three stages, you may never have encoded it well, the trace may have decayed in storage, or it may be intact but inaccessible because you lack the right cue to retrieve it.

Encoding comes in flavors that differ in depth. ACOUSTIC encoding stores information by sound (rehearsing a phone number by saying it). VISUAL encoding stores it as images. SEMANTIC encoding stores it by meaning, and this is the deepest, most durable form. This grades into the LEVELS-OF-PROCESSING framework (Craik & Lockhart): the more deeply you process information (more semantically, more self-relevantly), the better it is remembered. Shallow processing (noticing the font a word is printed in) yields weak memory; deep processing (deciding whether a word describes you) yields strong memory. ELABORATIVE rehearsal, linking new information to existing knowledge, is far more effective than MAINTENANCE rehearsal (simple rote repetition), precisely because it engages semantic encoding rather than just keeping items active by sound.

## KEY

The self-reference effect: information processed in relation to yourself is remembered best of all. It is the deepest form of semantic encoding because 'you' is the richest, most elaborated network of associations you possess.

TIP

Mnemonic devices (method of loci, acronyms, peg-word systems, chunking) work by forcing deep, organized, often visual or self-relevant encoding. If a question describes a memory aid, the mechanism is almost always 'improves encoding/organization,' not 'increases the raw storage capacity of long term memory.'

## The Multi-Store Model: Sensory, Short-Term, and Long-Term Memory

The dominant MCAT framework is the Atkinson-Shiffrin (1968) MODAL or 'three-box' model. Information flows through three stores that differ in capacity and duration. SENSORY MEMORY is an ultra-brief, high-capacity buffer that holds an essentially complete sensory snapshot for a fraction of a second. ICONIC memory is the visual store (~0.25-0.5 s, demonstrated by Sperling's partial-report experiments); ECHOIC memory is the auditory store (~2-4 s, which is why you can briefly 'replay' the last few words someone said). Most of this information decays almost immediately; only what you ATTEND to passes into the next store, which makes attention the gatekeeper between sensory memory and STM.

SHORT-TERM MEMORY (STM) holds the information you are currently aware of. Its capacity is famously about 7 plus or minus 2 items (Miller's 'magic number'), though more modern estimates often cite ~4 chunks (Cowan). Its duration is only ~15-30 seconds without rehearsal (Peterson & Peterson found steep loss by ~18 s once rehearsal is blocked). CHUNKING, grouping items into meaningful units, expands EFFECTIVE capacity (a 10-digit phone number becomes 3 chunks). REHEARSAL keeps items active in STM and, through repetition (especially elaborative rehearsal), transfers them into long term storage. LONG-TERM MEMORY (LTM) has effectively unlimited capacity and can last a lifetime; moving information into LTM is the goal of effective studying.

Comparison of the three memory stores

Store	Capacity	Duration	Key fact / example
Sensory memory	Very large (near-complete snapshot)	Iconic ~0.25-0.5 s; Echoic ~2-4 s	Sperling partial-report; decays unless attended to
Short-term / working	~7 plus or minus 2 items (~4 chunks)	~15-30 s without rehearsal	Miller's magic number; effective capacity expanded by chunking
Long-term	Effectively unlimited	Minutes to a lifetime	Requires consolidation; durable

Baddeley and Hitch refined STM into WORKING MEMORY, an active system that not only HOLDS but MANIPULATES information. It has a CENTRAL EXECUTIVE (an attentional controller) directing two subsystems: the PHONOLOGICAL LOOP (verbal/acoustic information, your inner voice) and the VISUOSPATIAL SKETCHPAD (visual and spatial information, your mind's eye). Baddeley later added the EPISODIC BUFFER, which integrates information across the subsystems and links working memory to long term memory. The MCAT distinction to hold onto: classic STM emphasizes passive holding, whereas working memory emphasizes active processing under attentional control.

## TRAP

Do not confuse the CAPACITY of short term/working memory (~7 plus or minus 2 items) with its DURATION (~15-30 s), questions deliberately swap these numbers. And keep the extremes straight: sensory memory has the LARGEST capacity but the SHORTEST duration, long term memory has the largest capacity AND the longest duration, and short term memory has the SMALLEST capacity of the three stores.

## Types of Long-Term Memory: Explicit vs Implicit

Long-term memory is not monolithic, it divides along whether retrieval is conscious. EXPLICIT (DECLARATIVE) memory is consciously recalled and can be 'declared' in words. It splits into EPISODIC memory (personally experienced events tied to a time and place, your last birthday) and SEMANTIC memory (general facts and concepts divorced from any personal context, the capital of France, or that a triangle has three sides). IMPLICIT (NONDECLARATIVE) memory operates without conscious awareness. Its main subtype is PROCEDURAL memory (skills and habits, riding a bike, typing), and it also includes classically conditioned responses and PRIMING.

This division is anatomically real and high yield. Explicit memories depend on the HIPPOCAMPUS and adjacent medial temporal lobe structures for formation and consolidation. Implicit memories do NOT depend on the hippocampus: procedural and motor skills rely on the CEREBELLUM and BASAL GANGLIA, while emotionally charged memories engage the AMYGDALA. This is why patient H.M. (whose hippocampus was largely removed) could learn new motor skills, his mirror-drawing improved from day to day, even though he had no conscious memory of ever having practiced: his implicit learning was intact while his ability to form new explicit memories was destroyed.

Long-term memory taxonomy

Type	Subtype	Conscious?	Example	Brain region
Explicit (declarative)	Episodic	Yes	Your high-school graduation	Hippocampus / medial temporal lobe
Explicit (declarative)	Semantic	Yes	Water is H2O	Hippocampus (initial) + neocortex
Implicit (nondeclarative)	Procedural	No	Riding a bicycle	Cerebellum, basal ganglia
Implicit (nondeclarative)	Priming / conditioning	No	Faster word recognition after exposure	Neocortex; amygdala (emotional conditioning)

## KEY

Memorize the split: Explicit = Episodic + Semantic (conscious; hippocampus/medial temporal lobe). Implicit = Procedural + priming + conditioning (unconscious; cerebellum, basal ganglia, amygdala). Anterograde amnesia from hippocampal damage impairs new EXPLICIT learning while sparing implicit learning.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient suffers bilateral hippocampal damage. Over several days, a researcher teaches her to trace a star while watching only her hand's reflection in a mirror. Her tracing improves each day, yet every day she insists she has never done the task before. Which memory systems are intact and which are impaired?

The improving motor performance reflects PROCEDURAL (implicit) memory, which depends on the cerebellum and basal ganglia, not the hippocampus, so it is INTACT. Her inability to consciously recall ever having done the task reflects EPISODIC (explicit, declarative) memory, which requires the hippocampus to form new memories, so it is IMPAIRED. This dissociation (the classic H.M./mirror-drawing finding) demonstrates that explicit and implicit memory are separate systems with separate neural substrates. The deficit is specifically in forming NEW explicit memories, which defines anterograde amnesia; older, already-consolidated explicit memories would typically be spared.

## Forgetting: The Ebbinghaus Curve, Decay, and Savings

Hermann Ebbinghaus, learning lists of nonsense syllables on himself, produced the FORGETTING CURVE: retention drops steeply in the minutes and hours immediately after learning, then the rate of loss slows, leaving a small, relatively stable residue. The shape is roughly exponential, most forgetting happens fast, soon after acquisition. The MCAT wants you to recognize the curve's shape (a sharp initial drop, then a long shallow tail) and to know that it is flattened by deeper encoding, OVERLEARNING (continuing to study past the point of first mastery), and especially by spaced review.

R = e^(-t/S)

A common idealization of Ebbinghaus's data. R = memory retention (fraction remembered), t = time since learning, S = relative strength/stability of the memory (a larger S means slower forgetting). The negative exponential captures rapid early loss that levels off. You do not need to compute with this formula, just recognize that retention falls fast and then plateaus, and that strengthening the memory (larger S) flattens the curve.

Ebbinghaus also gave us SAVINGS: even after a memory seems 'gone,' relearning it takes less time than learning it the first time. That saved time is evidence that the original trace was not entirely erased. RELEARNING is therefore the most sensitive measure of memory, more sensitive than recall or recognition, because it can detect traces too weak to be retrieved consciously. The two retrieval-based measures rank below it: RECALL (producing information with no cues, fill-in-the-blank) is harder than RECOGNITION (identifying the correct item among options, multiple choice), because recognition supplies its own retrieval cue. In sensitivity, then: relearning > recognition > recall.

One curve starts at 100% and drops steeply within the first hour, then flattens into a long shallow tail, the classic forgetting curve. TIP

The SPACING EFFECT (distributed practice beats massed cramming) and the TESTING EFFECT / retrieval practice (self-testing beats re-reading) are the two evidence-based study findings the MCAT loves. Both flatten the forgetting curve. Retrieval practice strengthens memory more than passive review precisely because it exercises the retrieval pathway itself.

## Why We Forget: Interference, Decay, and Retrieval Failure

Forgetting has several distinct mechanisms, and the MCAT expects you to tell them apart. DECAY theory holds that unused memory traces fade with time (clearest for sensory and short term memory). RETRIEVAL FAILURE holds that the memory is stored but momentarily inaccessible, the tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon is the canonical example, where you know that you know something but cannot pull it out, often because the right cue is missing. INTERFERENCE, the most heavily tested mechanism, holds that other memories compete with and disrupt the target memory at retrieval.

Interference comes in two directions. PROACTIVE interference: OLD information disrupts the retrieval of NEW information ('pro' = forward; the old memory reaches forward in time to block the new one). Example: after years with an old phone number, you keep recalling it instead of your new one. RETROACTIVE interference: NEW information disrupts retrieval of OLD information ('retro' = backward; the new memory reaches back to block the old one). Example: learning a new locker combination makes last year's combination hard to recall. The key conceptual point: interference is a RETRIEVAL problem, the competing memory is not erased, it is just being out-competed by another.

Mechanisms of forgetting

Mechanism	What happens	MCAT signature
Decay	Unused trace fades over time	Loss with mere passage of time; strongest in sensory/STM
Proactive interference	OLD memory blocks NEW	Previously learned material disrupts later learning
Retroactive interference	NEW memory blocks OLD	Newly learned material disrupts earlier memories
Retrieval failure	Stored but inaccessible	Tip-of-the-tongue; recalled later with the right cue
Motivated forgetting (repression)	Anxiety-driven blocking (Freudian, controversial)	Emotionally threatening memories

## TRAP

PROactive = the PRIOR (old) memory interferes forward; REtroactive = the RECENT (new) memory interferes backward in time. Students reliably reverse these under time pressure. Anchor on the DIRECTION the interfering memory 'reaches,' and remember that in both cases the memory is intact, the failure is at retrieval, not in storage.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A student studies French in the morning and Spanish in the afternoon. That evening, she finds she keeps inserting Spanish words when trying to recall the French vocabulary she learned earlier. Which type of interference is this, and how would the result differ if she instead struggled to recall the Spanish?

When the LATER-learned Spanish disrupts the EARLIER-learned French, NEW information is interfering with OLD information, this is RETROACTIVE interference (the new material reaches backward to block the old). If instead the EARLIER-learned French disrupted recall of the later Spanish, that would be PROACTIVE interference (the old material reaching forward to block the new). Note that in both cases the memories are still stored; the problem is competition at retrieval, which is why a strong, distinctive retrieval cue could still recover the correct language's vocabulary.

## Retrieval Cues, Context, and Spreading Activation

A RETRIEVAL CUE is any stimulus that helps you access a stored memory. The ENCODING SPECIFICITY PRINCIPLE (Tulving) states that memory is best when the cues present at retrieval match those present at encoding. Two famous applications follow. CONTEXT-DEPENDENT memory: you recall information better in the same EXTERNAL environment where you learned it (Godden & Baddeley had divers learn word lists either underwater or on land and recall best in the matching setting). STATE-DEPENDENT memory: you recall better when your INTERNAL physiological or mood state at retrieval matches the state at encoding (e.g., learning while caffeinated and recalling better while caffeinated). MOOD-CONGRUENT memory is related but distinct: a given mood biases you toward retrieving memories whose emotional tone is consistent with that mood.

How do cues actually find memories? The SEMANTIC NETWORK model pictures long term memory as an interconnected web of concept nodes, with related concepts linked by associations of varying strength. SPREADING ACTIVATION is the mechanism: activating one node (say, 'red') automatically sends activation along its links to associated nodes ('fire truck,' 'apple,' 'rose'), making them easier to retrieve. This is also the engine behind PRIMING, exposure to one stimulus unconsciously speeds processing of a related stimulus (seeing 'doctor' makes you recognize 'nurse' faster). Priming is a form of IMPLICIT memory; it requires no conscious awareness of the prime.

## KEY

Encoding specificity ties it all together: context-dependent cues (external environment), state-dependent cues (internal physiological/mood state), and even the testing format are all retrieval cues. Recreate the encoding conditions at retrieval and you remember more, which is also why studying under conditions similar to the test environment can help.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Students study a word list either sober or intoxicated, then are tested either sober or intoxicated. Recall is best when the test state matches the study state (sober-sober and drunk-drunk outperform the mismatched conditions). What principle explains this, and what type of cue is the state?

This is STATE-DEPENDENT memory, a specific case of the ENCODING SPECIFICITY PRINCIPLE: retrieval improves when conditions at recall match conditions at encoding. Here the relevant cue is INTERNAL, the person's physiological/drug state, rather than the external environment (which would be context-dependent memory). The internal state present during encoding becomes part of the memory trace and serves as a retrieval cue; reinstating that state at test reactivates the associated memory, boosting recall even though the drug itself impairs absolute performance. The effect is about the MATCH between study and test states, not about the states being beneficial in themselves.

## Serial Position, Memory Construction, and the Neurobiology of Memory

When people study a list and then recall it freely, the SERIAL POSITION EFFECT appears: items at the beginning (the PRIMACY effect) and at the end (the RECENCY effect) are remembered better than middle items, producing a U-shaped curve. The two ends have different causes. Primacy occurs because early items received the most REHEARSAL and had time to be encoded into LONG-TERM memory. Recency occurs because the last items are still sitting in SHORT-TERM memory at the moment of recall. This dissociation is testable: inserting a brief distractor task (counting backward for ~30 seconds) before recall ELIMINATES the recency effect, those STM items are flushed out, while leaving primacy intact, because primacy items are already safely in LTM.

Memory is RECONSTRUCTIVE, not a video recording. We rebuild memories at retrieval, filling gaps with schemas and expectations, which makes them vulnerable to distortion. The MISINFORMATION EFFECT (Loftus): exposure to misleading post-event information warps the memory of the original event, witnesses asked how fast cars were going when they 'smashed into' (versus 'hit') each other estimated higher speeds and later 'remembered' broken glass that was never there. SOURCE-MONITORING errors occur when you remember a piece of information but misattribute WHERE it came from, a key route to FALSE MEMORIES. The MCAT uses these findings to test the unreliability of eyewitness testimony.

Neurobiologically, two structures and one mechanism dominate. The HIPPOCAMPUS is essential for CONSOLIDATION, converting new explicit memories into stable long term storage; damage causes ANTEROGRADE amnesia (an inability to form new explicit memories) while older consolidated memories and implicit learning survive. RETROGRADE amnesia, by contrast, is the loss of memories formed BEFORE the injury. At the cellular level, LONG-TERM POTENTIATION (LTP), a persistent strengthening of synaptic transmission following repeated, high-frequency stimulation, heavily involving NMDA receptors and glutamate, is the leading model for how learning is physically encoded ('neurons that fire together wire together'). Clinically, ALZHEIMER'S disease degrades the hippocampus and acetylcholine pathways (early loss of recent and declarative memory, with amyloid plaques and tau tangles), and KORSAKOFF'S syndrome (thiamine/B1 deficiency, often from chronic alcoholism) produces severe anterograde amnesia plus CONFABULATION (filling memory gaps with fabricated material the patient sincerely believes).

Memory disorders and amnesia at a glance

Condition	Core deficit	MCAT hook
Anterograde amnesia	Cannot form NEW explicit memories	Hippocampal damage; implicit learning spared (H.M.)
Retrograde amnesia	Loss of memories from BEFORE the event	'Retro' = backward in time
Alzheimer's disease	Progressive loss of recent/declarative memory	Hippocampal degeneration; decreased acetylcholine; amyloid plaques + tau tangles
Korsakoff's syndrome	Severe anterograde amnesia + confabulation	Thiamine (B1) deficiency; chronic alcohol use

## TRAP

Anterograde vs retrograde is a guaranteed trap. ANTErograde = cannot move forward to make NEW memories (after the injury). RETROgrade = lose OLD memories from before the injury (retro = backward). H.M. and Korsakoff's syndrome are primarily ANTEROGRADE. Do not reverse the two, and do not assume amnesia means 'forgetting the past', the more commonly tested form is the inability to lay down new memories.

TIP

If a passage describes the recency effect disappearing after a delay or distractor task, the answer hinges on short term memory being flushed while primacy (already in long term memory) survives. This single dissociation is one of the cleanest pieces of evidence for separate STM and LTM stores.
