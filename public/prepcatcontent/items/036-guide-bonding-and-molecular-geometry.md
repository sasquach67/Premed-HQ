# Bonding and Molecular Geometry

Chemical bonds span a continuum from ionic to covalent set by electronegativity differences, and the arrangement of bonding and lone-pair electrons (VSEPR) plus hybridization fixes a molecule's shape, polarity, and intermolecular behavior. This guide links Lewis structures, geometry, and bond properties into one predictive framework.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Bonding is a continuum: large electronegativity difference (roughly >1.7) gives ionic, moderate (0.5-1.7) polar covalent, small (<0.5) nonpolar covalent.
VSEPR: count electron domains (bonds + lone pairs) for electron geometry; lone pairs are not part of the named molecular shape and they shrink bond angles.
Single = 1 sigma; double = 1 sigma + 1 pi; triple = 1 sigma + 2 pi. Hybridization = number of electron domains (2 sp, 3 sp2, 4 sp3).
Polar bonds do not make a polar molecule if the geometry is symmetric and the dipoles cancel (CO2, CCl4, BF3, SF6).
Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded directly to N, O, or F; it dominates boiling-point trends and is the strongest dipole-type force.
Higher bond order means shorter and stronger bonds; the best Lewis/resonance structure minimizes formal charge.

## Bond Types and Electronegativity

Atoms form chemical bonds to reach a lower-energy, more stable electron configuration, usually a full valence shell (the octet, or a duet for hydrogen). Bonding is best viewed as a continuum rather than as three discrete boxes: the same valence electrons can be transferred (ionic), shared unequally (polar covalent), or shared evenly (nonpolar covalent). What determines where a bond falls on this continuum is the difference in electronegativity (EN), the tendency of an atom to pull shared electrons toward itself.

An ionic bond forms when one atom transfers one or more electrons to another, producing a cation and an anion held together by electrostatic attraction. This typically occurs between a metal (low EN, easily loses electrons) and a nonmetal (high EN). Ionic compounds do not exist as discrete molecules; they form extended crystal lattices, which gives them high melting and boiling points, brittleness, and the ability to conduct electricity only when molten or dissolved, where the ions become mobile.

A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons between two atoms, characteristic of nonmetal to nonmetal bonding. When the two atoms are identical or have nearly equal EN (delta EN below about 0.5), the sharing is even and the bond is nonpolar covalent, as in H2 or Cl2. When EN differs moderately (about 0.5 to 1.7), electrons spend more time near the more electronegative atom, creating a bond dipole with partial charges (delta plus and delta minus); this is a polar covalent bond, such as the O to H bond in water.

In metallic bonding, valence electrons are delocalized into a shared sea that flows around fixed metal cations, explaining the conductivity, malleability, and luster of metals. A special case of covalent bonding is the coordinate (dative) covalent bond, in which both shared electrons come from the same atom; once formed it is indistinguishable from any other covalent bond. The bond donated by ammonia's lone pair to a proton in NH4+ is the classic example.

U is proportional to (Q1 x Q2) / r

Coulomb's law for ionic (lattice) energy: attraction grows with larger ionic charges (Q) and smaller separation, which tracks ionic radius (r). Higher charge magnitude and smaller ions give stronger ionic bonds and higher melting points (for example, MgO melts far higher than NaCl).

Classifying bonds by electronegativity difference (Pauling)

Delta EN	Bond type	Character
Below 0.5	Nonpolar covalent	Electrons shared roughly equally
0.5 to 1.7	Polar covalent	Unequal sharing; partial charges
Above 1.7	Ionic	Electron transfer; cation and anion

## KEY

Electronegativity increases up and to the right on the periodic table (excluding noble gases), so fluorine (3.98) is the most electronegative element, followed by oxygen (3.44) and nitrogen (3.04). Smaller atoms hold bonding electrons more tightly, which drives the trend. These cutoffs are guidelines, not sharp lines.

## Lewis Structures, Formal Charge, and Resonance

To draw a Lewis structure: (1) sum all valence electrons, adding electrons for negative charge and subtracting for positive; (2) place the least electronegative atom in the center, but never hydrogen, which forms only one bond; (3) connect atoms with single bonds; (4) distribute the remaining electrons as lone pairs to complete the octets of the outer atoms first; (5) if the central atom still lacks an octet, convert lone pairs from neighbors into double or triple bonds.

Formal charge = (valence electrons) - (nonbonding electrons) - (1/2)(bonding electrons)

Computed per atom assuming perfectly equal sharing. The sum of all formal charges equals the molecule's overall charge. Formal charge is a bookkeeping device, distinct from oxidation state (which assumes full transfer to the more electronegative atom).

Formal charge lets you compare candidate structures. The preferred Lewis structure (1) gives every atom a formal charge as close to zero as possible, (2) avoids placing like charges on adjacent atoms, and (3) puts any negative formal charge on the more electronegative element. These rules also decide which resonance form contributes most to the true structure.

Three classes of molecules break the octet rule. Incomplete octets: hydrogen is stable with 2 electrons, beryllium with 4, and boron with 6 (BF3 has only 6 electrons around boron). Expanded octets: elements in period 3 and below can exceed 8 electrons using accessible d orbitals (PCl5 holds 10, SF6 holds 12). Odd-electron species (free radicals) such as NO and NO2 carry an unpaired electron and cannot satisfy the octet for every atom.

## TRAP

A frequent error is drawing an expanded octet for a period-2 element. Carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and fluorine have no accessible d orbitals and can NEVER exceed 8 valence electrons. Only period 3 and beyond (P, S, Cl, Xe, and so on) can expand the octet.

When no single Lewis structure captures a molecule with delocalized electrons, the real structure is a resonance hybrid, a weighted average of several valid resonance structures that differ only in electron placement while the atoms stay fixed. Ozone (O3), the carbonate ion (CO3 2-), and benzene are classic cases. Resonance delocalization lowers energy and stabilizes the molecule, and all equivalent bonds become identical in length and strength, intermediate between single and double bonds.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Nitrous oxide (N2O, with connectivity N-N-O) has two reasonable resonance structures: (a) N triple-bond N - O and (b) N double-bond N double-bond O. Use formal charges to decide which contributes more to the hybrid.

N2O has 16 valence electrons. Structure (a): the terminal triple-bonded N keeps 1 lone pair, FC = 5 - 2 - 3 = 0; the central N has no lone pairs and four bonds, FC = 5 - 0 - 4 = +1; the singly bonded O has 3 lone pairs, FC = 6 - 6 - 1 = -1. Structure (b): the terminal double-bonded N (2 lone pairs) gives FC = 5 - 4 - 2 = -1; the central N is +1; the double-bonded O (2 lone pairs) gives FC = 6 - 4 - 2 = 0. Both have the same magnitudes, but structure (a) places the -1 formal charge on oxygen, the more electronegative atom, so (a) is the larger contributor to the resonance hybrid.

## VSEPR and Molecular Geometry

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts molecular shape from a single idea: electron domains around a central atom repel one another and spread out as far apart as possible. An electron domain (also called a region of electron density, or counted as the steric number) is any single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair. A multiple bond counts as just one domain. The number of domains gives the electron-domain geometry.

Electron geometry describes the arrangement of all domains; molecular geometry describes only the positions of the atoms, the shape you would actually see. When every domain is a bond, the two geometries match. When lone pairs are present, the molecular geometry is a subset of the electron geometry: water has four domains (tetrahedral electron geometry) but only two bonds, giving a bent shape. Lone pairs also take up more room than bonding pairs, compressing the remaining bond angles.

3	2	1	Trigonal planar	Bent	Below 120	SO2, O3
4	4	0	Tetrahedral	Tetrahedral	109.5	CH4
4	3	1	Tetrahedral	Trigonal pyramidal	About 107	NH3
4	2	2	Tetrahedral	Bent	About 104.5	H2O
5	5	0	Trigonal bipyramidal	Trigonal bipyramidal	90 and 120	PCl5
5	4	1	Trigonal bipyramidal	Seesaw	Below 90 and 120	SF4
5	3	2	Trigonal bipyramidal	T-shaped	Below 90	ClF3
5	2	3	Trigonal bipyramidal	Linear	180	XeF2, I3-

6	5	1	Octahedral	Square pyramidal	Below 90	BrF5
6	4	2	Octahedral	Square planar	90	XeF4

## TRAP

Do not confuse electron geometry with molecular geometry. Ammonia (NH3) and water (H2O) both have tetrahedral electron geometry (four domains), but their molecular shapes are trigonal pyramidal and bent because lone pairs are not part of the named shape. Always find the steric number first, then hide the lone pairs.

## KEY

Repulsion strength follows: lone pair to lone pair > lone pair to bonding pair > bonding pair to bonding pair. In a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement (5 domains), lone pairs always take the roomier equatorial positions, which is why SF4 is a seesaw, ClF3 is T-shaped, and XeF2 is linear.

Bond angles deviate from ideal values for three reasons: (1) lone pairs push bonding pairs closer together (ammonia is 107 degrees versus the ideal 109.5, and water is 104.5); (2) a more electronegative substituent (terminal atom) pulls bonding electrons away from the center and slightly shrinks angles (NF3 at 102 degrees versus NH3 at 107), while a more electronegative central atom does the opposite, pulling bonding density toward itself and widening the angle (water at 104.5 degrees versus H2S at 92); and (3) multiple bonds carry more electron density than single bonds, repel more strongly, and widen the angles around them.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Predict the electron geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angles of xenon tetrafluoride (XeF4).

Xenon contributes 8 valence electrons. Four bonds to fluorine use 8 electrons (4 of them from Xe), leaving 4 electrons as 2 lone pairs on Xe. Steric number = 4 bonds + 2 lone pairs = 6 domains, so the electron geometry is octahedral. To minimize repulsion the two lone pairs occupy opposite (trans) axial positions, leaving the four fluorines in one plane. The molecular geometry is square planar with 90 degree F-Xe-F angles. Xe expands its octet here, which is allowed because it sits in period 5.

## Hybridization, Sigma, and Pi Bonds

Hybridization reconciles VSEPR shapes with atomic orbitals. Isolated atoms have s and p orbitals pointing in fixed directions, which cannot explain methane's four identical 109.5 degree bonds. In valence bond theory, atomic orbitals on the central atom mix to form an equal number of degenerate hybrid orbitals aimed at the electron domains. The number of hybrid orbitals always equals the steric number.

Steric number, hybridization, and geometry

Domains	Hybridization	Electron geometry	Angle	Example
2	sp	Linear	180	CO2, BeCl2
3	sp2	Trigonal planar	120	BF3, SO3
4	sp3	Tetrahedral	109.5	CH4, NH3, H2O
5	sp3d	Trigonal bipyramidal	90 and 120	PCl5
6	sp3d2	Octahedral	90	SF6

Bonds form by two kinds of orbital overlap. A sigma bond results from head-on overlap directly along the internuclear axis; this includes every single bond and the first bond of any multiple bond. A pi bond results from side-by-side overlap of unhybridized p orbitals above and below that axis. Pi bonds are formed only by p orbitals that were left out of hybridization.

Counting is straightforward: a single bond is 1 sigma; a double bond is 1 sigma plus 1 pi; a triple bond is 1 sigma plus 2 pi. Every connection between two atoms therefore contains exactly one sigma bond, and any extra bonds are pi bonds. Sigma bonds are individually stronger because the overlap is more effective, but each pi bond still adds to the total bond order.

TIP

Fast hybridization shortcut: count the electron domains (bonded atoms plus lone pairs) on the atom of interest. 2 gives sp, 3 gives sp2, 4 gives sp3, 5 gives sp3d, 6 gives sp3d2. You never need orbital diagrams on the MCAT; the steric number gives both the geometry and the hybridization.

A key consequence: sigma bonds permit free rotation, but pi bonds lock the geometry because rotating would break the p-orbital overlap. This rotational restriction is why double bonds create cis and trans (E/Z) isomers. When pi bonds alternate with single bonds (conjugation, as in benzene or 1,3-butadiene), the pi electrons delocalize across adjacent p orbitals and lower the energy, which is the orbital basis of resonance stabilization.

WORKED EXAMPLE

In hydrogen cyanide (H-C triple-bond N), determine the hybridization of carbon and of nitrogen and count the total sigma and pi bonds.

Carbon is bonded to two atoms (H and N) with no lone pairs, so 2 electron domains, meaning sp hybridized and linear. Nitrogen has the triple bond plus one lone pair, also 2 domains, so it is sp hybridized too. Bonds: the H-C bond is 1 sigma; the C-to-N triple bond is 1 sigma plus 2 pi. Total = 2 sigma bonds and 2 pi bonds. The molecule is linear with a 180 degree H-C-N angle.

## Molecular Polarity and Intermolecular Forces

A molecule's overall polarity is the vector sum of its individual bond dipoles. Each polar bond is a vector pointing from delta plus toward delta minus, with a magnitude set by the electronegativity difference. If these vectors cancel by symmetry, the molecule has no net dipole moment and is nonpolar; if they do not cancel, the molecule is polar and carries a permanent dipole. Lone pairs on the central atom also add to the net dipole.

mu = Q x d

The dipole moment mu equals the magnitude of the partial charge (Q) times the separation distance (d), reported in debye (D). Larger charge separation gives a larger dipole. A molecule is nonpolar when the vector sum of all bond dipoles is zero.

Highly symmetric geometries cause bond dipoles to cancel even when the individual bonds are very polar. Linear CO2, trigonal planar BF3, tetrahedral CCl4, trigonal bipyramidal PCl5, and octahedral SF6 are all nonpolar despite polar bonds, because identical terminal atoms are arranged symmetrically. Swapping one terminal atom (as in CHCl3) breaks the symmetry and makes the molecule polar, and asymmetry from lone pairs is what makes H2O and NH3 polar.

## TRAP

Polar bonds do NOT guarantee a polar molecule. CO2 has two very polar C=O bonds, yet it is nonpolar because the linear geometry makes those two bond dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and cancel. Always check the molecular geometry and symmetry, not just the bonds.

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between molecules; they are far weaker than the intramolecular covalent or ionic bonds within a molecule but they govern physical properties such as boiling point. The weakest and most universal IMF is the London dispersion force, which arises from instantaneous, fluctuating dipoles in the electron cloud. Dispersion forces exist in all matter and grow stronger with more electrons and a larger, more polarizable electron cloud, which is why I2 is a solid while F2 is a gas and why larger hydrocarbons boil higher.

Polar molecules feel additional dipole-dipole forces, the alignment of permanent dipoles. Hydrogen bonding is an especially strong dipole-dipole interaction that occurs only when hydrogen is bonded directly to nitrogen, oxygen, or fluorine and is attracted to a lone pair on another N, O, or F. Ion-dipole forces, between an ion and a polar molecule, are the strongest of this group and are central to dissolving salts in water (hydration).

Intermolecular forces from weakest to strongest

Force	Occurs between	Relative strength	Example
London dispersion	All atoms and molecules (induced dipoles)	Weakest; grows with size and polarizability	N2, hexane, noble gases
Dipole-dipole	Polar molecules	Moderate	Acetone, HCl
Hydrogen bonding	H bonded to N/O/F with an N/O/F acceptor	Strong	H2O, NH3, HF
Ion-dipole	An ion with a polar molecule	Strongest of these	Na+ and Cl- dissolved in water

## KEY

Hydrogen bonding requires H bonded to N, O, or F (small, highly electronegative atoms). It explains water's unusually high boiling point, ice being less dense than liquid water, and DNA base pairing. General IMF ranking: ion-dipole > hydrogen bonding > dipole-dipole > London dispersion, though enough dispersion can overtake weaker dipole forces. Stronger IMFs raise boiling point, melting point, viscosity, and surface tension while lowering vapor pressure. Like dissolves like: polar and ionic solutes dissolve in polar solvents, nonpolar in nonpolar.

## Bond Order, Length, Energy, and MO Theory

Three bond properties move together and are governed by bond order, the number of shared electron pairs between two atoms. As bond order rises from single to double to triple, the atoms are pulled closer, so bond length decreases, and more energy is required to break the bond, so bond energy (bond strength) increases. Bond energy is always positive: breaking bonds is endothermic and forming bonds is exothermic.

Bond order versus length and strength (carbon to carbon)

Bond	Bond order	Approx. length	Approx. energy (kJ/mol)
C-C single	1	154 pm	About 347
C=C double	2	134 pm	About 614
C triple-bond C	3	120 pm	About 839

For bonds of the same order, larger atoms form longer and weaker bonds because their valence orbitals overlap less effectively and the shared pair sits farther from the nuclei. Down the halogens the H-X bond weakens: H-F > H-Cl > H-Br > H-I in bond strength. The same logic explains why bonds between small second-period atoms, such as the N-to-N triple bond at about 945 kJ/mol, are exceptionally strong.

## KEY

Memorize the core chain: higher bond order means shorter bond length means greater bond energy. Resonance produces fractional bond orders that are equal across equivalent positions. Each C-O bond in carbonate (CO3 2-) has a bond order of about 1.33 and an intermediate length, not alternating single and double bonds.

Molecular orbital (MO) theory gives a more complete picture by combining atomic orbitals into molecular orbitals that span the whole molecule. Constructive combination yields lower-energy bonding orbitals; destructive combination yields higher-energy antibonding orbitals, marked with an asterisk such as sigma-star. Electrons fill these orbitals from lowest energy upward, following the same rules used for atomic orbitals.

Bond order = (1/2) x (bonding electrons - antibonding electrons)

A positive bond order means a stable bond; a bond order of 0 (as in hypothetical He2) means no bond forms. Unlike Lewis structures, MO theory allows fractional bond orders.

MO theory succeeds where Lewis structures fail. A simple Lewis structure of O2 shows a double bond with all electrons paired, yet experiment shows O2 is paramagnetic, attracted to a magnetic field, because it has two unpaired electrons. MO theory correctly places those two electrons in degenerate pi-antibonding orbitals and still gives a bond order of 2. A species is paramagnetic if it has any unpaired electrons and diamagnetic if all electrons are paired.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Rank the carbon to oxygen bond lengths in carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), and the carbonate ion (CO3 2-) from shortest to longest.

Bond length is inversely related to bond order. CO has a triple bond (bond order about 3), the highest, so the shortest C-O bond (around 113 pm). CO2 has two C=O double bonds (bond order 2), intermediate (around 116 pm). Carbonate's three resonance structures spread the double-bond character over three bonds, giving each a bond order of about 1.33, the lowest, so the longest C-O bond (around 128 pm). Shortest to longest: CO < CO2 < CO3 2-.
