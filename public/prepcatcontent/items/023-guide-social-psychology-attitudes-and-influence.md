# Social Psychology: Attitudes & Influence

Social psychology studies how people perceive, influence, and relate to others, covering how we explain behavior (attribution), how we resolve the discomfort of inconsistent beliefs (cognitive dissonance), and how groups change individual thought and action (conformity, obedience, persuasion, group dynamics). The MCAT tests these because they tie classic experiments to real-world scenarios and demand that you distinguish closely related, easily confused terms.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Attribution biases: fundamental attribution error = over-attribute OTHERS' behavior to disposition; actor-observer bias = situational for ME, dispositional for YOU; self-serving bias = my successes are internal, my failures external; just-world hypothesis → victim-blaming.
Cognitive dissonance is discomfort from inconsistent cognitions; we reduce it by changing an attitude, adding a justifying cognition, or trivializing, so attitudes often change to MATCH behavior. Festinger-Carlsmith: the $1 group changed attitude MORE ($20 had external justification), insufficient justification effect.
Three influence types: conformity = match a group/norm (Asch lines, ~75% conformed at least once; normative = to be liked, informational = to be right); compliance = a direct request (foot-in-the-door, door-in-the-face); obedience = an authority's order (Milgram, ~65% to 450 V).
Elaboration Likelihood Model: CENTRAL route = argument quality, needs motivation AND ability → lasting, behavior-predictive change; PERIPHERAL route = superficial cues, low motivation OR ability → temporary, easily reversed change.
Bystander effect: more witnesses = less and slower help, via diffusion of responsibility (someone else will act) and pluralistic ignorance (others look calm, so it must be fine).
Group effects: social facilitation (others' presence helps SIMPLE tasks, hurts COMPLEX, arousal boosts the dominant response) vs. social loafing (less effort on shared tasks when your contribution is hidden); group polarization makes a group's prior leaning MORE extreme; groupthink suppresses dissent → bad decisions.

## Attribution Theory and Attributional Biases

Attribution theory describes how people explain the causes of behavior, their own and others'. Every time you ask 'why did she do that?', you make an attribution. Fritz Heider, the founder of attribution theory, proposed that we act as 'naive scientists' who assign behavior to one of two broad causes: internal (dispositional) attributions, which locate the cause inside the person (personality, attitude, ability, or effort), and external (situational) attributions, which locate the cause in the environment or circumstances (luck, task difficulty, social pressure, the weather). The whole topic on the MCAT hinges on whether an explanation points INWARD to the person or OUTWARD to the situation.

Harold Kelley refined this with the covariation model, which says we combine three kinds of information to decide between a dispositional and a situational cause. Consensus is whether OTHER people behave the same way toward the same stimulus (high consensus = many others do it too). Distinctiveness is whether the person behaves this way only toward this PARTICULAR stimulus or toward many stimuli (high distinctiveness = only this one stimulus). Consistency is whether the behavior REPEATS over time and across occasions in the same situation. The classic pattern of HIGH consensus + HIGH distinctiveness + HIGH consistency points to a situational (stimulus) attribution; LOW consensus + LOW distinctiveness + HIGH consistency points to a dispositional (person) attribution. Note that high consistency is needed for a confident attribution of EITHER kind, when consistency is low (a one-off, erratic behavior), we tend to attribute the behavior to passing circumstances or chance rather than to the stable person or stimulus.

Kelley's covariation model, the three informational dimensions

Information	Question it answers	Person (dispositional) attribution when…	Stimulus (situational) attribution when…
Consensus	Do others act this way toward the same thing?	Low (only this person does it)	High (everyone does it)
Distinctiveness	Only toward this one stimulus?	Low (this person reacts this way to everything)	High (only toward this stimulus)
Consistency	Does it repeat over time?	High (always happens)	High (always happens)

TIP

In Kelley's model, HIGH consistency is the prerequisite for a confident attribution either way, it is consensus and distinctiveness that swing the answer between person and stimulus. If consistency is LOW (the behavior is erratic and non-repeating), the cause is usually chalked up to special circumstances/chance. Don't memorize 'high consistency = dispositional'; consistency is high in BOTH classic patterns.

Where attribution gets tested most heavily is the systematic ERRORS we make. The fundamental attribution error (FAE), also called correspondence bias, is the tendency to OVER-attribute OTHER people's behavior to their disposition while UNDER-weighting the situation. When a driver cuts you off, you think 'what a jerk' (disposition) rather than 'maybe they have an emergency' (situation). The FAE is generally found to be more pronounced in individualistic Western cultures than in collectivistic cultures, which more readily incorporate situational and relational context.

The actor-observer bias extends this: we tend to attribute OUR OWN behavior to the situation but OTHERS' behavior to their disposition. If I fail a test, it's because the exam was unfair (situation); if you fail, you didn't study (disposition). Two reasons are usually cited: we have more information about the constraints and history behind our own behavior, and there is a perceptual difference, when we act, the situation is the most salient thing in our visual field, whereas when we watch someone else, that PERSON is the most salient object. The self-serving bias is a motivated, ego-protective pattern: we attribute our SUCCESSES to internal factors (I'm smart) and our FAILURES to external factors (the test was rigged), protecting self-esteem. Finally, the just-world hypothesis (belief in a just world) is the tendency to assume the world is fair, so that people get what they deserve, which can lead to victim-blaming (e.g., 'she must have done something to provoke it').

## TRAP

The MCAT loves to make you separate actor-observer bias from the fundamental attribution error. FAE is specifically about how you judge OTHERS (over-attributing to disposition). Actor-observer bias is the CONTRAST between how you judge yourself (situationally) versus how you judge others (dispositionally). If the question only involves judging another person, it's the FAE; if it explicitly contrasts self vs. other, it's actor-observer bias.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A manager observes an employee arrive late to a meeting and immediately concludes the employee is lazy and unprofessional. Later that week, the manager herself arrives late and explains it was because of unexpected traffic. Which two attributional phenomena are illustrated, and how?

Step 1, Judging the employee: The manager attributes the employee's lateness to disposition ('lazy, unprofessional') while ignoring possible situational causes (traffic, a family emergency). Over-attributing another person's behavior to disposition is the fundamental attribution error. Step 2, Judging herself: For her own identical behavior, she invokes a situational cause (traffic). Step 3, The CONTRAST between explaining her own behavior situationally and the employee's behavior dispositionally is the actor-observer bias. So the scenario illustrates the fundamental attribution error (in how she judges the employee specifically), embedded within the broader actor-observer pattern (situational for self, dispositional for other). Note: this is NOT self-serving bias, because self-serving bias specifically concerns attributing one's own SUCCESSES vs. FAILURES (to protect self-esteem), not late vs. on-time per se.

## Cognitive Dissonance and Attitude Change

Cognitive dissonance, formulated by Leon Festinger, is the psychological discomfort (an aversive arousal state) that arises when a person holds two or more inconsistent cognitions, beliefs, attitudes, or pieces of knowledge, or when behavior conflicts with an attitude. Because the discomfort is unpleasant, people are motivated to reduce it and restore consonance. The single most counterintuitive and most-tested lesson is that people frequently change their ATTITUDE to match their BEHAVIOR, rather than the more intuitive reverse. We rationalize what we have already done.

There are three main ways to reduce dissonance: (1) change one of the conflicting cognitions, most often the attitude, since past behavior usually can't be undone (e.g., 'I smoke' + 'smoking is unhealthy' resolves toward 'smoking isn't that bad / I'll quit later'); (2) add a new consonant cognition that justifies the inconsistency ('smoking relaxes me and lowers my stress, which has its own health benefits'); or (3) reduce the importance of, or trivialize, one of the cognitions ('I could get hit by a bus tomorrow anyway, so the long term risk doesn't matter much').

Festinger and Carlsmith's classic 1959 experiment is the canonical demonstration. Participants performed an extremely boring task, then were paid either $1 or $20 to tell the next participant that the task was enjoyable (a counter-attitudinal statement). Counterintuitively, those paid only $1 later rated the task as MORE enjoyable than those paid $20. The $20 group had ample external justification for lying ('I did it for the money'), so they felt little dissonance. The $1 group had INSUFFICIENT external justification, so to resolve the dissonance between 'the task was boring' and 'I just told someone it was fun for almost nothing,' they changed their attitude: 'I guess the task really was kind of interesting.'

## KEY

Dissonance-driven attitude change is STRONGEST when external justification is LOW (small reward, weak coercion), the 'insufficient justification' effect. Low reward / low pressure → more internal attitude change. High reward / strong pressure → little attitude change, because the behavior is already externally justified. This inverse relationship is a frequent correct answer.

### FIGURE: THE INSUFFICIENT-JUSTIFICATION EFFECT

A line graph with 'external justification for the behavior' on the x-axis (low to high) and 'amount of attitude change toward the behavior' on the y-axis. The curve slopes DOWNWARD: when external justification is low (e.g., paid $1), attitude change is high; when external justification is high (e.g., paid $20), attitude change is low. The figure teaches the inverse relationship, the less you can blame an outside cause for your counter-attitudinal behavior, the more you must change your own attitude internally to reduce dissonance.

Two named sub-phenomena recur on the exam. Justification of effort: the harder or more costly it is to attain something (a painful hazing, an expensive purchase), the more we tend to value it, because devaluing it would create dissonance with the suffering or cost we endured. Post-decisional dissonance (and the resulting 'spreading of alternatives'): after making a difficult choice between close options, we boost our evaluation of the CHOSEN option and downgrade the REJECTED one, to feel better about a decision we can no longer reverse.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A college student endures a humiliating, physically demanding week-long initiation to join a fraternity. After joining, she rates the fraternity as exceptionally prestigious and worthwhile, more so than members who joined an identical fraternity with no initiation. Which dissonance concept explains this, and what is the underlying reasoning?

Step 1, Identify the inconsistency: 'I suffered greatly to get in' conflicts with 'this group might not be worth it.' Holding both cognitions is dissonant. Step 2, Identify the resolution: Since the suffering is already in the past and cannot be undone, the student resolves the dissonance by changing the cognition she CAN change, her evaluation of the group, inflating its value so that the effort feels justified. Step 3, Name it: This is justification of effort, a specific form of cognitive dissonance reduction. The comparison group with no initiation has no suffering to justify, so they feel no comparable pressure to inflate the group's value (which is why the no-initiation members rate it lower). Common trap: this is NOT simply conformity or persuasion, the driver is internal discomfort produced by the student's OWN costly behavior, the hallmark of dissonance.

## Conformity, Compliance, and Obedience

Social influence comes in three flavors that the MCAT insists you keep straight. Conformity is changing your behavior or beliefs to match a group, usually WITHOUT any explicit request, it is a response to a (often implicit) norm. Compliance is changing behavior in response to a direct REQUEST from someone who has no authority over you. Obedience is changing behavior in response to a direct ORDER from an authority figure. The dimension that increases across them is roughly the directness and the power of the source: norm (conformity) → request (compliance) → command from an authority (obedience).

Three forms of social influence

Type	Trigger	Source	Classic study
Conformity	Group norm (often implicit)	Peers / the group	Asch (line judgment)
Compliance	A direct request	Anyone (no authority required)	Foot-in-the-door / door-in-the-face
Obedience	A direct order/command	An authority figure	Milgram (shock experiment)

Solomon Asch's line experiments are the conformity benchmark. Participants judged which of three comparison lines matched a standard line, an easy, unambiguous perceptual task. But confederates unanimously gave an obviously WRONG answer first. About 75% of real participants conformed to the wrong answer on at least ONE trial, while only about a third (~37%) of total responses across critical trials conformed, most people went along at least once but not every time. Asch's task was deliberately UNAMBIGUOUS, so the conformity it produced was largely NORMATIVE (going along to fit in) rather than informational.

Asch's findings let us distinguish the two engines of conformity. Normative social influence is conforming to be liked or accepted and to avoid rejection, you go along publicly even if you privately disagree (public compliance without private acceptance). Informational social influence is conforming because you believe the group has accurate information, especially in AMBIGUOUS situations, here you actually change your private belief (private acceptance). Because Asch's perceptual judgment was clear-cut, participants who conformed usually knew the group was wrong, so the influence was predominantly normative.

Asch also identified the factors that change conformity. Conformity INCREASES with group size up to roughly 3 4 confederates and then plateaus (adding more people beyond that adds little). Conformity drops dramatically if there is even ONE dissenter who breaks the group's unanimity, unanimity is critical, and a single ally restores most independence. Conformity is also higher when responses are PUBLIC rather than private and when the individual feels less competent on the task.

TIP

Decide normative vs. informational by asking WHY the person conformed. To fit in / avoid disapproval / be liked → normative ('norms = social acceptance'). Because they think the group is RIGHT, especially when the answer is unclear → informational ('information = getting it right'). Ambiguous task usually means informational; clear task with peer pressure usually means normative.

Stanley Milgram's obedience studies are the most-cited social-psychology experiments on the exam. Participants, assigned the role of 'teacher,' were ordered by an experimenter in a lab coat to deliver escalating electric shocks (labeled up to 450 volts) to a 'learner' (a confederate who was never actually shocked) for wrong answers. Despite the learner's scripted screams and apparent distress, about 65% of participants obeyed fully, all the way to the maximum 450-volt switch. Milgram concluded that ordinary people will obey a legitimate authority even to the point of harming another person, partly by entering an 'agentic state,' in which they see themselves as instruments carrying out the authority's will and shift responsibility onto the experimenter.

Obedience in Milgram's paradigm DECREASED when: the authority figure was physically distant or absent; the authority lacked legitimacy (an ordinary man rather than a scientist gave the orders); the experiment was moved away from a prestigious institution (e.g., run from a run-down office building); the victim was made more proximate (in the same room, or the teacher had to physically place the learner's hand on a shock plate); or other 'teachers' (confederates) rebelled and refused. The Stanford Prison Experiment (Philip Zimbardo) is often paired with Milgram to illustrate how assigned social ROLES and deindividuation can produce cruel behavior. It is heavily criticized methodologically (small sample, demand characteristics, lack of true experimental control), so for the MCAT, associate it with the POWER OF SITUATIONAL ROLES and with deindividuation (loss of individual identity and self-awareness within a group, leading to impulsive, norm-violating behavior) rather than treating it as rigorous proof.

## TRAP

Don't confuse Asch with Milgram. Asch = peers + an unambiguous perceptual line task = CONFORMITY (~75% conformed on at least one trial). Milgram = an authority's orders + delivering apparent harm = OBEDIENCE (~65% went to the maximum 450 V). Swapping the percentages, the mechanisms (norms vs. authority), or the labels is a classic distractor.

## Persuasion and Attitude Formation

An attitude is an evaluation, favorable or unfavorable, of a person, object, or idea, classically described with three components (the ABC model): Affective (feelings), Behavioral (action tendencies), and Cognitive (beliefs). A central question is WHEN attitudes actually predict behavior. Attitudes best predict behavior when they are strong, specific to the behavior in question, formed through direct personal experience, easily accessible (come to mind quickly), and when situational pressures are weak. Weak or general attitudes, or strong external pressures, can decouple attitude from behavior.

Persuasion is the process of changing attitudes. The dominant MCAT framework is the Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) of Petty and Cacioppo, which proposes two routes to persuasion. The CENTRAL route persuades through the quality and logic of the ARGUMENTS, the listener carefully elaborates on (thinks deeply about) the content. It requires BOTH motivation (the issue matters to them) AND ability (they have the cognitive resources, time, and knowledge to evaluate it). Central-route persuasion produces attitude change that is more LASTING, more resistant to counter-persuasion, and more predictive of behavior. The PERIPHERAL route persuades through superficial cues UNRELATED to argument quality, the attractiveness or perceived credibility of the source, the sheer NUMBER (not quality) of arguments, catchy slogans, or emotional appeals. It operates when motivation OR ability is low, and it produces change that is more temporary and easily reversed once the cue fades.

Elaboration Likelihood Model, central vs. peripheral routes

Feature	Central route	Peripheral route
What persuades	Argument quality / logic / evidence	Superficial cues (looks, source, length, slogans)
Requires	High motivation AND high ability	Low motivation OR low ability
Depth of processing	Deep elaboration	Shallow / heuristic
Durability of change	Lasting, resistant to counterargument	Temporary, easily reversed
Predicts behavior?	Yes, well	Weakly

Other persuasion factors worth knowing: source credibility and trustworthiness boost persuasion; MODERATE (not extreme) fear appeals work best, but only when paired with a clear, achievable action the audience can take to reduce the threat (too much fear without a remedy triggers denial or avoidance); and the message-learning ('Yale') approach highlights characteristics of the source (who is speaking), the message (what is said), and the audience (to whom). The foot-in-the-door technique (a compliance strategy) gets a SMALL request agreed to first, which makes a later LARGE request more likely to succeed, and is often explained via consistency / self-perception (people align later behavior with their initial commitment and self-image). The door-in-the-face technique does the reverse: start with an unreasonably LARGE request that gets refused, then the real, SMALLER request seems reasonable by contrast (explained via the contrast effect and reciprocal concessions, the requester appears to have 'compromised').

WORKED EXAMPLE

A public-health campaign wants to durably change young adults' attitudes about vaccination. Version A airs a 15-second ad featuring a popular, attractive celebrity simply saying 'Get vaccinated, it's the cool thing to do.' Version B provides a detailed, evidence-based explanation of how vaccines work and their measured risk reduction, targeted at people who have already shown interest in the topic. Using the Elaboration Likelihood Model, predict which version produces more durable attitude change and why.

Step 1, Classify the routes: Version A relies on a peripheral cue (an attractive, popular celebrity) and offers no substantive argument, so it engages the PERIPHERAL route. Version B presents high-quality arguments to a motivated, able audience (already interested, and given evidence they can evaluate), so it engages the CENTRAL route. Step 2, Apply ELM predictions: Central-route persuasion produces attitude change that is more lasting, more resistant to counterargument, and more predictive of actual behavior, whereas peripheral-route change is temporary and easily reversed once the cue (the celebrity's appeal) fades. Step 3, Conclusion: Version B should produce more DURABLE change in attitudes (and better follow-through on actually getting vaccinated). Worth noting (and the kind of nuance the MCAT may reward): Version A could still reach a BROADER, less-motivated audience and create useful short term shifts, but the question asks specifically about DURABILITY, where the central route wins.

## Group Dynamics and Social Behavior

Being in a group changes individual behavior in predictable ways. Social facilitation is the tendency to perform SIMPLE or well-learned tasks BETTER, but COMPLEX or novel tasks WORSE, in the presence of others. The mechanism (Zajonc's arousal explanation) is that the presence of others increases physiological arousal, and arousal strengthens the DOMINANT (most likely) response, which is the correct response for easy, well-practiced tasks (so performance improves) but the incorrect response for hard, unpracticed tasks (so performance suffers). Social loafing is a different group effect, in which individuals exert LESS effort on a COLLECTIVE task than when working alone, because individual contributions are not identifiable and responsibility is diffused; loafing shrinks when individual outputs can be measured and evaluated.

## TRAP

Social facilitation vs. social loafing: facilitation is about being WATCHED/EVALUATED while doing YOUR OWN task (arousal boosts the dominant response, helps easy tasks, hurts hard ones). Loafing is about a SHARED group output where your individual contribution is hidden, so you slack off. Note the seeming paradox: an audience can IMPROVE your performance (facilitation), yet being part of a pooled group effort can REDUCE it (loafing), different setups, don't merge them.

Group decision-making has its own distortions. Group polarization is the tendency for group discussion to STRENGTHEN the members' pre-existing average attitude, so that the group's final position is MORE extreme (in the same direction) than the average of the members' initial individual positions (a cautious group becomes more cautious; a risk-leaning group becomes riskier, the 'risky shift' is just one direction of this). Groupthink (Irving Janis) is a mode of thinking in highly cohesive groups in which the desire for harmony and unanimity OVERRIDES realistic appraisal of alternatives, leading to poor decisions. Symptoms include an illusion of invulnerability, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, an illusion of unanimity, rationalization, and 'mindguards' who shield the group from contrary information. Prevention includes encouraging open dissent, appointing a devil's advocate, and seeking outside opinions.

Group polarization vs. groupthink

Concept	Core idea	What it produces
Group polarization	Discussion intensifies the group's pre-existing leaning	A MORE EXTREME version of the members' prior average attitude (same direction)
Groupthink	Cohesion + desire for harmony suppresses dissent	Poor, unchallenged decisions; a faulty premature consensus

Finally, the bystander effect is a heavily tested helping-behavior phenomenon (popularized by the Kitty Genovese case and studied experimentally by Latané and Darley). The more bystanders present at an emergency, the LESS likely any single person is to help, and the longer help is delayed. Two mechanisms drive it: diffusion of responsibility (each person assumes someone else will or should act, so personal responsibility is spread thinly across the crowd) and pluralistic ignorance (people look to others for cues; seeing others remain calm and inactive, each privately concludes there is no real emergency). Latané and Darley's decision model requires a potential helper to (1) NOTICE the event, (2) INTERPRET it as an emergency, (3) assume RESPONSIBILITY, (4) know HOW to help, and (5) DECIDE to act and implement help, failing at any single stage blocks helping.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A man collapses on a crowded subway platform with 40 people present. Several people glance at him, see that no one else is reacting, and continue on their way; each silently assumes another commuter has already called for help. No one assists for several minutes. Identify the overarching effect and the TWO specific mechanisms at work.

Step 1, Overarching effect: Help is withheld and delayed despite many witnesses, this is the bystander effect, in which a LARGER number of bystanders reduces the likelihood that any one individual helps. Step 2, Mechanism A: Each person 'silently assumes another commuter has already called for help.' Spreading personal responsibility across the crowd so that no individual feels obligated is diffusion of responsibility. Step 3, Mechanism B: People 'glance at him, see that no one else is reacting,' and use others' calm inaction as evidence that nothing is wrong. Misreading others' nonresponse as a sign that there is no emergency is pluralistic ignorance. The two mechanisms compound to suppress helping. Tip for the exam: if the question stresses 'no one feels it's their job,' that's diffusion of responsibility; if it stresses 'everyone else looked calm, so I figured it was fine,' that's pluralistic ignorance.
