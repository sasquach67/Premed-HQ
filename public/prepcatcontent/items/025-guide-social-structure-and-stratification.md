# Social Structure & Stratification

Social structure is the patterned web of institutions, statuses, and roles that organizes society and distributes resources unequally; the MCAT tests how the three major sociological theories explain it, how stratification and mobility work, and how demographic forces shape populations and health. Expect passage questions that ask you to apply a theoretical lens or interpret stratification and population data.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Three theories by level + core question: functionalism = macro, social stability/shared function; conflict theory = macro, competition over scarce resources and power (Marx: bourgeoisie vs. proletariat); symbolic interactionism = micro, meaning built through everyday interaction (Mead, Cooley, Goffman).
Merton's functions: manifest = intended and recognized; latent = unintended and unrecognized; dysfunction = disrupts stability. 'Unintended' alone defines latent, NOT dysfunction, a classic trap.
Weber's stratification is multidimensional: CLASS (economic/wealth), STATUS (prestige/honor), POWER (political influence); ranking high on one and low on another is status inconsistency. Davis-Moore is the functionalist claim that inequality is necessary to motivate talent.
Stratification systems run closed to open: caste (closed, ascribed) < estate < class (open, achievement-based) < meritocracy (ideal). Mobility is vertical vs. horizontal, intergenerational vs. intragenerational, and can be structural or downward.
The demographic transition's signature is the LAG: mortality falls first (Stage 2 = fastest growth), then fertility falls (Stage 3); replacement fertility is ~2.1. Population pyramids reveal age-sex structure and growth trajectory.
There is a social gradient in health: each step up in SES predicts better health all the way up the ladder, not just for the poorest, the MCAT's favorite link from stratification to medicine, and conflict theory fits it best.

## Social Structure and Social Institutions

Social structure refers to the relatively stable, patterned arrangements of relationships, institutions, statuses, and roles that organize social life. Rather than treating society as a random collection of individuals, sociologists view it as a structured system in which people occupy positions (statuses) and follow expected behaviors (roles) that persist beyond any single person. This is the 'macro' scaffolding the MCAT asks you to recognize: when a behavior is shaped by a person's position in society rather than their individual psychology, you are looking at social structure.

Social institutions are the well-established, enduring patterns of organization that meet a society's fundamental needs. The MCAT expects fluency with the major ones: the FAMILY (regulates reproduction, socializes children, provides emotional and material support), EDUCATION (transmits knowledge, skills, and cultural values; sorts people into roles), RELIGION (provides shared meaning, moral norms, and social cohesion), GOVERNMENT/POLITICS (makes and enforces laws, distributes power), the ECONOMY (organizes production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services), and HEALTH AND MEDICINE (defines and treats illness, organizes care). Mass media is sometimes added as a modern institution that shapes information and norms.

A key distinction: a status is a social position (e.g., 'physician,' 'daughter'), while a role is the set of behaviors and expectations attached to that status. An ASCRIBED status is assigned at birth or involuntarily (race, sex, the family you are born into); an ACHIEVED status is earned through effort or choice (college graduate, surgeon). A MASTER STATUS is the one status that dominates how others perceive a person and overrides their other statuses. Conflicts arise as role strain (tension WITHIN a single role) and role conflict (tension BETWEEN two or more different roles); a related idea is role exit, the process of disengaging from a role central to one's identity.

## KEY

Institutions are not buildings or organizations, they are durable patterns of norms and relationships that fulfill core societal needs. A specific hospital is an organization; 'medicine' as a system of defining and treating illness is the institution.

The Major Social Institutions and Their Functions

Institution	Primary societal function	Example MCAT angle
Family	Reproduction, socialization, emotional/economic support	Primary agent of socialization in childhood
Education	Transmits knowledge and cultural values; social sorting	Latent functions: peer networks, 'hidden curriculum'
Religion	Shared meaning, moral norms, social cohesion	Durkheim: collective conscience and solidarity
Government	Lawmaking, distribution and regulation of power	Site of power and legitimate authority
Economy	Production and distribution of goods/services	Source of class divisions (conflict theory)
Medicine	Defining and treating illness; organizing care	The 'sick role'; medicalization

## TRAP

Don't confuse role strain with role conflict. Role strain is tension among competing demands WITHIN ONE role (a professor who must both mentor and grade the same student). Role conflict is tension BETWEEN two or more separate roles (being a parent and an on-call physician at the same time). The keyword is whether the conflicting demands come from one role or several.

## The Three Major Theoretical Perspectives

Almost every social-structure passage on the MCAT can be analyzed through one of three theoretical lenses. Knowing the level of analysis (macro vs. micro) and the core question each theory asks lets you label a paragraph in seconds.

FUNCTIONALISM (structural functionalism) is a macro-level theory that views society as a system of interconnected parts, each contributing to the stability and survival of the whole, much like organs in a body. Associated with Émile Durkheim and later Talcott Parsons and Robert Merton, it asks: 'What function does this structure serve?' Merton refined it with two crucial pairs. MANIFEST FUNCTIONS are the intended, openly recognized consequences of a structure (schools educate children). LATENT FUNCTIONS are unintended, often unrecognized consequences (schools provide childcare, create friendship and dating networks). A DYSFUNCTION is any consequence that disrupts social stability or the smooth operation of the system.

CONFLICT THEORY is also macro-level but emphasizes competition over scarce resources, power, and inequality rather than harmony. Rooted in Karl Marx, it frames society as an arena of struggle between groups with opposing interests, classically the BOURGEOISIE (owners of the means of production) versus the PROLETARIAT (workers who sell their labor). Conflict theorists argue that institutions tend to benefit dominant groups and perpetuate inequality. Max Weber broadened the tradition beyond purely economic class to include status and power. Modern extensions include race-conflict and gender-conflict approaches. The core question: 'Who benefits, and who is exploited?'

SYMBOLIC INTERACTIONISM is a MICRO-level theory focused on how individuals create and interpret meaning through everyday interactions and shared symbols (especially language). Associated with George Herbert Mead, Charles Horton Cooley, Herbert Blumer (who named the perspective), and Erving Goffman, it holds that social reality is built and negotiated face-to-face. Mead described how the self develops through taking the role of others (the 'I' and the 'me,' culminating in the 'generalized other'). Cooley's 'looking-glass self' holds that we form our self-concept based on how we imagine others perceive and judge us. Goffman's dramaturgy frames social life as theater, with front-stage and back-stage performances. The core question: 'How do people construct shared meaning?'

Comparing the Three Core Theories

Feature	Functionalism	Conflict theory	Symbolic interactionism
Level of analysis	Macro	Macro	Micro
Society is...	A stable system of interdependent parts	An arena of competition and inequality	A product of everyday interaction and meaning
Key figures	Durkheim, Parsons, Merton	Marx (also Weber)	Mead, Cooley, Blumer, Goffman
Central question	What function does it serve?	Who benefits / who is exploited?	How is meaning created?
View of inequality	Functional and necessary (Davis-Moore)	Result of exploitation by elites	Reproduced through labels and interaction
Weakness	Downplays conflict and change	Downplays stability and consensus	Misses large-scale structure

TIP

Two extra perspectives sometimes appear. FEMINIST theory is a conflict-style lens centered on gender inequality and patriarchy. SOCIAL CONSTRUCTIONISM (closely tied to symbolic interactionism) holds that categories like race, gender, and illness are created and defined by society rather than fixed by nature. RATIONAL CHOICE / exchange theory models behavior as cost-benefit maximization, with people seeking to maximize rewards and minimize costs in interactions.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A study finds that, beyond educating students, public schools keep children supervised during work hours, allowing parents to participate in the labor force. A researcher calls this an important but rarely acknowledged effect of mass education. Which concept best describes this effect, and from which theoretical tradition does the concept come?

Educating students is the MANIFEST function of schools, the openly intended, recognized purpose. Providing childcare so parents can work is an unintended, generally unacknowledged consequence, which is the definition of a LATENT function. The manifest/latent distinction was developed by Robert Merton within the FUNCTIONALIST tradition, which analyzes the consequences (functions) that structures have for the stability of the social system. Note that the effect is beneficial and stabilizing, so it is a latent function, not a dysfunction (a dysfunction would disrupt the system). The trap answer would call this a dysfunction simply because it is unintended, but 'unintended' alone defines latent, not dysfunctional.

## Social Stratification and Class

Social stratification is the structured ranking of groups of people in a hierarchy of unequal wealth, power, and prestige. Crucially, stratification is a property of SOCIETY, not of individuals, it persists across generations and is built into institutions. The MCAT wants you to see inequality as patterned and reproduced, not merely the sum of individual differences.

Max Weber argued that stratification is MULTIDIMENSIONAL, captured by three distinct components. CLASS is the economic dimension, a person's wealth, income, and relationship to the market. STATUS (or prestige) is the social-honor dimension, the respect and esteem attached to a position (clergy and professors may have high prestige but modest income). POWER is the political dimension, the ability to achieve goals and influence others even against opposition (Weber located the organized pursuit of power in 'parties'). A person can rank high on one dimension and low on another, which is called STATUS INCONSISTENCY.

Socioeconomic status (SES) is the composite measure most often used to rank individuals, combining income, education, and occupational prestige (and sometimes wealth). Note the difference between INCOME (the flow of money earned over time, e.g., a salary or wages) and WEALTH (the total stock of assets owned minus debts, i.e., net worth). Wealth is far more unequally distributed than income and is more readily passed across generations, which is central to how inequality reproduces itself.

Stratification systems lie on a continuum from CLOSED (mobility nearly impossible; position is ascribed) to OPEN (mobility possible; position is achieved). A CASTE system is closed: position is fixed at birth, justified by tradition or religion, and intermarriage between strata is restricted (an endogamous system). A CLASS system is open: position is based largely on achievement and economic standing, and movement is possible. Between them, the historical ESTATE system (feudal nobility, clergy, and commoners) was largely closed but allowed limited mobility. MERITOCRACY is the idealized open system in which position is determined purely by individual merit and effort, an ideal that real societies only approximate.

Stratification Systems on the Closed-to-Open Continuum

System	Open or closed?	Basis of position	Mobility
Caste	Closed	Ascribed at birth (often religious/traditional)	Essentially none
Estate	Mostly closed	Birth + landholding (feudal)	Very limited
Class	Open	Achievement + economic standing	Possible (up or down)
Meritocracy (ideal)	Fully open	Individual merit and effort alone	Determined by ability

Two theories explain WHY stratification exists. The DAVIS-MOORE THESIS (functionalist) argues that stratification is universal and necessary because society must offer greater rewards to motivate the most talented people to fill the most important and difficult positions, inequality is functional. Conflict theorists counter that stratification mainly reflects the ability of dominant groups to hoard resources and perpetuate their advantage, and that 'importance' is often defined to justify existing privilege rather than to reflect genuine social need. A common objection is that the thesis cannot explain why many clearly essential jobs (e.g., sanitation, caregiving) are poorly rewarded.

## TRAP

The MCAT loves testing the Davis-Moore thesis as the FUNCTIONALIST justification of inequality (inequality motivates talent into important roles). If a question describes inequality as 'necessary' or 'functional,' think Davis-Moore / functionalism, NOT conflict theory. Conversely, if inequality is framed as exploitation or resource-hoarding by elites, that is the conflict critique of Davis-Moore.

## KEY

Weber's three dimensions, CLASS (economic), STATUS (prestige), POWER (political), are the single most testable stratification fact. Memorize them and remember that someone can rank high on one and low on another (status inconsistency).

## Social Mobility, Poverty, and Social Reproduction

Social mobility is movement of individuals or groups within the stratification hierarchy. The MCAT tests four overlapping types. VERTICAL mobility is movement UP or DOWN to a different social class (a promotion that changes class standing). HORIZONTAL mobility is movement to a different position at the SAME level (changing jobs without changing class). INTERGENERATIONAL mobility compares a person's standing to that of their parents, across generations. INTRAGENERATIONAL mobility describes change within a single person's own lifetime.

Mobility can be driven by individual effort or by large structural shifts. STRUCTURAL mobility is movement caused by changes in society itself, for example, an entire cohort moving up because industrialization or a tech boom created many new higher-status jobs, independent of any individual's merit. Distinguishing structural from individual mobility is a favorite MCAT nuance because it shows that 'success' often reflects the economy's shape, not just personal achievement.

Poverty is defined two ways. ABSOLUTE POVERTY is the inability to secure the basic necessities for survival (food, shelter, clean water), a fixed, life-threatening threshold. RELATIVE POVERTY is falling below the standard of living considered normal in one's own society; it is relational and varies by place and time. A related idea is SOCIAL EXCLUSION, being shut out of full participation in economic, political, and social life. The CYCLE OF POVERTY describes how poverty perpetuates itself across generations: limited resources restrict education and opportunity, trapping families in disadvantage.

SOCIAL REPRODUCTION is the broader process by which social inequality is transmitted from one generation to the next through institutions, especially the family and schools. Pierre Bourdieu argued this works through forms of CAPITAL: economic capital (money/assets), CULTURAL CAPITAL (knowledge, tastes, credentials, and habits valued by dominant groups), and SOCIAL CAPITAL (valuable networks and connections). Advantaged families pass on cultural and social capital that schools reward, so inequality is reproduced even in nominally 'meritocratic' systems.

Types of Social Mobility

Type	Definition	Example
Vertical	Up or down to a different class	Factory worker becomes a manager
Horizontal	Move at the same level	Nurse switches to a teaching job, same SES
Intergenerational	Compared to one's parents	Child of laborers earns a professional degree
Intragenerational	Within one's own lifetime	Clerk works up to company executive
Structural	Driven by societal/economic change	A whole cohort rises due to a tech boom

TIP

Bourdieu's three capitals are high yield. ECONOMIC = money/assets; CULTURAL = knowledge, credentials, and 'highbrow' tastes; SOCIAL = networks and connections ('who you know'). Cultural and social capital explain why meritocracy is imperfect, children of advantaged families arrive at school already fluent in the dominant culture schools reward.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A man grows up in a working-class household. After college he becomes a corporate attorney, a higher class position than his parents held. Sociologists note that during this period the economy shifted toward professional service jobs, expanding the number of such positions overall. Identify the form(s) of mobility illustrated and explain the role of structural factors.

Relative to his PARENTS, the man has moved up, so this is INTERGENERATIONAL mobility. Because he changed CLASS position (working class to professional/upper-middle class), it is also VERTICAL (upward) mobility, and since the rise occurred within his own career and lifetime it is INTRAGENERATIONAL as well. The note that the economy expanded professional jobs points to STRUCTURAL mobility: part of his rise reflects a society-wide increase in higher-status positions, not purely individual merit. A strong MCAT answer recognizes that the same case can be classified along several axes at once (intergenerational + vertical + intragenerational + structural), and that structural change can lift many people regardless of individual effort.

## TRAP

Mobility is not always upward. DOWNWARD vertical mobility (e.g., losing class standing after job loss or illness) is just as real. And horizontal mobility involves NO change in class rank, don't assume 'mobility' means 'moving up.'

## Demographics and Population Dynamics

Demography is the statistical study of human populations, their size, composition, and change over time. The MCAT tests demographic STRUCTURE (how a population is distributed across categories like age, sex, race/ethnicity, immigration status, and SES) and the PROCESSES that change it: fertility (births), mortality (deaths), and migration (movement in and out). These three drivers determine whether a population grows or shrinks.

Population change = (Births − Deaths) + (Immigration − Emigration)

Births − Deaths is the natural increase (or decrease); Immigration − Emigration is net migration. Rates are usually expressed per 1,000 people per year. The crude rate of natural increase = crude birth rate − crude death rate, and excludes migration.

Key measures to recognize: the CRUDE BIRTH RATE (births per 1,000 population per year) and the TOTAL FERTILITY RATE (the average number of children a woman is expected to have over her lifetime). A total fertility rate of about 2.1 is REPLACEMENT-LEVEL fertility, just enough to keep a population stable over the long run, with the value exceeding 2.0 to account for the sex ratio at birth (slightly more boys are born) and mortality before reproductive age. Below 2.1, a population will eventually shrink without immigration. Mortality measures include the crude death rate, life expectancy, and the infant mortality rate (deaths under age one per 1,000 live births), which is a sensitive indicator of a society's overall health and development.

Migration involves PUSH factors (conditions driving people away, war, poverty, persecution) and PULL factors (conditions attracting people to a destination, jobs, safety, family). Immigration adds to a receiving population, emigration subtracts from a sending one. Migration reshapes the age and ethnic composition of both regions and is a major source of population change in low-fertility countries.

### FIGURE: POPULATION PYRAMIDS (AGE-SEX STRUCTURE)

A population pyramid is a back-to-back horizontal bar chart with age groups (cohorts) stacked vertically (youngest at the bottom, oldest at the top) and one sex on each side. A pyramid with a WIDE base tapering to a narrow top indicates an EXPANSIVE population: high birth rates, many children, rapid growth, typical of less-developed countries. A roughly rectangular, COLUMN-shaped pyramid with similar-width bars across most ages indicates a STATIONARY population with stable, near-replacement fertility and slow or no growth. A pyramid that is NARROW at the base and bulges in the middle/upper ages is a CONSTRICTIVE population: low birth rates and an aging population, with a shrinking young cohort, typical of wealthy, post-industrial countries. Reading the shape tells you the population's growth trajectory and its future dependency burden (the ratio of dependents to working-age people).

The DEMOGRAPHIC TRANSITION MODEL describes how populations shift as societies industrialize, in four (sometimes five) stages. Stage 1 (pre-industrial): high birth rates AND high death rates, so population is stable and low. Stage 2 (early industrializing): death rates fall sharply (better sanitation, food, and medicine) while birth rates stay high, producing RAPID population growth. Stage 3 (mature industrial): birth rates finally fall as children become costly, women gain education and enter the workforce, and child survival improves; growth slows. Stage 4 (post-industrial): both rates are low, so population stabilizes (and in a sometimes-added Stage 5 may decline). The model's key insight is that mortality falls BEFORE fertility, and the lag between them is what drives a society's population boom.

Stages of the Demographic Transition

Stage	Birth rate	Death rate	Population change
1 Pre-industrial	High	High	Stable (low)
2 Early industrial	High	Falling rapidly	Rapid growth
3 Mature industrial	Falling	Low	Growth slows
4 Post-industrial	Low	Low	Stable or declining

## KEY

The defining feature of the demographic transition is the LAG: death rates drop before birth rates do (Stage 2). That gap is exactly when population growth is fastest. If a question describes booming growth from falling mortality but still-high fertility, that is Stage 2.

## TRAP

Distinguish FERTILITY (actual births occurring) from FECUNDITY (the biological maximum potential to reproduce). Fertility is always lower than fecundity. Also, replacement-level fertility is ~2.1, not 2.0, the fraction above 2 covers childhood mortality and the slightly male-skewed sex ratio at birth.

## Spatial Inequality, Globalization, and Health Disparities

Stratification is not only social but SPATIAL, inequality is mapped onto geography. URBANIZATION is the growing concentration of populations in cities, driven historically by industrialization and rural-to-urban migration. Within and around cities, RESIDENTIAL SEGREGATION sorts groups by race and class into different neighborhoods, concentrating advantage in some areas and disadvantage in others. SUBURBANIZATION and the outflow of resources and jobs can leave under-resourced urban or rural areas behind, creating ghettos or food and medical 'deserts.' The result is SPATIAL INEQUALITY: where you live shapes your access to schools, jobs, safety, healthy food, and medical care.

GLOBALIZATION, the increasing economic, political, and cultural interconnection of societies across the world, extends stratification to the global scale. It can spread technology and raise living standards but also intensify inequality between and within nations, shift labor across borders, and link local outcomes to distant economic forces. On the MCAT, globalization is often paired with conflict theory (who gains and who loses from global integration?).

For the MCAT's psych/soc section, the most important application of stratification is to HEALTH. There is a well-documented SOCIAL GRADIENT IN HEALTH: health outcomes improve steadily with each step UP the socioeconomic ladder. This is not simply 'the poor are sick and everyone else is fine', even middle-class people have worse average health than the wealthy, and the wealthy outlive the merely comfortable. The gradient operates through material conditions (housing, nutrition, environmental exposures), access to and quality of healthcare, chronic stress, and health behaviors shaped by circumstances. HEALTH DISPARITIES are differences in health outcomes across social groups (by race, class, gender, or geography) that are avoidable and rooted in social inequality rather than biology.

Related concepts the MCAT may invoke: the SICK ROLE (Parsons, society grants ill people exemption from normal duties and absence of blame for the illness, in exchange for their viewing illness as undesirable, seeking competent help, and trying to recover), MEDICALIZATION (the process by which non-medical problems come to be defined and treated as medical conditions), and the distinction between social epidemiology's focus on how social position 'gets under the skin' versus purely biological explanations. SES, race, and gender intersect to shape exposure to risk and access to care, INTERSECTIONALITY is the idea that overlapping disadvantaged identities compound inequality rather than simply adding up.

## KEY

The SOCIAL GRADIENT in health is a top exam concept: each step up in SES predicts better health, all the way up the ladder. It reframes health disparities as a structural, society-wide pattern rather than an individual failing.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A public-health study finds that across an entire city, residents' average life expectancy rises consistently with neighborhood median income, not only is the poorest neighborhood the unhealthiest, but each successively wealthier neighborhood is healthier than the one below it, even among comfortably middle-income areas. Which sociological concepts best explain this finding, and which theoretical perspective most naturally interprets it?

The stepwise relationship, health improving at every increment of income rather than only at the bottom, is the textbook signature of the SOCIAL GRADIENT IN HEALTH. The fact that outcomes track NEIGHBORHOOD income points to SPATIAL INEQUALITY and the way residential segregation concentrates resources and risks geographically; these are HEALTH DISPARITIES rooted in social position, not biology. CONFLICT THEORY most naturally interprets the pattern, because it frames the gradient as a product of the unequal distribution of resources and power: higher-SES groups secure better housing, environments, and healthcare, while lower-SES groups bear concentrated disadvantage. (A functionalist reading is strained here, since a steep, persistent health gradient is hard to portray as a beneficial, system-stabilizing arrangement.) A complete answer names the social gradient, ties it to spatial inequality and SES-based health disparities, and selects the conflict lens.

TIP

When a passage links a SOCIAL variable (income, race, neighborhood, education) to a HEALTH outcome and asks for a theory, conflict theory is usually the best fit because it foregrounds unequal access to resources. Use symbolic interactionism instead when the focus is on labeling, stigma, or the meaning of illness in face-to-face interaction, and functionalism when the focus is on illness's effect on social roles (the sick role).
