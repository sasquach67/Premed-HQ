# Glycolysis

Glycolysis is the universal, cytosolic pathway that splits one glucose into two pyruvate, netting 2 ATP and 2 NADH. The MCAT tests it because it integrates enzyme kinetics, thermodynamics, allosteric regulation, and metabolic crossroads, and because PFK-1 control and pyruvate's fates appear constantly in passages.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

1 glucose → 2 pyruvate, netting 2 ATP and 2 NADH (4 ATP made gross − 2 invested); occurs in the cytosol of all cells and requires no oxygen.
PFK-1 (step 3) is the rate limiting AND committed step: activated by AMP, ADP, and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate; inhibited by ATP and citrate.
Three irreversible/regulated steps, hexokinase (1), PFK-1 (3), pyruvate kinase (10), are the control points and the reactions bypassed in gluconeogenesis.
ATP comes from substrate-level phosphorylation at phosphoglycerate kinase (7) and pyruvate kinase (10); NADH is made only at G3P dehydrogenase (step 6), the lone redox step.
Anaerobically pyruvate → lactate (LDH, animals) or ethanol + CO₂ (yeast) solely to regenerate NAD⁺, no extra ATP; aerobically pyruvate → acetyl-CoA via PDH → Krebs.
Hexokinase: low Km, G6P-inhibited, everywhere. Glucokinase: high Km, not product-inhibited, liver/β-cell glucose sensor, insulin-induced.
Insulin turns glycolysis ON (↑F-2,6-BP via PFK-2); glucagon turns it OFF and gluconeogenesis ON (↓F-2,6-BP).

## The Big Picture: Why Cells Run Glycolysis

Glycolysis (literally 'sugar splitting') is the central, most ancient pathway of carbohydrate catabolism. It takes one six-carbon molecule of glucose and breaks it into two three-carbon molecules of pyruvate. Along the way the cell captures a modest amount of energy as ATP and reducing power as NADH. Every step happens in the cytosol, no membranes or organelles are required, which is why even cells lacking mitochondria (mature red blood cells) and organisms living without oxygen can run it. This universality is exactly why the MCAT loves it: glycolysis is the hub where enzyme kinetics, thermodynamics, and metabolic regulation all converge.

Think of glycolysis in two halves. The first half is the 'energy investment phase' (steps 1 5): the cell actually spends 2 ATP to prime and destabilize glucose, then splits it into two interconvertible three-carbon sugars. The second half is the 'energy payoff phase' (steps 6 10): each three-carbon fragment is oxidized and dephosphorylated, harvesting 2 NADH and 4 ATP in total. Because everything downstream of the split happens twice (once per three-carbon molecule), you multiply the payoff-phase products by two.

Glucose + 2 NAD⁺ + 2 ADP + 2 Pᵢ → 2 Pyruvate + 2 NADH + 2 H⁺ + 2 ATP + 2 H₂O

The net reaction of glycolysis. NET ATP is 2 (4 produced − 2 invested). 2 NAD⁺ are reduced to 2 NADH. Two molecules of water are released (one per turn at the enolase step). This equation is the single most important thing to memorize.

## KEY

Net yield per glucose: 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvate. Gross ATP made = 4; ATP invested = 2. If a passage asks for 'net' vs. 'total/gross' ATP, read carefully, the MCAT exploits this distinction.

TIP

Glycolysis is anaerobic in the sense that it requires no O₂, but it is NOT the same as fermentation. Glycolysis runs identically whether oxygen is present or not. What changes with oxygen is the FATE of pyruvate and how NADH is reoxidized to NAD⁺. Keep these two ideas separate.

## The Ten Steps, Enzyme by Enzyme

You do not need to memorize every intermediate's structure, but you must know the enzymes at the three regulated (irreversible) steps, which steps consume or produce ATP/NADH, and the general flow. The three irreversible steps, catalyzed by hexokinase, PFK-1, and pyruvate kinase, have large negative ΔG values under cellular conditions and serve as the control points. Everything else is near-equilibrium and freely reversible (these reversible steps are shared with gluconeogenesis, which simply bypasses the three irreversible ones with its own enzymes).

The 10 Steps of Glycolysis

Step	Reaction	Enzyme	Energy event	Reversible?
1	Glucose → Glucose-6-phosphate (G6P)	Hexokinase / glucokinase	−1 ATP	Irreversible
2	G6P → Fructose-6-phosphate (F6P)	Phosphoglucose isomerase	·	Reversible
3	F6P → Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (F1,6BP)	Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)	−1 ATP (rate limiting + committed)	Irreversible
4	F1,6BP → DHAP + G3P	Aldolase	splits 6C → two 3C	Reversible
5	DHAP ⇌ G3P	Triose phosphate isomerase	interconverts the two trioses	Reversible
6	G3P → 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG)	Glyceraldehyde-3-P dehydrogenase	+1 NADH; adds Pᵢ (oxidation)	Reversible
7	1,3-BPG → 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PG)	Phosphoglycerate kinase	+1 ATP (substrate-level)	Reversible
8	3-PG → 2-phosphoglycerate (2-PG)	Phosphoglycerate mutase	moves the phosphate	Reversible
9	2-PG → Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)	Enolase	−1 H₂O (dehydration; H₂O released)	Reversible
10	PEP → Pyruvate	Pyruvate kinase	+1 ATP (substrate-level)	Irreversible

Walking through the logic: in step 1, phosphorylating glucose traps it inside the cell (the charged phosphate cannot cross the membrane) and keeps intracellular free glucose low to maintain the gradient favoring uptake. Step 3 (PFK-1) is the true commitment, once F6P becomes fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, the molecule is destined to be split and catabolized. Step 4 cleaves the six-carbon sugar into dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P); step 5 converts DHAP into a second G3P so that both halves proceed through the payoff phase. From step 6 onward, count everything twice.

A horizontal split shows F1,6BP cleaving into DHAP and G3P, with a curved arrow converting DHAP into a second G3P. The lower 'payoff' block runs as parallel duplicate lines (×2): each G3P → 1,3-BPG (with a green NADH arrow OUT) → 3-PG (with a green ATP arrow OUT at phosphoglycerate kinase) → 2-PG → PEP (with a blue H₂O arrow OUT at enolase) → pyruvate (with a second green ATP arrow OUT at pyruvate kinase). The figure visually shows why payoff products are doubled: there are two parallel lower tracks but only one upper track.

## TRAP

Step 6 (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase) is the ONLY oxidation reduction step in glycolysis and the only place NADH is made. The phosphate added here comes from inorganic phosphate (Pᵢ) in solution, NOT from ATP, this is how glycolysis builds the high energy acyl-phosphate of 1,3-BPG without spending ATP. Students wrongly assume an ATP is consumed to add this phosphate; it is not. The energy comes from the oxidation of the aldehyde that simultaneously reduces NAD⁺ to NADH.

## Hexokinase vs. Glucokinase: A Tale of Two Km's

Step 1 is catalyzed by hexokinase in most tissues, but the liver and pancreatic β-cells use an isozyme called glucokinase (hexokinase IV). The differences between them are a favorite MCAT enzyme-kinetics question because they elegantly link Km, Vmax, and physiological role.

Hexokinase has a LOW Km (high affinity), so it is essentially saturated even at low blood glucose, muscle and brain grab glucose greedily and phosphorylate it. Hexokinase is also feedback-inhibited by its own product, glucose-6-phosphate, so when downstream G6P accumulates, the enzyme slows. Glucokinase has a HIGH Km (low affinity) and a higher Vmax, so its activity rises steeply as blood glucose climbs after a meal. Crucially, glucokinase is NOT inhibited by G6P, allowing the liver to keep taking up glucose to store as glycogen when sugar is abundant. This makes glucokinase a 'glucose sensor.'

Hexokinase vs. Glucokinase

Property	Hexokinase	Glucokinase (Hexokinase IV)
Tissue	Most cells (e.g., muscle, brain)	Liver, pancreatic β-cells
Km for glucose	Low (high affinity)	High (low affinity)
Saturated at low (fasting) glucose?	Yes	No (activity rises with glucose)
Inhibited by G6P?	Yes (product inhibition)	No
Vmax	Lower	Higher
Role	Ensure constant glucose uptake	Glucose sensor; store excess as glycogen
Induced by insulin?	No	Yes

WORKED EXAMPLE

After a carbohydrate-rich meal, blood glucose rises from 5 mM to 10 mM. Why does the liver dramatically increase glucose phosphorylation while resting muscle does not change much?

Muscle hexokinase has a very low Km (~0.1 mM), so at both 5 mM and 10 mM glucose it is already essentially fully saturated, it is running at nearly Vmax in both cases and cannot speed up much. Liver glucokinase has a high Km (its half-maximal point, S0.5, is around 7 8 mM), placing the normal physiological range right on the steep, rising part of its rate-versus-substrate curve. Going from 5 to 10 mM glucose therefore moves glucokinase from a low fraction of Vmax up toward roughly half of Vmax, sharply increasing flux. (If you model glucokinase with simple Michaelis Menten kinetics using Km ≈ 10 mM, then v/Vmax goes from 5/(5+10) = 1/3 at 5 mM to 10/(10+10) = 1/2 at 10 mM, a real, sizable jump.) In addition, glucokinase is not product-inhibited by G6P, so the G6P being channeled into glycogen does not throttle it. The net result: the liver acts as a sink for the post-meal glucose load, exactly what a 'glucose sensor' should do.

TIP

Memory hook: 'gluCokinase is in the liver and is Cool with high glucose (high Km, no product inhibition).' Hexokinase is the high-affinity workhorse everywhere else. Note: glucokinase's real curve is sigmoidal, but the MCAT only expects the high-Km vs. low-Km contrast and what it means physiologically.

## PFK-1: The Rate-Limiting and Committed Step

If you remember only one regulatory enzyme in all of metabolism for the MCAT, make it phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1). It catalyzes step 3, the transfer of a phosphate from ATP onto fructose-6-phosphate to make fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. This is both the rate limiting step (the slowest, flux-determining step) and the committed step (once F1,6BP forms, the molecule cannot easily turn back and is committed to glycolysis). Step 1 is not truly committing because G6P can branch off into glycogen synthesis or the pentose phosphate pathway; PFK-1 is the point of no return.

PFK-1 is an allosteric enzyme, meaning effectors bind sites other than the active site to shift it between its high-activity (R) and low-activity (T) states. The logic of its regulation is beautifully intuitive: it speeds up when the cell is energy-poor and slows when the cell is energy-rich or already has plenty of building blocks.

Allosteric Regulators of PFK-1

Regulator	Effect on PFK-1	Physiological signal
ATP	Inhibits (at a separate allosteric site)	High energy charge, slow glycolysis
AMP	Activates	Low energy charge, speed up glycolysis
ADP	Activates	Low energy charge
Citrate	Inhibits	Krebs cycle full / abundant building blocks
Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F2,6BP)	Activates (most potent activator)	Insulin / fed state, promote glycolysis
H⁺ (low pH)	Inhibits	Excess lactate, prevent severe acidosis

## TRAP

ATP is both a SUBSTRATE and an allosteric INHIBITOR of PFK-1. At high concentration, ATP binds a separate inhibitory (allosteric) site and lowers the enzyme's affinity for F6P. AMP relieves this inhibition. So 'high ATP slows glycolysis' is correct even though ATP is consumed in the reaction, the MCAT writes wrong answers exploiting this apparent paradox.

Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F2,6BP) deserves special attention. It is NOT a glycolytic intermediate, it is a dedicated regulatory molecule made from F6P by a second enzyme, PFK-2 (phosphofructokinase-2). F2,6BP is the most potent activator of PFK-1; it raises PFK-1's affinity for F6P and overrides ATP inhibition. The same bifunctional PFK-2/FBPase-2 enzyme is controlled by hormones: insulin (fed state) leads to activation of PFK-2 → more F2,6BP → glycolysis ON; glucagon (fasting) triggers phosphorylation (via PKA) that turns PFK-2 off and FBPase-2 on → less F2,6BP → glycolysis OFF and gluconeogenesis ON.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A muscle cell begins sprinting. ATP is rapidly hydrolyzed to ADP and AMP, and lactate accumulates, lowering pH. Predict the net effect on PFK-1 activity and explain the apparent conflict.

Two opposing signals appear. (1) Falling ATP and rising AMP/ADP strongly ACTIVATE PFK-1, exactly what a working muscle needs to accelerate ATP production. (2) The drop in pH from accumulating lactic acid INHIBITS PFK-1, a protective brake against severe acidosis. Early in exercise the energy-charge signal dominates: AMP rises sharply because the adenylate kinase reaction (2 ADP → ATP + AMP) amplifies small ADP changes into large AMP changes, so PFK-1 activity surges and glycolytic flux increases to meet ATP demand. As lactate and H⁺ build during sustained anaerobic effort, the pH brake gains influence, ultimately limiting flux and contributing to fatigue. The takeaway: PFK-1 integrates multiple signals, and the dominant one shifts with metabolic state.

## Counting Energy: ATP, NADH, and Substrate-Level Phosphorylation

The MCAT frequently asks you to tally ATP and NADH, so master the bookkeeping. ATP in glycolysis is generated exclusively by substrate-level phosphorylation, a phosphate is transferred directly from a high energy phosphorylated substrate onto ADP, with no electron transport chain or proton gradient involved. This contrasts with oxidative phosphorylation in mitochondria, which depends on the proton motive force across the inner membrane.

Net ATP = (4 produced) − (2 invested) = 2 ATP per glucose

Investment: 1 ATP at hexokinase (step 1) + 1 ATP at PFK-1 (step 3) = 2 spent. Payoff (×2 because of the two trioses): 1 ATP at phosphoglycerate kinase (step 7) + 1 ATP at pyruvate kinase (step 10), each ×2 = 4 made. Net = 2.

Where Energy Is Spent and Made (per glucose)

Step	Enzyme	ATP	NADH
1	Hexokinase / glucokinase	−1	·
3	PFK-1	−1	·
6	G3P dehydrogenase (×2)	·	+2 NADH
7	Phosphoglycerate kinase (×2)	+2	·
10	Pyruvate kinase (×2)	+2	·
Net		+2 ATP	+2 NADH

About the 2 NADH: under aerobic conditions, this cytosolic NADH cannot directly cross the inner mitochondrial membrane. Its electrons are shuttled in by either the malate aspartate shuttle (which regenerates mitochondrial NADH, worth ~2.5 ATP each in modern P/O counts) or the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle (which feeds FADH₂/ubiquinone, worth ~1.5 ATP each). This is why the 'complete oxidation of glucose' gives a slightly variable total (~30 32 ATP), depending on which shuttle the tissue uses. You do not need to memorize the exact totals, but know that cytosolic NADH must be shuttled and that the shuttle choice changes the yield.

## KEY

Substrate-level phosphorylation occurs at two glycolytic enzymes, phosphoglycerate kinase (step 7) and pyruvate kinase (step 10), plus one place in the Krebs cycle (succinyl-CoA synthetase). All ATP from glycolysis is substrate-level; none requires oxygen or the electron transport chain.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A red blood cell metabolizes 3 molecules of glucose to pyruvate via glycolysis. Mature RBCs lack mitochondria. How many net ATP and how many NADH are produced, and what must happen to that NADH for glycolysis to continue?

Per glucose: net 2 ATP and 2 NADH. For 3 glucose: 3 × 2 = 6 net ATP and 3 × 2 = 6 NADH produced. Because RBCs have no mitochondria, there is no electron transport chain to reoxidize the NADH back to NAD⁺. Glycolysis would soon halt at step 6 once the small NAD⁺ pool is exhausted (step 6 absolutely requires NAD⁺). The RBC solves this by fermentation: pyruvate is reduced to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase, a reaction that oxidizes NADH back to NAD⁺. This regenerated NAD⁺ recycles into step 6, sustaining a continuous, oxygen-independent supply of net 2 ATP per glucose, the RBC's only ATP source. (Note: a fraction of 1,3-BPG can also be diverted to 2,3-BPG in RBCs, which slightly lowers ATP yield, but the core logic, NAD⁺ must be regenerated by lactate fermentation, is the key MCAT point.)

## Fates of Pyruvate: Aerobic vs. Anaerobic

Glycolysis ends with pyruvate, a metabolic crossroads. Its fate depends on oxygen availability and the cell type. The single most important reason any of these fates exist is redox balance: glycolysis consumes NAD⁺ at step 6, and the cell has only a small NAD⁺ pool. NAD⁺ must be regenerated continuously or glycolysis stops dead. The three classic fates each solve this problem differently.

Fates of Pyruvate

Condition / organism	Fate of pyruvate	Enzyme	What regenerates NAD⁺?
Aerobic (O₂ present)	→ Acetyl-CoA, enters Krebs cycle	Pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) complex	Electron transport chain (O₂ as final acceptor, in mitochondria)
Anaerobic, animals / RBC / muscle	→ Lactate (lactic acid fermentation)	Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)	Reoxidation of NADH → NAD⁺ during lactate formation
Anaerobic, yeast / some bacteria	→ Acetaldehyde → Ethanol + CO₂	Pyruvate decarboxylase, then alcohol dehydrogenase	Reoxidation of NADH → NAD⁺ during ethanol formation

Aerobic fate: pyruvate is transported into the mitochondrion and oxidatively decarboxylated by the pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) complex to acetyl-CoA, releasing CO₂ and producing one NADH. Acetyl-CoA then feeds the Krebs (citric acid) cycle. Here glycolytic NADH (via shuttles) plus PDH and Krebs NADH/FADH₂ are reoxidized by the electron transport chain using O₂ as the final electron acceptor, this is why aerobic conditions do not require fermentation.

Anaerobic fate (animals): lactate dehydrogenase reduces pyruvate to lactate, oxidizing NADH back to NAD⁺ in the process. No additional ATP is made in this step, its entire purpose is to recycle NAD⁺ so that net ATP production from glycolysis (2 per glucose) can continue. Lactate made in muscle can travel to the liver and be converted back to glucose via gluconeogenesis (the Cori cycle). Fermentation in yeast instead converts pyruvate to ethanol and CO₂ in two steps (decarboxylation to acetaldehyde, then reduction to ethanol), again reoxidizing NADH to NAD⁺.

The upward arrow (labeled 'O₂ present') leads into a mitochondrion where PDH converts pyruvate → acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH, feeding the Krebs cycle and ETC, with O₂ as the terminal electron acceptor regenerating NAD⁺. ## TRAP

Fermentation produces NO additional net ATP beyond the 2 from glycolysis. The lactate and ethanol steps are purely for regenerating NAD⁺. A common wrong answer claims fermentation 'generates ATP', it does not; glycolysis is the ATP source, and fermentation merely keeps glycolysis running by recycling NAD⁺.

TIP

Don't confuse pyruvate DECARBOXYLASE (yeast; makes acetaldehyde; anaerobic) with pyruvate DEHYDROGENASE (PDH; aerobic; makes acetyl-CoA). Different enzymes, different conditions, different products. Both, however, use thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP, vitamin B1) as a cofactor.

## Integrated Regulation, Hormones, and Feeder Pathways

Zooming out, glycolysis is controlled at its three irreversible enzymes, hexokinase/glucokinase, PFK-1, and pyruvate kinase, by local metabolites and by whole-body hormones. PFK-1 is the dominant control point, but pyruvate kinase is also regulated: it is activated feed-forward by fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (the product of PFK-1, so committing more substrate primes the last step) and inhibited by ATP, alanine, and acetyl-CoA. In the liver, pyruvate kinase is additionally inhibited by glucagon-driven (PKA) phosphorylation during fasting.

Hormonal logic: insulin (high blood glucose, fed state) promotes glycolysis, it leads to activation of PFK-2 to raise F2,6BP (turning PFK-1 on), induces glucokinase, and activates pyruvate kinase. Glucagon and epinephrine (low blood glucose, fasting/stress) inhibit glycolysis and favor gluconeogenesis, glucagon raises cAMP, activates protein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates the PFK-2/FBPase-2 enzyme to lower F2,6BP and phosphorylates (inactivates) liver pyruvate kinase. Knowing 'insulin = glycolysis ON, glucagon = glycolysis OFF / gluconeogenesis ON' answers many questions outright.

Feeder pathways: glucose is not the only sugar that enters glycolysis. (1) Fructose: in the liver, fructose is phosphorylated by fructokinase to fructose-1-phosphate, split by aldolase B into DHAP and glyceraldehyde, which enter glycolysis below the PFK-1 control point, meaning dietary fructose largely bypasses the main regulatory step. (2) Galactose: converted via the Leloir pathway (galactokinase → galactose-1-phosphate → glucose-1-phosphate → G6P); a defect in the enzyme GALT causes classic galactosemia. (3) Glycogen: glycogen phosphorylase releases glucose-1-phosphate, which phosphoglucomutase converts to G6P, feeding glycolysis while skipping the hexokinase ATP cost. (4) Mannose enters as F6P. The MCAT most often tests fructose bypassing PFK-1 and the glucose-1-phosphate ⇌ glucose-6-phosphate link.

Feeder Sugars Entering Glycolysis

Sugar	Key enzyme(s)	Entry intermediate
Glycogen	Glycogen phosphorylase + phosphoglucomutase	Glucose-6-phosphate
Galactose	Leloir pathway (galactokinase, GALT, etc.)	Glucose-6-phosphate
Fructose (liver)	Fructokinase + aldolase B	DHAP + glyceraldehyde (below PFK-1)
Mannose	Hexokinase + phosphomannose isomerase	Fructose-6-phosphate

## KEY

Galactose and glycogen both funnel in as glucose-6-phosphate; liver fructose enters lower down as DHAP and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, bypassing PFK-1's regulation. This is why excess dietary fructose is metabolized in a largely unregulated fashion and is implicated in hepatic lipogenesis.

TIP

High-yield asides the MCAT may slip in: (1) Arsenate mimics phosphate at step 6, forming an unstable 1-arseno-3-phosphoglycerate that hydrolyzes spontaneously; glycolysis still proceeds, but the phosphoglycerate kinase step (step 7) makes NO ATP, arsenate 'uncouples' that ATP synthesis, so net ATP from glycolysis drops to zero. (2) Fluoride inhibits enolase (step 9), which is why fluoride is added to blood collection tubes to halt glycolysis and preserve the glucose reading. (3) 2,3-BPG is a side branch from 1,3-BPG in RBCs that lowers hemoglobin's O₂ affinity (right-shifts the curve). (4) The Warburg effect: cancer cells favor glycolysis followed by lactate fermentation even when oxygen is present ('aerobic glycolysis').
