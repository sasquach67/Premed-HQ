# The Biological Basis of Behavior

Behavior emerges from the nervous and endocrine systems acting together, from single neurons firing action potentials to whole brain regions coordinating cognition and emotion. This guide traces that hierarchy and the genetic and environmental forces that shape it.

## Neurons and Neural Communication

Neurons are the signaling cells of the nervous system. Dendrites receive incoming signals and the cell body (soma) integrates them. If the integrated signal reaching the axon hillock crosses threshold, an action potential travels down the axon to the axon terminals, where it is passed to the next cell. Many axons are wrapped in a myelin sheath, an insulating layer interrupted by gaps called nodes of Ranvier.

Glial cells outnumber neurons and support neural function. Astrocytes regulate the chemical environment and help form the blood-brain barrier, microglia act as the immune cells of the CNS, and ependymal cells produce cerebrospinal fluid. Oligodendrocytes myelinate axons in the central nervous system, while Schwann cells do so in the peripheral nervous system.

At rest, the inside of a neuron sits at about -70 mV relative to the outside. This negative resting potential is maintained by the sodium-potassium pump, which uses ATP to push 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per cycle, and by the membrane being more permeable to K+ at rest through potassium leak channels. The result is high Na+ and Cl- outside the cell and high K+ and large anions inside.

E_ion = (61.5 mV / z) * log10([ion]_outside / [ion]_inside)

The Nernst equation gives the equilibrium potential of a single ion at body temperature, where z is the ion's charge. For K+ this is about -90 mV, which is why the resting membrane (mostly K+ permeable) sits near, but not at, that value.

An action potential has distinct phases. A stimulus depolarizes the membrane to threshold (about -55 mV), opening voltage-gated Na+ channels so Na+ rushes in and the membrane shoots toward roughly +35 mV (depolarization). The Na+ channels then inactivate while voltage-gated K+ channels open, letting K+ flow out to repolarize the cell. A brief undershoot below the resting value (hyperpolarization) follows before the membrane settles back to -70 mV.

## KEY

An action potential is all-or-none: once threshold is reached it fires at full amplitude, and stimulus strength is encoded by firing frequency, not spike size. During the absolute refractory period the Na+ channels are inactivated and no new spike can fire, which caps the firing rate and forces one-way propagation. In myelinated axons the signal jumps from node to node (saltatory conduction), greatly increasing speed.

At the synapse, the arriving action potential opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels in the axon terminal. Ca2+ influx triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis. The neurotransmitter diffuses across and binds receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, changing its potential. The signal is then terminated by reuptake into the presynaptic neuron, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion out of the cleft.

## TRAP

Common traps: the Na+/K+ pump does not produce the rapid voltage swings of the action potential (voltage-gated channels do that); the pump restores the ion gradients more slowly. Myelin comes from oligodendrocytes in the CNS but Schwann cells in the PNS. Repolarization is driven by K+ leaving the cell, not by Na+ flowing back in.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A toxin selectively blocks voltage-gated sodium channels on an axon, while the resting potential stays normal. What happens when a suprathreshold stimulus is applied, and why?

No action potential fires. Reaching threshold normally opens voltage-gated Na+ channels, and the resulting regenerative Na+ influx is what drives the rising phase of the spike. With those channels blocked, the membrane cannot depolarize past threshold, so the action potential fails to propagate even though the Na+/K+ pump still maintains the resting potential. This is the mechanism of tetrodotoxin and of local anesthetics such as lidocaine.

## Neurotransmitters and Receptors

Once released, a neurotransmitter binds receptors that are either ionotropic (ligand-gated ion channels that act within milliseconds) or metabotropic (G protein-coupled receptors that act more slowly through second messengers). Its action ends when it is cleared from the cleft, for example serotonin and dopamine by reuptake transporters and acetylcholine by enzymatic breakdown via acetylcholinesterase. Many psychiatric drugs work precisely by altering this clearance.

Major Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitter	Main function(s)	Clinical / behavioral note
Acetylcholine	Voluntary muscle movement at the neuromuscular junction; parasympathetic signaling; attention, arousal, memory	Depleted in Alzheimer disease; broken down by acetylcholinesterase
Dopamine	Movement, reward, motivation, executive function	Low in Parkinson disease (substantia nigra); overactive in schizophrenia (dopamine hypothesis)
Serotonin (5-HT)	Mood, sleep, appetite, arousal	Low levels linked to depression; target of SSRIs
Norepinephrine	Alertness, arousal, fight-or-flight, mood	Also released as a hormone (noradrenaline)
GABA	Major inhibitory transmitter in the brain (Cl- influx, hyperpolarization)	Reduces anxiety; target of benzodiazepines and alcohol
Glutamate	Major excitatory transmitter; learning and memory	Acts at NMDA receptors in long-term potentiation; excess causes excitotoxicity
Glycine	Inhibitory transmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem	Hyperpolarizes via Cl- channels
Endorphins	Natural pain reduction, reward	Act on opioid receptors

Whether a synapse excites or inhibits the postsynaptic neuron depends on the receptor and the ions it admits. Excitatory neurotransmitters like glutamate produce excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs) that depolarize the cell, while inhibitory neurotransmitters like GABA and glycine produce inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSPs) that hyperpolarize it. The postsynaptic neuron sums these inputs across space (spatial summation) and time (temporal summation) and fires only if the net depolarization at the axon hillock reaches threshold.

## KEY

High-yield associations to memorize: dopamine is low in Parkinson disease and overactive in schizophrenia; serotonin is low in depression; acetylcholine is depleted in Alzheimer disease and drives skeletal muscle contraction at the neuromuscular junction; GABA is the brain's main inhibitory transmitter and the target of anti-anxiety drugs.

Drugs act on synapses in predictable ways. Agonists mimic or enhance a neurotransmitter's effect, while antagonists block it. Reuptake inhibitors such as SSRIs and enzyme inhibitors leave more transmitter in the cleft, prolonging signaling. Knowing whether a drug increases or decreases activity at a given receptor lets you predict its behavioral effect.

## TRAP

A neurotransmitter is not inherently excitatory or inhibitory; its effect depends on the receptor it binds. Acetylcholine excites skeletal muscle at nicotinic receptors but slows the heart through muscarinic receptors. Also distinguish agonists (mimic or enhance) from antagonists (block); an SSRI is a reuptake inhibitor that raises serotonin in the cleft without directly binding the postsynaptic receptor.

## Organization of the Nervous System

The nervous system splits into the central nervous system (CNS), the brain and spinal cord, and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), all the nerves outside it. The CNS integrates information and generates responses, while the PNS carries sensory information in and motor commands out. Afferent neurons bring sensory signals toward the CNS, and efferent neurons carry motor signals away from it.

The PNS divides into the somatic nervous system, which controls voluntary skeletal muscle and carries sensation, and the autonomic nervous system, which controls involuntary functions of glands, cardiac muscle, and smooth muscle. The autonomic system is further split into the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, which generally oppose each other to fine-tune organ activity.

Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic

Feature	Sympathetic	Parasympathetic
Overall role	Fight-or-flight	Rest-and-digest
Heart rate	Increases	Decreases
Pupils	Dilate	Constrict

Bronchioles	Dilate	Constrict
Outflow region	Thoracolumbar	Craniosacral (e.g., vagus nerve)
Postganglionic transmitter	Norepinephrine (mostly)	Acetylcholine
Adrenal medulla	Releases epinephrine into the blood	Not involved

A reflex arc lets the body respond before the brain is involved. In the simplest (monosynaptic) reflex, such as the knee-jerk, a sensory neuron synapses directly onto a motor neuron in the spinal cord. Polysynaptic reflexes, like the withdrawal reflex from a painful stimulus, insert one or more interneurons. Because integration happens in the spinal cord, the response is fast and automatic.

## KEY

Sympathetic activation is the fight-or-flight response: pupils dilate, heart rate and blood pressure rise, airways open, and digestion shuts down. Preganglionic neurons of both divisions release acetylcholine; sympathetic postganglionic neurons mostly release norepinephrine, while all parasympathetic postganglionic neurons release acetylcholine. The adrenal medulla acts like a modified sympathetic ganglion, releasing epinephrine directly into the blood.

## TRAP

Afferent neurons carry sensory information toward the CNS (Afferent = Arrive), and efferent neurons carry motor commands away (Efferent = Exit). In a reflex arc the integration occurs in the spinal cord, so the protective response can happen before the brain consciously registers the stimulus.

## The Brain: Structure and Function

The hindbrain governs basic survival functions. The medulla oblongata controls vital involuntary processes such as heart rate, breathing, and blood pressure. The pons sits above it and relays signals while helping regulate breathing and sleep. The cerebellum coordinates movement, balance, posture, and procedural (motor) learning, so damage to it produces clumsy, uncoordinated movement.

The midbrain manages sensorimotor reflexes and arousal. The superior colliculi handle visual reflexes and the inferior colliculi handle auditory reflexes, for example reflexively orienting toward a sudden sound. The reticular formation, which runs through the brainstem, regulates arousal, alertness, and the sleep-wake cycle.

The forebrain houses higher functions. The thalamus is the brain's sensory relay station, routing input from all senses except smell to the cortex. The hypothalamus maintains homeostasis, regulating hunger, thirst, body temperature, and sexual behavior, and it controls the endocrine system through the pituitary gland. The basal ganglia help smooth and coordinate voluntary movement.

The limbic system is the emotional and memory core of the brain. The amygdala processes fear, aggression, and emotional memory, while the hippocampus is essential for forming new long-term (explicit) memories and for spatial navigation. Other components include the fornix, the septal nuclei (linked to reward), and the cingulate gyrus.

Cerebral Cortex: Lobes

Lobe	Key areas	Functions
Frontal	Primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus), prefrontal cortex, Broca's area	Voluntary movement, executive function, planning, personality, impulse control, speech production
Parietal	Primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus)	Touch, pain, temperature, spatial processing
Temporal	Auditory cortex, Wernicke's area	Hearing, language comprehension, memory
Occipital	Primary visual cortex	Vision

The two cerebral hemispheres control the opposite (contralateral) side of the body and are connected by the corpus callosum. In most people the left hemisphere is dominant for language and analytical processing, while the right is specialized for spatial reasoning, facial recognition, and aspects of emotion. Split-brain studies, in which the corpus callosum is severed, reveal how the hemispheres can process information independently.

Two cortical regions are central to language. Broca's area in the frontal lobe controls speech production; damage causes Broca's (expressive) aphasia, marked by halting, effortful speech with preserved comprehension. Wernicke's area in the temporal lobe handles language comprehension; damage causes Wernicke's (receptive) aphasia, marked by fluent but meaningless speech and poor understanding.

## TRAP

Do not confuse the thalamus and the hypothalamus. The thalamus is the sensory relay station for all senses except smell, which bypasses it. The hypothalamus controls homeostasis and the endocrine system through the pituitary. Also note that the hippocampus is required to form new explicit memories but is not where they are permanently stored.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A stroke patient speaks in slow, effortful, telegraphic phrases but understands spoken and written language well and is visibly frustrated by their own errors. Where is the lesion most likely located?

In Broca's area, in the inferior frontal gyrus of the (usually left) frontal lobe. This is expressive (Broca's) aphasia: speech production is impaired while comprehension stays intact, and patients are typically aware of and frustrated by their deficit. Contrast this with Wernicke's aphasia, from a temporal-lobe lesion, where speech is fluent but meaningless and comprehension is poor.

## Methods for Studying the Brain

Early knowledge of brain function came from lesion and ablation studies, in which damage to a region is linked to a change in behavior. The case of Phineas Gage, whose personality changed dramatically after a rod destroyed much of his frontal lobe, helped reveal the prefrontal cortex's role in impulse control and personality. Because lesions remove a region, they support causal claims about what that region does.

Common Methods

Method	What it measures	Strength
EEG	Summed electrical activity at the scalp	Excellent temporal resolution (sleep stages, seizures); poor spatial resolution
CT	X-ray based structure	Quick structural images
MRI	Magnetic structural imaging	High-resolution anatomy with no radiation
fMRI	Blood oxygenation (BOLD signal)	Function plus structure; good spatial, poorer temporal resolution
PET	Uptake of a radioactive tracer	Maps metabolic activity and receptor distribution
Lesion / ablation	Behavior after tissue loss or removal	Links a region to a function (causal)

Electrical methods record or stimulate neural activity directly. EEG records summed cortical activity through scalp electrodes and is the standard for studying sleep stages and seizures because it captures events on a millisecond timescale. Single-cell recording probes the firing of individual neurons, and electrical or magnetic stimulation can transiently activate or disrupt a region to test its causal role.

TIP

On test day, match the method to the question. If it asks about timing or fast electrical events (sleep stages, seizures, millisecond responses), pick EEG for its temporal resolution. If it asks where in the brain something happens, pick fMRI or PET for their spatial resolution. Lesion and stimulation studies establish causation, while correlational imaging only shows association.

No single method is best for everything; researchers choose based on the question, the needed resolution, and practical constraints such as cost, radiation, and whether the technique can be used in living, behaving people. Combining methods, such as pairing EEG timing with fMRI localization, often gives a fuller picture than any one technique alone.

## Hormones and Behavior

Hormones influence behavior more slowly and diffusely than neurotransmitters, but their effects often last longer. The endocrine glands and their feedback loops are covered in depth in the endocrine signaling guide; here the focus is their behavioral output. Because hormones travel in the blood, a single hormone can reach many tissues at once and shift mood, motivation, aggression, and reproductive behavior together.

The classic stress pathway is the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis: the hypothalamus releases CRH, which prompts the anterior pituitary to release ACTH, which drives the adrenal cortex to release cortisol. Cortisol mobilizes energy and sustains the stress response, complementing the rapid surge of epinephrine from the adrenal medulla. Chronically elevated cortisol is linked to impaired memory and mood disturbances.

## KEY

Two control systems work in parallel. The nervous system is fast and short-lived (milliseconds, electrical and chemical, narrowly targeted), while the endocrine system is slow and long-lasting (seconds to days, hormones in the blood, widespread). The hypothalamus is the bridge between them, linking the brain to the pituitary and the rest of the endocrine system.

## Genes, Environment, and Behavior

The nature versus nurture debate asks how much of behavior comes from genes and how much from experience. The modern answer is that the two interact: genes set ranges and predispositions while environment shapes how they are expressed. Behavioral genetics studies this interaction using family, twin, and adoption studies to separate genetic from environmental contributions to behavioral variation.

Heritability is the proportion of variation in a trait within a population that is attributable to genetic variation, expressed on a scale from 0 to 1. It is a statement about variance across a group, not about how genetically determined a trait is in any one person, and it depends on the particular population and environment studied.

h^2 = 2 * (r_MZ - r_DZ)

Falconer's formula estimates heritability from the difference between monozygotic (r_MZ) and dizygotic (r_DZ) twin correlations, exploiting the fact that MZ twins share ~100% of their genes and DZ twins ~50%.

Twin studies compare monozygotic (identical) twins, who share nearly all their genes, with dizygotic (fraternal) twins, who share about half. If a trait shows higher concordance in MZ than DZ twins, that points to a genetic contribution. Adoption studies add another angle by comparing children with their biological versus adoptive parents, helping to disentangle shared genes from shared environment.

## TRAP

Heritability is one of the most misread terms on the MCAT. It is the share of trait variation in a population due to genetic variation; it says nothing about an individual and it is not fixed. A heritability of 0.8 does not mean a trait is 80% genetic in you or that the environment cannot change it. Heritability is specific to the population and environment in which it was measured.

Epigenetics explains how the same genome can yield different phenotypes. Mechanisms such as DNA methylation and histone modification turn genes on or off without altering the underlying DNA sequence. Environmental factors including stress, diet, and maternal care can change these marks, and some changes can be passed to offspring, providing a molecular route through which experience shapes biology.

Evolutionary psychology argues that many behaviors are adaptations shaped by natural selection because they improved survival and reproduction. Inclusive fitness extends this beyond personal reproduction: helping genetic relatives (kin selection) can spread shared genes, which helps explain altruism toward family. Innate, species-typical behaviors such as fixed action patterns and reflexes contrast with learned behaviors that depend on experience.

WORKED EXAMPLE

For a trait, monozygotic twins show a correlation of 0.80 and dizygotic twins 0.40. Estimate the heritability and interpret it.

Using Falconer's formula, h^2 = 2(r_MZ - r_DZ) = 2(0.80 - 0.40) = 0.80. So about 80% of the variation in this trait across the studied population is attributable to genetic variation. This describes population variance, not how genetically fixed the trait is in any one person, and it is specific to the population and environment measured. Because the MZ correlation is below 1.0, environment still contributes to the remaining variation.
