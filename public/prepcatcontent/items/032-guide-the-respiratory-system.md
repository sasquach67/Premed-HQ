# The Respiratory System

The respiratory system ventilates the lungs for gas exchange, carries oxygen and carbon dioxide in the blood, and helps regulate blood pH. This guide covers airway anatomy, the mechanics of breathing, lung volumes, gas exchange and transport, and the neural control of ventilation.

## Airway Anatomy and the Two Functional Zones

The respiratory tract is divided into two functional regions. The conducting zone is the plumbing that moves air, and the respiratory zone is where gas actually crosses into the blood. Air travels nose or mouth, then pharynx, larynx, trachea, primary bronchi, smaller bronchi, bronchioles, terminal bronchioles, respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and finally the alveoli. Everything from the nose down to the terminal bronchioles is conducting; the respiratory bronchioles onward make up the respiratory zone.

The conducting zone has three main jobs: it warms incoming air to body temperature, humidifies it to full saturation with water vapor, and filters out particles. Filtering relies on the mucociliary escalator, a layer of mucus (secreted by goblet cells) that traps debris and is swept upward by beating cilia toward the pharynx to be swallowed or expelled. Because no gas exchange occurs here, the roughly 150 mL of air filling these airways at the end of each inspiration is called the anatomic dead space.

The respiratory zone is built for diffusion. Hundreds of millions of alveoli give an enormous gas-exchange surface area (roughly 70 square meters in an adult), and each alveolus is wrapped in pulmonary capillaries. The barrier between alveolar air and blood, the respiratory membrane, is extremely thin: alveolar epithelium, a fused basement membrane, and capillary endothelium. This short diffusion distance and large area let O2 and CO2 equilibrate rapidly.

The alveolar wall contains two epithelial cell types. Type I pneumocytes are large, flat squamous cells that form roughly 95% of the alveolar surface area and provide the thin barrier for gas diffusion. Type II pneumocytes are smaller cuboidal cells that secrete pulmonary surfactant and also act as progenitor cells to regenerate the epithelium after injury. Roaming alveolar macrophages (dust cells) engulf any pathogens or particles that reach the alveoli.

Surfactant is a mixture rich in phospholipid (chiefly dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine). It coats the moist inner alveolar surface and disrupts the cohesive hydrogen bonding among water molecules, lowering surface tension. This serves two purposes: it reduces the inward collapsing force on alveoli, and it increases lung compliance so that less effort is needed to inflate the lungs. Premature infants whose type II cells have not yet matured make too little surfactant, causing neonatal respiratory distress syndrome with stiff, collapse-prone lungs.

P = 2T / r

Laplace's law for an alveolus: collapsing pressure P rises as surface tension T increases or radius r decreases. Without surfactant, smaller alveoli would generate higher pressures and empty into larger ones; surfactant lowers T more in small alveoli, stabilizing them.

## KEY

Only the respiratory zone exchanges gas. The roughly 150 mL of conducting-airway air at end-inspiration is anatomic dead space and never reaches the alveoli. This is why slow, deep breaths ventilate the alveoli more effectively than rapid, shallow breaths that move the same total volume per minute, because a smaller fraction of each shallow breath is wasted filling dead space.

## Mechanics of Ventilation

Air moves by bulk flow down a pressure gradient: gas flows from high to low pressure. The body cannot pump air directly, so it changes the volume of the thoracic cavity, which changes alveolar pressure by Boyle's law. Increasing thoracic volume drops alveolar pressure below atmospheric, drawing air in; decreasing volume raises alveolar pressure above atmospheric, pushing air out.

P1 * V1 = P2 * V2

Boyle's law (constant temperature and amount of gas): pressure and volume are inversely related. Expanding the chest increases lung volume, so alveolar pressure falls and air flows inward.

Inspiration is an active process. The diaphragm contracts and flattens downward, and the external intercostal muscles pull the rib cage up and out, both expanding the thorax. During heavy breathing, accessory muscles (scalenes, sternocleidomastoid) add to this. Quiet expiration at rest is passive: the muscles simply relax and the stretched elastic tissue of the lungs recoils, shrinking thoracic volume. Forced or active expiration recruits the internal intercostals and the abdominal muscles, which push the diaphragm upward and squeeze the rib cage down.

The lungs are enclosed by the pleura: a visceral layer on the lung and a parietal layer on the chest wall, separated by a thin fluid-filled pleural cavity. The intrapleural pressure in this space is normally negative (a few mmHg below atmospheric) because the elastic lungs tend to recoil inward while the chest wall tends to spring outward, and the two pull against each other across the fluid. The transpulmonary pressure (alveolar pressure minus intrapleural pressure) is the distending pressure that holds the alveoli open.

## TRAP

Intrapleural pressure is normally negative (subatmospheric), and this negative pressure is what keeps the lungs adhered to the chest wall and inflated. Students often wrongly picture positive pressure pushing the lungs open. If the pleural space is breached (pneumothorax), air rushes in, intrapleural pressure rises toward atmospheric, transpulmonary pressure falls, and the lung's elastic recoil collapses it.

Two mechanical properties govern how easily the lungs inflate. Compliance is the change in lung volume per unit change in pressure: high compliance means the lungs stretch easily. Elastic recoil is the tendency to spring back. Surfactant raises compliance by lowering alveolar surface tension, whereas elastin provides the elastic recoil that resists inflation and therefore lowers compliance; fibrosis (scarring) also lowers it, making lungs stiff and hard to inflate. Emphysema destroys elastic tissue, which raises compliance but cripples the recoil needed to drive passive expiration, so air is trapped.

## Lung Volumes and Capacities

Spirometry records the volume of air moved in and out over time, defining four non-overlapping lung volumes. Tidal volume (TV) is the air per normal quiet breath. Inspiratory reserve volume (IRV) is the extra air you can inhale beyond a tidal breath. Expiratory reserve volume (ERV) is the extra air you can forcibly exhale beyond a tidal breath. Residual volume (RV) is the air that always remains after maximal exhalation, keeping alveoli from collapsing.

Term	Definition	Approx. value
Tidal volume (TV)	Air per normal quiet breath	500 mL
Inspiratory reserve (IRV)	Extra air inhaled beyond TV	3000 mL
Expiratory reserve (ERV)	Extra air exhaled beyond TV	1100 mL
Residual volume (RV)	Air left after maximal exhalation	1200 mL
Inspiratory capacity (IC)	TV + IRV	3500 mL
Functional residual capacity (FRC)	ERV + RV	2300 mL
Vital capacity (VC)	IRV + TV + ERV	4600 mL
Total lung capacity (TLC)	VC + RV	5800 mL

Capacities are simply sums of two or more volumes. Inspiratory capacity (IC = TV + IRV) is the most you can inhale starting from a normal resting exhalation. Functional residual capacity (FRC = ERV + RV) is what stays in the lungs after a normal passive exhale. Vital capacity (VC = IRV + TV + ERV) is the maximum you can exhale after a maximum inhale. Total lung capacity (TLC = VC + RV) is everything the lungs can hold.

## KEY

Spirometry only measures air that actually moves in and out of the mouth, so it cannot measure residual volume (RV) directly, nor any capacity that includes RV, namely FRC and TLC. These must be found indirectly using helium dilution or body plethysmography. Vital capacity, in contrast, contains no RV term and is measured directly.

Timed spirometry distinguishes disease patterns. FEV1 is the volume forcibly exhaled in the first second, and FVC is the total forced vital capacity; the FEV1/FVC ratio is normally about 0.8. In obstructive disease (asthma, COPD), airflow out is limited, so FEV1 falls more than FVC and the ratio drops below about 0.7. In restrictive disease (pulmonary fibrosis), the lungs are stiff and all volumes shrink, but airflow is not obstructed, so the FEV1/FVC ratio is preserved or even increased.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient has TV = 500 mL, IRV = 3100 mL, ERV = 1200 mL, and RV = 1200 mL. Calculate the vital capacity (VC), functional residual capacity (FRC), and total lung capacity (TLC). Which of these can a simple spirometer measure directly?

VC = IRV + TV + ERV = 3100 + 500 + 1200 = 4800 mL. FRC = ERV + RV = 1200 + 1200 = 2400 mL. TLC = VC + RV = 4800 + 1200 = 6000 mL. Only VC can be measured directly by spirometry, because it contains no residual volume term. FRC and TLC both include RV, which never leaves the lungs and so cannot be exhaled into a spirometer; they require an indirect method such as helium dilution or body plethysmography.

## Gas Exchange and Partial Pressures

Each gas in a mixture exerts its own partial pressure, and gases diffuse independently down their own partial-pressure gradients (Dalton's law). At sea level, atmospheric pressure is 760 mmHg and dry air is about 21% O2, so inspired PO2 is roughly 160 mmHg. As air is humidified and mixes with alveolar gas, alveolar PO2 falls to about 100-104 mmHg and alveolar PCO2 sits near 40 mmHg.

P_gas = X_gas * P_total

Dalton's law: a gas's partial pressure equals its mole fraction times total pressure. Example: dry-air O2 = 0.21 * 760 ≈ 160 mmHg.

Gas exchange follows these gradients. Mixed venous blood arriving at the alveoli has a PO2 near 40 mmHg and a PCO2 near 46 mmHg. Because alveolar PO2 (about 100) exceeds blood PO2 (40), O2 diffuses into the blood until it equilibrates near 100 mmHg; because blood PCO2 (46) exceeds alveolar PCO2 (40), CO2 diffuses out. The freshly oxygenated blood leaving the lungs is therefore roughly PO2 100 and PCO2 40 mmHg. At the systemic tissues the gradients reverse: tissue PO2 is low and tissue PCO2 is high, so O2 unloads and CO2 is picked up.

C_dissolved = k_H * P_gas

Henry's law: the amount of a gas dissolved in plasma is proportional to its partial pressure and its solubility constant. This governs how much O2 and CO2 physically dissolve, separate from gas bound to carriers.

The rate of diffusion across the respiratory membrane increases with greater surface area, a larger partial-pressure gradient, and higher gas solubility, and decreases with a thicker membrane (Fick's law of diffusion). Disease attacks these factors: emphysema destroys alveolar walls and shrinks surface area; pulmonary fibrosis and pulmonary edema thicken or add fluid to the diffusion barrier, slowing equilibration.

## TRAP

Even though CO2's partial-pressure gradient across the membrane (about 6 mmHg) is far smaller than O2's (about 60 mmHg), CO2 still crosses easily because it is roughly 20 times more soluble in the membrane and plasma (Henry's law). Do not assume a bigger pressure gradient automatically means faster diffusion; solubility matters just as much.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Atmospheric pressure at sea level is 760 mmHg and oxygen makes up about 21% of dry air. What is the partial pressure of oxygen in inspired dry air, and why is alveolar PO2 lower (about 100 mmHg)?

PO2 = 0.21 * 760 ≈ 160 mmHg in dry inspired air. Alveolar PO2 is lower for three reasons: inspired air is humidified, so water vapor (about 47 mmHg) dilutes the other gases; fresh air mixes with the CO2-rich, O2-poor gas already in the alveoli and dead space; and O2 is continuously removed by pulmonary capillary blood. The balance of fresh O2 delivery against ongoing uptake settles alveolar PO2 near 100-104 mmHg.

## Oxygen and Carbon Dioxide Transport in Blood

Only a small fraction of oxygen dissolves in plasma; the vast majority (about 98%) is carried bound to hemoglobin inside red blood cells. Each hemoglobin has four subunits (two alpha and two beta), each with a heme iron that binds one O2, so one hemoglobin carries up to four O2. Binding is cooperative: the first O2 bound makes the next easier, which produces the characteristic sigmoidal (S-shaped) oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve.

OXYGEN-HEMOGLOBIN DISSOCIATION CURVE

A sigmoidal plot of percent hemoglobin saturation (y-axis) against blood PO2 in mmHg (x-axis). Cooperative binding makes saturation rise steeply between about 10 and 40 mmHg, then plateau above roughly 70 mmHg; P50 (the PO2 at 50% saturation) sits near 26-27 mmHg. A right shift (high CO2, low pH, high temperature, high 2,3-BPG) moves the curve rightward and raises P50, favoring O2 unloading; a left shift (fetal hemoglobin, alkalosis, hypothermia, carbon monoxide) lowers P50, favoring O2 loading. The upper plateau explains why arterial saturation stays high even when alveolar PO2 dips moderately.

Hemoglobin's affinity is not fixed. The Bohr effect describes how increased CO2, increased H+ (lower pH), increased temperature, and increased 2,3-bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG) all shift the curve to the right, lowering O2 affinity. These are exactly the conditions in metabolically active tissue, so the Bohr effect cleverly causes hemoglobin to release more O2 right where it is needed most.

## KEY

Memorize the right shift with the mnemonic CADET, face right: increased CO2, Acid (low pH), 2,3-DPG, Exercise, and Temperature all lower hemoglobin O2 affinity, raise P50, and promote unloading to tissues. The left shift is the opposite (alkalosis, low CO2, hypothermia, fetal hemoglobin, carbon monoxide): higher affinity, favoring loading.

Fetal hemoglobin (HbF, two alpha and two gamma chains) binds 2,3-BPG weakly, giving it higher O2 affinity than adult hemoglobin and a left-shifted curve. This lets fetal blood pull O2 from maternal blood across the placenta. Carbon monoxide is the dangerous counterpart: it binds hemoglobin roughly 200 to 250 times more tightly than O2, both blocking O2 binding and left-shifting the remaining sites so that whatever O2 is bound is released poorly.

Carbon dioxide is carried three ways. About 70% is converted to bicarbonate: CO2 enters red blood cells, where carbonic anhydrase rapidly hydrates it to carbonic acid, which dissociates to H+ and HCO3-. The bicarbonate exits the cell in exchange for chloride (the chloride shift, or Hamburger effect) to preserve electroneutrality. About 23% binds directly to the globin portion of hemoglobin as carbaminohemoglobin, and about 7% is dissolved in plasma. By the Haldane effect, deoxygenated hemoglobin carries CO2 more readily, so unloading O2 at tissues helps load CO2.

CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-

Catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase inside red blood cells. This reaction links CO2 transport to blood pH: more CO2 generates more H+, lowering pH, which is why ventilation directly tunes acid-base balance.

WORKED EXAMPLE

During intense exercise, an active muscle has elevated CO2, lactic acid (lower pH), and increased temperature. How do these conditions change hemoglobin's O2 affinity, and how does the change benefit the muscle?

Increased CO2, increased H+ (lower pH), and increased temperature all shift the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve to the right (the Bohr effect), lowering hemoglobin's O2 affinity and raising P50. At the same tissue PO2, hemoglobin therefore releases a larger fraction of its bound oxygen, delivering more O2 exactly where metabolic demand and CO2 production are highest. In the lungs the opposite happens: unloading CO2 and raising pH increases affinity, promoting efficient O2 loading.

## Control of Breathing and Other Functions

Breathing rhythm is generated in the brainstem. The medulla oblongata contains the dorsal respiratory group, which drives the basic rhythm of inspiration, and the ventral respiratory group, which is active during forced breathing and helps drive expiration. The pons fine-tunes this rhythm: the pneumotaxic center limits the duration of inspiration and helps set rate, while the apneustic center tends to prolong inspiration.

Central chemoreceptors in the medulla are the dominant sensors. They do not detect CO2 directly; instead, CO2 from arterial blood crosses the blood-brain barrier into the cerebrospinal fluid, where carbonic anhydrase produces H+. The central receptors sense this rise in CSF H+ (a fall in CSF pH) and increase ventilation. Because H+ and HCO3- themselves cross the barrier poorly, it is really CO2 that the central system tracks.

Peripheral chemoreceptors provide a faster, complementary signal. The carotid bodies (sending signals via the glossopharyngeal nerve, CN IX) and aortic bodies (via the vagus nerve, CN X) monitor arterial PO2, PCO2, and pH directly in the blood. They respond strongly to a large drop in PO2, but only once arterial PO2 falls below roughly 60 mmHg, which is why oxygen is normally a backup rather than the main driver of breathing.

## KEY

Under normal conditions arterial PCO2, sensed as CSF H+ by central chemoreceptors, is the primary minute-to-minute regulator of ventilation. Low PO2 becomes a strong stimulus only when arterial PO2 drops below about 60 mmHg, acting through the peripheral carotid and aortic bodies. So a small rise in CO2 affects breathing more than a comparable small fall in O2.

Because ventilation sets arterial CO2, the lungs are a rapid arm of acid-base regulation. Hyperventilation blows off CO2, lowering H+ and raising blood pH (respiratory alkalosis); hypoventilation retains CO2, raising H+ and lowering pH (respiratory acidosis). The respiratory system can compensate for a metabolic acid-base disturbance within minutes (for example, deep rapid Kussmaul breathing in metabolic acidosis), whereas the kidneys compensate for respiratory disturbances slowly, over hours to days, by adjusting bicarbonate.

Respiratory Acid-Base Disturbances

Respiratory acidosis	Respiratory alkalosis
Primary problem	Hypoventilation	Hyperventilation
Arterial PCO2	High (CO2 retained)	Low (CO2 blown off)

Beyond gas exchange, the respiratory system has several secondary roles. It contributes to acid-base balance as above, helps regulate body temperature and water loss through evaporation in the airways, and enables vocalization as air passes the larynx. It defends the body through the mucociliary escalator and alveolar macrophages. The pulmonary endothelium also houses angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), which converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II, linking the lungs to blood pressure regulation.
