# Endocrine & Hormonal Signaling

The endocrine system uses blood-borne hormones to coordinate slow, widespread, long-lasting physiological responses; the MCAT tests it heavily because it ties together cell signaling, feedback control, metabolism, and physiology. Master the peptide-vs-steroid distinction, second messenger cascades, feedback loops, and the major glands and their hormones, and you can reason through most endocrine passages.

## Endocrine Logic and the Three Hormone Classes

The endocrine system is the body's slow, broadcast communication network. Endocrine glands are ductless: they secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream, which carries them throughout the body. Any cell expressing the matching receptor, the target cell, responds. Contrast this with the nervous system, which is fast, point-to-point, and electrical, and with paracrine (acting on nearby cells), autocrine (acting on the secreting cell itself), and exocrine (secreting through ducts onto a surface, e.g., sweat or digestive enzymes) signaling. On the MCAT, the key trade-off to remember is speed vs. reach: nerves are fast and local; hormones are slower but reach the whole body and act longer.

Because hormones travel in blood and reach every tissue, specificity comes entirely from the receptor, only cells with the right receptor respond, and the SAME hormone can produce different effects in different tissues depending on the receptor subtype and downstream machinery. Epinephrine, for example, dilates blood vessels in skeletal muscle (β2 receptors) but constricts them in the skin and gut (α1 receptors). This is why 'one hormone, many effects' is a recurring passage theme.

Hormones fall into three chemical classes, and the single most important fact about each is whether it is water soluble (hydrophilic) or lipid soluble (lipophilic), because solubility dictates how the hormone travels in blood, where its receptor is, and how fast and how long it acts.

## KEY

All peptide and amine hormones are derived from amino acids. All steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol. That is why the same organ (the adrenal cortex) can make many different steroids, they share a cholesterol backbone and differ only by which enzymes modify it.

## TRAP

Thyroid hormone is the classic exception. It is made from the amino acid tyrosine (plus iodine), so it is technically an amine, BUT it is lipophilic and behaves like a steroid: it is carried by plasma proteins, enters the cell, and binds an intracellular receptor that alters transcription. If a question describes a tyrosine-derived hormone with a slow, long-lasting, gene-regulating action, that is thyroid hormone, not a catecholamine (the other tyrosine-derived hormones, epinephrine/norepinephrine, are hydrophilic and act fast at surface receptors).

## How Peptide and Steroid Hormones Act: Two Fundamentally Different Mechanisms

Because peptide hormones are hydrophilic, they cannot cross the lipid bilayer. They bind receptors embedded in the plasma membrane. The hormone (the 'first messenger') never enters the cell; instead, binding triggers production of an intracellular 'second messenger' that relays and amplifies the signal. This produces a rapid response, often within seconds, but the response is short-lived because the second messenger is quickly degraded. Crucially, peptide hormones act mainly by modifying the activity of pre-existing enzymes (typically by phosphorylation), so no new protein synthesis is required for the immediate effect, which is why they are fast.

Steroid hormones are lipophilic, so they diffuse directly through the plasma membrane and bind receptors in the cytoplasm or nucleus. The hormone receptor complex itself acts as a transcription factor: it binds a hormone response element on DNA and turns specific genes on or off. The response is therefore slow (minutes to hours, because transcription and translation take time) but long-lasting (the newly made proteins persist). One of the most testable consequences: because steroids change gene expression, their effects can outlast the presence of the hormone itself.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A novel hormone is added to cultured cells. Its effect is abolished by actinomycin D (a drug that blocks transcription) but is unaffected by a drug that inhibits adenylyl cyclase (and therefore blocks cAMP-based second-messenger signaling). Is this hormone most likely a peptide or a steroid/thyroid-type hormone? Where is its receptor, and would you expect its effect to appear within seconds or over hours?

Read each clue and ask what mechanism it points to. (1) Blocking transcription abolishes the effect. This means the hormone produces its response by changing gene expression, i.e., it works through new transcription and translation. That is the signature of the steroid/thyroid mechanism, in which the hormone receptor complex acts as a transcription factor. Peptide hormones act on pre-existing enzymes through second messengers and do NOT require new transcription for their immediate effect, so a transcription blocker would not abolish a true peptide response. (2) Inhibiting adenylyl cyclase (the cAMP pathway) has no effect. This rules OUT a cAMP-based peptide mechanism, if the hormone depended on cAMP, blocking adenylyl cyclase would have blunted it. Both clues point the same way: this is a steroid/thyroid-type hormone. Therefore its receptor is intracellular (cytoplasm or nucleus), it acts as a transcription factor, and its effect appears slowly, over hours, because making new protein takes time. (Contrast: a peptide hormone would have been knocked out by the adenylyl cyclase inhibitor, not by actinomycin D, and would act within seconds to minutes.)

## Second Messenger Systems and Signal Amplification

Second messengers are the heart of peptide-hormone signaling, and the MCAT expects you to know the major pathways cold. The most important is the cAMP (cyclic AMP) pathway, which works through a G protein coupled receptor (GPCR). When a hormone binds the GPCR, the receptor activates an associated G protein. A stimulatory G protein (Gs) activates the membrane enzyme adenylyl cyclase, which converts ATP into cAMP. cAMP then activates protein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates target enzymes to change their activity. An inhibitory G protein (Gi) does the opposite, it inhibits adenylyl cyclase and lowers cAMP. So the same enzyme can be turned up or down depending on which G protein the receptor couples to.

Hormone → GPCR → Gs protein → adenylyl cyclase → ATP ⟶ cAMP → PKA → phosphorylated target enzymes → cellular response

This is the canonical cAMP cascade. 'Hormone' is the first messenger; 'cAMP' is the second messenger; PKA (protein kinase A) is the effector kinase. Gs stimulates and Gi inhibits adenylyl cyclase. cAMP is degraded back to AMP by phosphodiesterase, terminating the signal.

The second major pathway is the phosphoinositide (IP3/DAG) pathway. A hormone binds a GPCR coupled to a different G protein (Gq), which activates phospholipase C. Phospholipase C cleaves the membrane lipid PIP2 into two second messengers: IP3 and DAG. IP3 diffuses to the endoplasmic reticulum and triggers release of stored Ca2+ into the cytoplasm; DAG stays in the membrane and activates protein kinase C (PKC). Calcium itself is a key second messenger and often acts by binding the protein calmodulin. So three intracellular messengers, IP3, DAG, and Ca2+, all emerge from this one pathway.

Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are a distinct, non-GPCR mechanism worth knowing because insulin uses one. Here the hormone receptor IS an enzyme: hormone binding causes two receptor subunits to come together (dimerize) and autophosphorylate on tyrosine residues, creating docking sites that launch intracellular signaling (e.g., the Ras/MAP kinase and PI3K cascades). The takeaway for the MCAT: insulin's receptor is a receptor tyrosine kinase, not a GPCR, and it does not use cAMP.

Why bother with a multi-step cascade? Amplification. Each enzyme in the chain can activate many molecules of the next, so a single hormone molecule can ultimately trigger thousands or millions of downstream events. One activated receptor activates several G proteins; each activated adenylyl cyclase makes many cAMP; each PKA phosphorylates many substrates. This cascade amplification is the reason hormones are effective at the very low (nanomolar picomolar) concentrations found in blood.

## TRAP

Don't confuse the messengers. cAMP comes from ATP via adenylyl cyclase and activates PKA. IP3 and DAG come from PIP2 via phospholipase C; IP3 releases Ca2+ from the ER, DAG activates PKC. A common MCAT distractor swaps these, e.g., claiming adenylyl cyclase makes IP3, or that cAMP directly releases calcium. Keep the enzyme-to-messenger pairings straight.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Caffeine inhibits phosphodiesterase. A passage shows that adding caffeine to cells already stimulated by a peptide hormone dramatically prolongs and intensifies the hormone's effect. Mechanistically, why?

Phosphodiesterase is the enzyme that breaks down cAMP into AMP, terminating the cAMP signal. The peptide hormone works by binding a GPCR → activating Gs → adenylyl cyclase → raising cAMP → activating PKA. Normally, phosphodiesterase steadily degrades cAMP, so the signal is self-limiting and short-lived. If caffeine inhibits phosphodiesterase, cAMP is no longer cleared, so cAMP levels stay high and even accumulate. With cAMP elevated for longer, PKA stays active longer, and the downstream phosphorylation response is both prolonged and amplified. The key reasoning chain the MCAT rewards: less degradation of the second messenger → higher steady-state second messenger → stronger, longer response. This also illustrates a general principle: you can boost a signaling pathway either by increasing production (more adenylyl cyclase activity) OR by decreasing breakdown (less phosphodiesterase activity).

## Feedback Loops: How the Body Holds the Setpoint

Endocrine control runs almost entirely on feedback. In negative feedback, the output of a system inhibits its own production, pushing the system back toward a setpoint, this is the dominant mode and the reason hormone levels stay within tight ranges. In positive feedback, the output amplifies its own production, driving the system away from baseline toward a definitive end event; it is rare and usually used to push a process to completion.

The classic negative-feedback architecture is a three-tier axis: hypothalamus → anterior pituitary → peripheral endocrine gland → final hormone. The final hormone feeds back to inhibit BOTH the hypothalamus and the anterior pituitary. For example, in the thyroid axis, the hypothalamus releases TRH, which stimulates the pituitary to release TSH, which stimulates the thyroid to release thyroid hormone (T3/T4). Rising T3/T4 then inhibits TRH and TSH release, throttling its own production. Understanding this loop lets you predict lab patterns instantly.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient's blood tests show LOW thyroid hormone (T4) but HIGH TSH. Is the problem most likely in the thyroid gland itself (primary) or in the pituitary (secondary)? Explain using feedback logic.

Use the negative-feedback loop: T4 normally inhibits TSH. If T4 is low, the brake on TSH is released, so the pituitary ramps up TSH to try to drive the thyroid harder. The fact that TSH is HIGH means the pituitary is working correctly and responding appropriately to the low T4, it is 'shouting' at the thyroid. So the defect must be downstream, in the thyroid gland, which cannot make T4 despite strong TSH stimulation. This is PRIMARY hypothyroidism (problem in the gland itself), signaled by low target hormone + high tropic signal. Contrast: if the pituitary were the problem (secondary), TSH would be LOW or inappropriately normal alongside low T4, because the gland driving the thyroid is failing. General rule: high tropic hormone + low target hormone = the target gland is the problem (primary); low tropic hormone + low target hormone = the pituitary/hypothalamus is the problem (secondary).

## KEY

The two positive-feedback endocrine examples the MCAT expects: (1) oxytocin during labor, cervical stretch → oxytocin → stronger contractions → more stretch → more oxytocin, until delivery; and (2) the estrogen-driven LH surge, late in the follicular phase, high estrogen flips to STIMULATING the pituitary, causing the LH spike that triggers ovulation. Note that estrogen's feedback is normally negative but becomes positive at sustained high levels right before ovulation.

TIP

When a question gives you exogenous (injected) hormone, e.g., anabolic steroids or chronic corticosteroid therapy, expect negative feedback to SUPPRESS the natural axis. Injected testosterone shuts down LH/FSH (causing the testes to shrink and sperm production to fall); chronic prednisone suppresses ACTH and can atrophy the adrenal cortex. Recognizing axis suppression from an outside hormone is a frequent passage twist.

## The Hypothalamic Pituitary Axis: The Master Control

The hypothalamus is the bridge between the nervous and endocrine systems: it receives neural input and converts it into hormonal output, primarily by controlling the pituitary gland (hypophysis) just below it. The pituitary has two functionally distinct lobes that you must not confuse: the anterior pituitary (adenohypophysis) and the posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis).

The ANTERIOR pituitary is true glandular tissue. The hypothalamus controls it via a private blood route called the hypophyseal portal system: the hypothalamus secretes releasing and inhibiting hormones into these portal vessels, which deliver them directly to the anterior pituitary, where they stimulate or inhibit release of the anterior pituitary's own hormones. The anterior pituitary makes six major hormones, neatly remembered by FLAT PiG: FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin (the 'Pi'), and GH. Of these, FSH, LH, ACTH, and TSH are tropic hormones (they target other endocrine glands), while prolactin and GH act mainly on non-endocrine body tissues (GH also works partly via IGF-1 released from the liver).

The POSTERIOR pituitary is NOT glandular, it is neural tissue, an extension of the hypothalamus. It does not synthesize hormones. Instead, two hormones (ADH and oxytocin) are made in hypothalamic neuron cell bodies, transported down the axons, and stored in the posterior pituitary until a neural signal triggers their release into blood. This is why the posterior pituitary is best thought of as a storage-and-release terminal, not a factory.

### FIGURE: TWO-ROUTE CONTROL OF THE PITUITARY

The hypothalamus sits on top. The contrast is the teaching point: anterior = controlled by blood-borne hormones and makes its own products; posterior = controlled by direct nerve fibers and merely releases pre-made hypothalamic hormones.

## TRAP

The #1 pituitary trap: assuming the posterior pituitary 'produces' ADH and oxytocin. It does not, the hypothalamus makes them; the posterior pituitary only stores and releases them. Also remember the posterior pituitary is controlled by direct neurons, NOT by the portal system; the hypophyseal portal system serves only the ANTERIOR pituitary.

TIP

Tropic vs. direct: a 'tropic' hormone acts on another endocrine gland (TSH→thyroid, ACTH→adrenal cortex, FSH/LH→gonads). Direct hormones act on non-endocrine target tissues. GH and prolactin are the main anterior-pituitary hormones that act directly. Knowing which are tropic tells you how many feedback tiers exist.

## The Major Glands and Their Hormones

This is the highest-yield memorization in endocrine biology. For each gland, know the hormone, its class (peptide/steroid/amine), and its main action. Focus on the patterns: antagonistic pairs, what raises vs. lowers a regulated variable, and which hormones are steroids.

Thyroid: produces thyroid hormones T3 and T4 (amine, lipophilic), which RAISE basal metabolic rate, heat production, and developmental/growth pace, they require iodine. The thyroid's parafollicular C-cells also make calcitonin (peptide), which LOWERS blood calcium by inhibiting bone resorption ('calciTONin TONes DOWN calcium'). The parathyroid glands (four small glands on the back of the thyroid) make parathyroid hormone (PTH, peptide), which RAISES blood calcium by stimulating bone breakdown (osteoclasts), increasing renal Ca2+ reabsorption, and activating vitamin D to boost gut absorption. PTH and calcitonin are an antagonistic pair for calcium.

Adrenal glands have two parts. The adrenal CORTEX (outer) makes three classes of steroid: glucocorticoids (cortisol, raises blood glucose, mediates the stress response, is immunosuppressive), mineralocorticoids (aldosterone, raises Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion in the kidney, raising blood volume/pressure), and cortical sex steroids (androgens such as DHEA). The adrenal MEDULLA (inner) is essentially modified sympathetic nervous tissue and secretes the catecholamines epinephrine and norepinephrine (amines) for the fast 'fight-or-flight' response, increased heart rate, bronchodilation, and mobilization of glucose. Cortex = slow steroids; medulla = fast catecholamines.

Pancreas (endocrine islets of Langerhans): beta cells secrete insulin (peptide), which LOWERS blood glucose by promoting cellular uptake and storage (glycogenesis, lipogenesis); alpha cells secrete glucagon (peptide), which RAISES blood glucose via glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis; delta cells secrete somatostatin, which inhibits the release of both insulin and glucagon. Insulin and glucagon are the central antagonistic pair for blood glucose.

Gonads: the ovaries make estrogen and progesterone (steroids), and the testes make testosterone (steroid), all under FSH/LH control from the anterior pituitary. These drive secondary sexual characteristics and the reproductive cycle. The posterior pituitary releases ADH (antidiuretic hormone/vasopressin), which makes the kidney reabsorb water to concentrate urine and raise blood volume, and oxytocin, which drives uterine contraction in labor and milk ejection (let-down). The pineal gland secretes melatonin, regulating circadian rhythm.

## KEY

Memorize the steroid producers: adrenal CORTEX and GONADS (both build hormones from cholesterol). The amine hormones are thyroid T3/T4 (lipophilic, acts like a steroid) and the adrenal-medulla catecholamines (hydrophilic, act fast at surface receptors). Everything else, pituitary hormones, insulin, glucagon, PTH, calcitonin, ADH, oxytocin, is a peptide. This single rule lets you assign hormone class and predict receptor location for almost any hormone on test day.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A passage describes aldosterone and ADH both acting to increase blood pressure but through different stimuli and mechanisms. A drug blocks aldosterone's receptor. Predict the effects on blood Na+, blood K+, and blood volume, and explain why aldosterone (a steroid) acts more slowly than ADH (a peptide).

Aldosterone is a mineralocorticoid from the adrenal cortex. Its normal action: in the distal nephron, it increases Na+ reabsorption (Na+ moves from urine back into blood) and increases K+ secretion (K+ moves into urine). Water follows reabsorbed Na+ osmotically, so blood volume rises. Now block aldosterone's receptor: Na+ reabsorption falls, so MORE Na+ is lost in urine → blood volume falls (this is exactly how aldosterone-antagonist diuretics like spironolactone lower blood pressure). Because aldosterone normally promotes K+ secretion, blocking it means LESS K+ is lost in urine → blood K+ rises (a known side effect: hyperkalemia). Summary: ↓ blood volume, ↑ blood K+, and a tendency toward ↓ blood Na+ (more Na+ is excreted; in practice plasma Na+ may shift only modestly because volume is lost along with it, but directionally the kidney holds onto less Na+). Why is aldosterone slower than ADH? Aldosterone is a STEROID, so it diffuses into tubule cells, binds an intracellular receptor, and acts as a transcription factor, it must synthesize new Na+ channels and Na+/K+ pumps, which takes hours. ADH is a peptide acting via a surface GPCR and a cAMP second messenger that rapidly inserts pre-made aquaporin water channels into the membrane, a response in minutes. Steroid = transcription = slow; peptide = second messenger = fast.

## MCAT Application: Hormone Interactions, Transport, and Glucose/RAAS Integration

Beyond memorizing glands, the MCAT tests how hormones combine and how whole regulatory systems behave. Three interaction concepts recur. Antagonism: two hormones with opposing effects (insulin vs. glucagon; PTH vs. calcitonin). Synergism: two hormones whose combined effect exceeds the sum of their individual effects. Permissiveness: one hormone has little effect alone but is REQUIRED for another hormone to act, the classic example is thyroid hormone permitting epinephrine's full effect; without baseline thyroid hormone, catecholamines work poorly.

Hormone transport and 'free vs. bound' is a subtle but testable point. Lipophilic hormones (steroids, thyroid hormone) are poorly soluble in plasma, so they travel bound to carrier proteins (albumin, sex-hormone-binding globulin, thyroxine-binding globulin). Only the FREE, unbound fraction is biologically active and can enter cells; the bound fraction is an inactive reservoir. So if a passage increases carrier-protein levels, total hormone may rise but free (active) hormone can transiently fall. Peptide and catecholamine hormones are water soluble and travel freely dissolved, needing no carrier.

Receptor regulation matters too. Target cells adjust sensitivity by changing receptor number: downregulation (fewer receptors) typically follows chronically HIGH hormone, blunting the response (this contributes to insulin resistance in type 2 diabetes, persistently high insulin leads to fewer and/or less responsive receptors). Up-regulation (more receptors) follows chronically LOW hormone, increasing sensitivity. Recognize that a tissue's responsiveness depends not just on hormone level but on receptor density.

Glucose regulation is the single most tested integrated system. After a meal, blood glucose rises; pancreatic beta cells release insulin, which drives glucose into cells (especially via GLUT4 transporters recruited in muscle and fat), promotes glycogen synthesis in liver and muscle, and stores fat, lowering blood glucose. During fasting, glucagon from alpha cells (backed by cortisol and epinephrine) breaks down glycogen (glycogenolysis) and makes new glucose (gluconeogenesis), raising blood glucose. Epinephrine and cortisol are 'stress' glucose-raisers, reinforcing glucagon. Know the direction of each arrow.

Blood pressure/volume regulation via the renin angiotensin aldosterone system (RAAS) is the other big integrated loop. When blood pressure or blood volume falls, the kidneys release renin, which converts angiotensinogen (from the liver) to angiotensin I; ACE (largely in the lungs) converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II, a potent vasoconstrictor that also stimulates aldosterone release from the adrenal cortex. Aldosterone increases Na+ (and water) reabsorption, raising blood volume and pressure. ADH adds direct water reabsorption. Together, RAAS + ADH restore blood pressure, a tidy negative-feedback loop the MCAT loves to trace.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A person with untreated type 1 diabetes (no insulin) has high blood glucose. Explain, using hormone logic, why their cells still behave as if they are 'starving' and break down fat and protein, and why glucagon's effects go unopposed.

Insulin is the only hormone that drives glucose INTO insulin-dependent cells and promotes storage. In type 1 diabetes the beta cells make no insulin, so even though blood glucose is high, glucose cannot enter insulin-dependent tissues (muscle, fat) because GLUT4 transporters are not recruited to the membrane. From those cells' perspective, glucose is unavailable, they are 'starving' despite abundant blood glucose. The body interprets this as a fasting state, so it ramps up the counter-regulatory hormones. Critically, insulin normally opposes glucagon; with insulin absent, glucagon acts UNOPPOSED. Unopposed glucagon (plus cortisol and epinephrine) maximizes glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, pushing blood glucose even higher, and triggers lipolysis (fat breakdown) and proteolysis to supply substrates. Fat breakdown floods the liver with fatty acids, which are converted to ketone bodies, producing the diabetic ketoacidosis characteristic of type 1 diabetes. The unifying insight: insulin and glucagon are an antagonistic pair, so removing insulin doesn't just reduce glucose uptake, it unleashes the full catabolic program (high blood glucose, ketones, and breakdown of fat and protein).

## TRAP

Permissiveness vs. synergism is a frequent trap. Synergism = the combined effect is GREATER than the sum of the individual effects (both hormones act, and together they amplify the response). Permissiveness = one hormone does little or nothing alone but is REQUIRED for the second to work at all (it 'permits' the action). If the passage says hormone A has no effect by itself but is needed for hormone B's full response, that's permissiveness, not synergism.

## KEY

When tracing any feedback loop in a passage, identify three things: the regulated variable (glucose, Ca2+, blood pressure, hormone X), the sensor/controller, and the direction each hormone pushes. Then ask 'what restores the setpoint?' Almost every endocrine question reduces to predicting whether a perturbation triggers a hormone that opposes (negative feedback) or amplifies (positive feedback) the change.
