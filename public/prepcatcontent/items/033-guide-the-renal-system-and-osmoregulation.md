# The Renal System and Osmoregulation

How the kidney filters blood at the glomerulus, fine-tunes that filtrate through tubular reabsorption and secretion, and uses the loop of Henle countercurrent multiplier together with hormones (ADH, aldosterone, RAAS, ANP) to regulate water balance, electrolytes, blood pressure, and acid-base status.

## KEY ESSENTIALS

## Kidney Anatomy and the Nephron

The kidneys do far more than make urine. Their jobs cluster into four categories: (1) excretion of nitrogenous and metabolic waste (urea from protein/amino acid breakdown, creatinine from muscle, uric acid from purines), (2) osmoregulation and volume control (balancing water and electrolytes such as Na+, K+, Ca2+, and phosphate), (3) acid-base regulation (reclaiming bicarbonate and excreting H+), and (4) endocrine functions: secreting renin (blood pressure), erythropoietin (red cell production), and activating vitamin D to calcitriol (the final hydroxylation that enables intestinal Ca2+ absorption). The kidneys are also a major site of gluconeogenesis during fasting.

Grossly, each kidney has an outer cortex and an inner medulla organized into renal pyramids. Filtrate drains from the pyramid tips (papillae) into minor and major calyces, then the renal pelvis, and leaves via the ureter. The two ureters carry urine to the bladder, which empties through the single urethra. Note the order of the path so you can trace it: nephron tubule to collecting duct to papilla to calyx to renal pelvis to ureter to bladder to urethra.

Blood supply is unusual and high-yield. The renal artery branches down to an afferent arteriole that feeds the glomerulus (a capillary tuft). Blood then leaves the glomerulus through an efferent arteriole, not a venule, and enters a second capillary bed, the peritubular capillaries (and the vasa recta around juxtamedullary loops), before draining to the renal vein. Two capillary beds in series connected by an arteriole make this a portal system, like the hepatic portal and hypophyseal portal systems. The kidneys receive roughly 20 to 25 percent of cardiac output.

## KEY

The arteriole-between-two-capillary-beds design is the key to renal function: the glomerular bed is built for high-pressure filtration, while the downstream peritubular bed is low-pressure and built for reabsorption. Adjusting the afferent versus efferent arteriole independently lets the kidney control filtration pressure without changing whole-body blood pressure.

The nephron is the functional unit (about a million per kidney). Filtrate travels in order through: Bowman's capsule (surrounding the glomerulus), the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), the descending then ascending loop of Henle, the distal convoluted tubule (DCT), and finally the collecting duct (shared by many nephrons). Cortical nephrons have short loops; juxtamedullary nephrons have long loops that dip deep into the medulla and are responsible for producing concentrated urine.

Where the thick ascending limb passes back by its own glomerulus sits the juxtaglomerular apparatus (JGA). It has two functional cell types: the macula densa (tubule cells that sense NaCl delivery in the distal nephron) and the juxtaglomerular (granular) cells in the afferent arteriole wall that store and release renin. The JGA links tubular flow to blood pressure control and provides tubuloglomerular feedback that helps autoregulate GFR.

## Glomerular Filtration and GFR

Filtration is bulk movement of fluid from glomerular blood into Bowman's space, driven by pressure across a three-layer barrier: (1) fenestrated capillary endothelium (large pores exclude blood cells), (2) the glomerular basement membrane (rich in negatively charged heparan sulfate), and (3) podocyte foot processes whose interdigitations form filtration slits bridged by slit diaphragms. The filtrate that results is essentially protein-free plasma.

The barrier selects by size and by charge. Water, ions, glucose, amino acids, and small wastes (urea, creatinine) pass freely; cells and large plasma proteins are held back. Because the basement membrane is negatively charged, it repels negatively charged proteins like albumin, enhancing their retention. In nephrotic syndrome, loss of this negative charge or damage to podocytes lets albumin slip through, producing proteinuria, low plasma oncotic pressure, and edema.

Filtration is governed by Starling forces. Glomerular capillary hydrostatic pressure pushes fluid out (favoring filtration). Opposing it are the hydrostatic pressure in Bowman's space and the oncotic (colloid osmotic) pressure of plasma proteins remaining in the capillary, both of which oppose filtration. Because the filtrate is normally protein-free, the oncotic pressure in Bowman's space is approximately zero and drops out of the equation.

NFP = (P_GC - P_BS) - (pi_GC - pi_BS); with pi_BS ~ 0: NFP = P_GC - P_BS - pi_GC

P_GC = glomerular capillary hydrostatic pressure (~55 mmHg); P_BS = Bowman's space hydrostatic pressure (~15 mmHg); pi_GC = glomerular plasma oncotic pressure (~30 mmHg). Net filtration pressure is roughly 55 - 15 - 30 = ~10 mmHg outward.

GFR is the volume of plasma filtered per unit time, about 125 mL/min, or roughly 180 L/day (the entire plasma volume is filtered dozens of times daily, yet only ~1 to 2 L of urine is excreted because >99 percent is reabsorbed). Renal plasma flow (RPF) is roughly 600 mL/min, so the filtration fraction, FF = GFR/RPF, is about 0.2: about one-fifth of the plasma entering the glomerulus is filtered.

GFR is tightly autoregulated over a wide range of blood pressures by two mechanisms: the myogenic response (afferent arteriole stretch triggers reflex constriction) and tubuloglomerular feedback (the macula densa senses NaCl delivery; high distal NaCl signals afferent constriction to lower GFR). Arteriolar tone is the main lever: constricting the afferent arteriole reduces inflow and lowers glomerular pressure and GFR, while constricting the efferent arteriole dams up blood within the glomerulus, raising glomerular pressure and GFR.

## TRAP

Afferent versus efferent is a classic trap. Afferent constriction lowers GFR; efferent constriction raises it (up to a point). This explains two drug effects: NSAIDs block prostaglandins that normally dilate the afferent arteriole, so they can drop GFR; ACE inhibitors block angiotensin II, which normally constricts the efferent arteriole, so they relax the efferent arteriole and can also drop GFR, especially in renal artery stenosis.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient's afferent arteriole constricts so glomerular hydrostatic pressure falls from 55 to 45 mmHg. Bowman's space pressure is 15 mmHg and glomerular oncotic pressure is 30 mmHg. What happens to net filtration pressure and GFR?

Before: NFP = 55 - 15 - 30 = 10 mmHg (favoring filtration). After: NFP = 45 - 15 - 30 = 0 mmHg. Net filtration pressure collapses to about zero, so GFR falls sharply toward zero. Afferent constriction reduces blood delivery and glomerular pressure, lowering GFR. Efferent constriction would instead raise glomerular pressure and increase GFR, illustrating how the kidney tunes filtration by adjusting the two arterioles in opposite directions.

## Tubular Reabsorption and Secretion

After filtration, the tubule reclaims what the body needs (reabsorption, filtrate back to blood) and dumps additional unwanted solutes (secretion, blood to filtrate). Nearly all transport is ultimately powered by the basolateral Na+/K+ ATPase, which keeps intracellular Na+ low so that Na+ can flow downhill into tubule cells from the lumen, dragging glucose, amino acids, and other solutes via secondary active transport (cotransport) along the way. Water and some ions follow paracellularly (between cells) as well as transcellularly.

The PCT is the workhorse, reabsorbing roughly 65 percent of filtered Na+ and water and essentially 100 percent of filtered glucose and amino acids (via sodium-glucose cotransporters, SGLT). It reclaims most filtered bicarbonate (using carbonic anhydrase, the target of acetazolamide), and it is the main site of secretion of H+, organic acids and bases, and many drugs and toxins. PCT reabsorption is isosmotic: solute and water leave together so the filtrate stays near plasma osmolarity (~300 mOsm).

Carrier-mediated reabsorption is saturable, defined by a transport maximum (Tm). Glucose normally is fully reabsorbed, but once plasma glucose pushes the filtered load above the Tm (the carriers saturate), glucose spills into the urine (glucosuria). The plasma level at which spillage begins is the renal threshold (about 180 to 200 mg/dL), below the plasma glucose at which the transport maximum is reached, because individual nephrons saturate at different loads (an effect called splay).

## TRAP

Glucosuria does not mean the kidney is broken, it means the filtered load exceeded the SGLT transport maximum. In uncontrolled diabetes, high plasma glucose overwhelms reabsorption, so glucose remains in the tubule and osmotically holds water there (osmotic diuresis), causing the classic polyuria and dehydration.

The loop of Henle splits water and salt handling. The descending limb is permeable to water but not solute, so as it dives into the salty medulla, water leaves and the filtrate becomes progressively more concentrated. The thick ascending limb is the reverse: impermeable to water but actively reabsorbing NaCl through the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter (NKCC2). This makes it the diluting segment, lowering tubular fluid osmolarity (to ~100 mOsm) while loading the medullary interstitium with salt. Loop diuretics such as furosemide block NKCC2.

The DCT continues NaCl reabsorption via the Na-Cl cotransporter (NCC, blocked by thiazide diuretics) and is a key site of PTH-regulated Ca2+ reabsorption. The collecting duct performs the final, hormonally controlled adjustments: principal cells reabsorb Na+ through ENaC and secrete K+ (both stimulated by aldosterone) and reabsorb water through aquaporin-2 channels (inserted by ADH), while intercalated cells handle acid-base by secreting H+ or HCO3-. This is where urine volume and concentration are finalized.

WORKED EXAMPLE

GFR is 125 mL/min and the glucose transport maximum is about 375 mg/min. At roughly what plasma glucose concentration is reabsorptive capacity fully exceeded?

Filtered load = GFR x plasma glucose. Set the filtered load equal to the Tm: 375 mg/min = 125 mL/min x P, so P = 375/125 = 3 mg/mL = 300 mg/dL. Above this, every transporter is saturated and excess glucose is excreted. In practice glucosuria starts earlier, near the renal threshold of 180 to 200 mg/dL, because individual nephrons saturate at slightly different loads (splay). This is why diabetics spill glucose and develop osmotic diuresis.

## The Countercurrent Multiplier and Urine Concentration

To excrete waste while conserving water, the kidney must produce urine more concentrated than plasma. It does this by maintaining a standing osmotic gradient in the medulla, from about 300 mOsm at the cortex to roughly 1200 mOsm at the deep inner medulla. A concentrated collecting duct passing through this gradient can pull water out of the filtrate by osmosis, but only if the gradient exists first.

The loop of Henle builds the gradient through countercurrent multiplication. The 'single effect' is the thick ascending limb pumping NaCl out into the interstitium without water following (it is water-impermeable). This makes the interstitium saltier, which then draws water out of the adjacent water-permeable descending limb, concentrating the fluid that will next enter the ascending limb. Because the two limbs run antiparallel (countercurrent) and the descending fluid keeps feeding the pump, a modest single effect is multiplied along the loop's length into a steep tip-to-base gradient. Longer loops (juxtamedullary nephrons) generate steeper gradients.

## KEY

Separate the two steps: the loop of Henle BUILDS the medullary gradient (a permanent feature, independent of hydration), and ADH USES that gradient to decide how much water to reabsorb in the collecting duct. ADH does not create the gradient; it controls collecting-duct water permeability.

Urea recycling sharpens the gradient. The inner medullary collecting duct (under ADH) is permeable to urea, which diffuses into the deep interstitium and adds to its osmolarity. Some urea reenters the thin loop and is recycled, trapping it in the medulla. Urea therefore contributes a large share of the inner medullary gradient alongside NaCl, which is why severely protein-deprived individuals concentrate urine less effectively.

The vasa recta (the long, hairpin capillaries paralleling juxtamedullary loops) act as a countercurrent exchanger. Because blood descends and ascends through the same gradient, solutes and water passively shuttle between the limbs, so blood leaving the medulla is nearly back to its entry osmolarity. This sluggish, U-shaped flow supplies the medulla with oxygen and nutrients while washing out minimal solute, preserving the gradient that the loop worked to build.

Antidiuretic hormone (ADH, vasopressin) sets the final urine concentration. When ADH is high (dehydration), it binds V2 receptors on collecting-duct principal cells and triggers insertion of aquaporin-2 channels into the luminal membrane, making the duct water-permeable; water flows out into the hyperosmotic medulla, yielding a small volume of concentrated urine (up to ~1200 mOsm). When ADH is low (overhydration), aquaporins are withdrawn, the duct stays water-impermeable, and dilute urine (as low as ~50 mOsm) is excreted in large volume. Lack of functional ADH causes diabetes insipidus: copious dilute urine.

## TRAP

Do not flip the loop limbs. The DESCENDING limb is permeable to water (water leaves, filtrate concentrates) but not solute; the ASCENDING limb is the opposite (solute leaves via NKCC2, water cannot follow, filtrate dilutes). Mixing these up reverses the entire concentrating mechanism on test day.

## Hormonal Control of Volume and Osmolarity

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) defends blood pressure and volume. Three signals make juxtaglomerular cells release renin: low afferent arteriole pressure, low NaCl sensed at the macula densa, and sympathetic (beta-1) stimulation. Renin is the rate-limiting enzyme; it cleaves liver-derived angiotensinogen into angiotensin I, which angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), mainly in pulmonary capillaries, converts to active angiotensin II.

Angiotensin II is a multi-target pressor hormone. It causes systemic arteriolar vasoconstriction (raising blood pressure), preferentially constricts the efferent arteriole (preserving GFR when renal perfusion drops), stimulates the adrenal cortex to release aldosterone, stimulates ADH release and thirst, and directly increases Na+/H+ exchange in the PCT to reabsorb more Na+. The net effect is to retain salt and water and raise blood pressure.

Aldosterone (a mineralocorticoid from the adrenal cortex, zona glomerulosa) acts on principal cells of the late distal tubule and collecting duct, upregulating ENaC and the basolateral Na+/K+ ATPase. It increases Na+ reabsorption (water follows osmotically) and K+ secretion, and indirectly promotes H+ secretion. Its key triggers are angiotensin II and, importantly, elevated plasma K+ directly. Aldosterone primarily defends blood volume and Na+ balance.

ADH (vasopressin) primarily defends plasma osmolarity. Hypothalamic osmoreceptors (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei) trigger ADH release from the posterior pituitary when plasma osmolarity rises even slightly; large drops in blood volume or pressure (sensed by baroreceptors) also drive ADH. ADH increases collecting-duct water reabsorption via aquaporin-2 and, at high concentrations, causes vasoconstriction. The contrast is useful: ADH reabsorbs pure water (changing concentration), whereas aldosterone reabsorbs Na+ with water following (changing volume).

Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), released by cardiac atria when stretched by high blood volume (and the related BNP from ventricles), opposes RAAS. ANP promotes natriuresis and diuresis (Na+ and water excretion) by dilating the afferent arteriole to raise GFR and by inhibiting Na+ reabsorption, and it suppresses renin and aldosterone and causes vasodilation. Think of ANP as the body's 'too much volume' signal that lowers blood pressure.

## TRAP

Aldosterone and ADH are easy to conflate. Aldosterone moves Na+ (volume regulation, with water following the salt), is triggered by angiotensin II and high K+, and comes from the adrenal cortex. ADH moves pure water (osmolarity regulation, via aquaporins), is triggered chiefly by plasma osmolarity, and comes from the posterior pituitary. They often act together but answer different physiologic questions.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A trauma patient acutely loses 1.5 L of blood. Predict the hormonal response and the resulting urine.

Falling blood volume and pressure reduce afferent arteriole stretch and NaCl delivery to the macula densa, while sympathetic tone rises, all stimulating renin release. Renin generates angiotensin II: systemic and efferent vasoconstriction (defending GFR), aldosterone secretion (Na+ retention, K+ loss), and stimulation of ADH and thirst. Baroreceptor-driven ADH adds aquaporin-2 to the collecting duct, reabsorbing water. The result is a low volume of concentrated urine as the body retains Na+ and water to restore blood volume. ANP would be low because atrial stretch is reduced.

## Clearance, Acid-Base Balance, and Integration

Renal clearance is the volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per unit time. It is a powerful conceptual tool: by comparing a substance's clearance to GFR, you can deduce whether the tubules net-reabsorb it, net-secrete it, or neither. Clearance lets clinicians estimate GFR and renal plasma flow noninvasively.

C_x = (U_x * V) / P_x

C_x = clearance of substance x (mL/min); U_x = urine concentration; V = urine flow rate (mL/min); P_x = plasma concentration. The numerator (U_x * V) is the amount excreted per minute.

Marker substances calibrate this. Inulin is freely filtered and neither reabsorbed nor secreted, so its clearance equals GFR exactly. Creatinine (an endogenous muscle byproduct) is freely filtered with only slight secretion, so creatinine clearance is a practical clinical estimate of GFR. Para-aminohippuric acid (PAH) is filtered and almost completely secreted, so nearly all PAH entering the kidney is removed in one pass, making PAH clearance a good estimate of renal plasma flow (RPF).

TIP

Use inulin (= GFR) as your reference line. If a substance's clearance is GREATER than GFR, the tubules must add it by net secretion (like PAH). If clearance is LESS than GFR, there is net reabsorption (like glucose, whose clearance is ~0 when fully reabsorbed). Clearance equal to GFR means filtered only (inulin).

WORKED EXAMPLE

Inulin is infused to steady state: plasma = 1 mg/mL, urine = 120 mg/mL, urine flow = 1 mL/min. A drug X measured at the same time has plasma = 0.2 mg/mL and urine = 60 mg/mL. Find GFR and decide whether X is net secreted or reabsorbed.

GFR = inulin clearance = (U x V)/P = (120 x 1)/1 = 120 mL/min. Clearance of X = (60 x 1)/0.2 = 300 mL/min. Because C_X (300) is greater than GFR (120), more X leaves in urine than was filtered, so the tubules must add X by net secretion. Had its clearance been below 120, the verdict would be net reabsorption; equal to 120 would mean filtered only, like inulin.

The kidney is the slow but powerful partner in acid-base homeostasis (the lungs handle the fast CO2 component). Two coordinated jobs maintain blood pH near 7.4. First, the PCT reabsorbs about 80 to 90 percent of filtered bicarbonate (the rest is reclaimed downstream, so almost none is lost in urine), using carbonic anhydrase to interconvert CO2/H2CO3/HCO3- across the cell. Second, the kidney excretes the daily fixed-acid load by secreting H+ in the distal nephron.

Secreted H+ cannot be excreted as free ions in large amounts (urine pH bottoms out near 4.5), so it is buffered. Titratable acids (mainly phosphate) trap H+, and the kidney also produces ammonia/ammonium: tubule cells deaminate glutamine to make NH3, which binds H+ to form NH4+ that is excreted. Each H+ excreted this way regenerates a new bicarbonate added to the blood, replacing buffer consumed by metabolic acids. This is why renal compensation for a respiratory acid-base disorder (retaining or excreting HCO3-) takes hours to days, whereas respiratory compensation for a metabolic disorder is nearly immediate.

Putting it together: filtration delivers a large, indiscriminate volume to the tubule; reabsorption and secretion sculpt its composition; the countercurrent gradient and ADH set its final concentration; and the RAAS/ANP axis tunes Na+ and volume to defend blood pressure. The bladder then stores urine until the micturition reflex (parasympathetic detrusor contraction with relaxation of the internal sphincter, plus voluntary relaxation of the external urethral sphincter) empties it. Across these layers the kidney simultaneously manages water, electrolytes, blood pressure, waste, and pH.
