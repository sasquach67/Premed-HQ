# Psychological Disorders

Psychological disorders cover how abnormal behavior is defined, classified (DSM-5), and explained through competing models, with detailed symptom profiles and biological correlates for mood, anxiety, and psychotic disorders. The MCAT tests this because it integrates biology (neurotransmitters), psychology (symptoms/criteria), and sociology (stigma, epidemiology, cultural context).

## KEY ESSENTIALS

Biopsychosocial model (NOT pure biomedical) dominates: disorders arise from interacting biological + psychological + social factors. Its mechanism is diathesis-stress: predisposition (diathesis) + trigger (stressor) → onset.
DSM-5 reclassification is the #1 trap: OCD and PTSD are NOT anxiety disorders, they have their own categories. MDD = ≥5/9 symptoms ≥2 weeks, must include depressed mood OR anhedonia (SIG E CAPS; ↓ serotonin/norepinephrine).
Mania (≥1 wk, marked impairment, may have psychosis) → Bipolar I; hypomania (≥4 days, no marked impairment, no psychosis) + MDE → Bipolar II. One lifetime manic episode = Bipolar I regardless of depression.
Schizophrenia: POSITIVE symptoms (additions: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech) ↔ EXCESS dopamine in the MESOLIMBIC pathway (D2-blockers treat them); NEGATIVE symptoms (5 A's) ↔ low MESOCORTICAL dopamine.
Personality clusters: A = odd/eccentric (Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal); B = dramatic (Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic); C = anxious (Avoidant, Dependent, OCPD). OCPD ≠ OCD (ego-syntonic, no true obsessions/compulsions).

## Defining Abnormality: Biomedical vs Biopsychosocial Models

Before classifying any disorder, you must understand what makes a behavior "abnormal." Clinicians generally rely on four criteria, often summarized as the "4 Ds": Deviance (the behavior violates cultural or statistical norms), Distress (it causes the individual subjective suffering), Dysfunction (it impairs daily functioning, work, relationships, self-care), and Danger (risk of harm to self or others). No single criterion is sufficient: a skydiver is statistically deviant but not disordered, and grief is distressing but normal. The MCAT loves to point out that abnormality is partly culturally relative, behavior considered pathological in one society may be normative in another. Dysfunction (clinically significant impairment) is the criterion the DSM weights most heavily.

Once a behavior is deemed abnormal, two explanatory frameworks compete to explain WHY it occurs. The biomedical (medical) model treats psychological disorders strictly as diseases of the body, specifically the brain, caused by genetics, biochemistry (neurotransmitter imbalances), and anatomy. It is reductionist: fix the biology (e.g., with medication), and you fix the disorder. Its strength is that it has driven pharmacological breakthroughs and reduced blame (the disorder is an illness, not a moral failing); its weakness is that it largely ignores life experience, environment, and meaning.

The biopsychosocial model, now the dominant framework in psychology, holds that disorders emerge from the dynamic interaction of three sets of factors: BIOLOGICAL (genes, neurotransmitters, hormones, brain structure), PSYCHOLOGICAL (thoughts, emotions, learned behaviors, coping styles, personality), and SOCIAL (culture, family, socioeconomic status, trauma, social support). The same depressive episode might reflect a genetic vulnerability, a pessimistic cognitive style, and a recent job loss all at once. This model explains why two people with similar brain chemistry can have very different outcomes, and why treatment is most effective when it combines medication, psychotherapy, and social support.

Biomedical vs Biopsychosocial Model

Feature	Biomedical Model	Biopsychosocial Model
Core cause	Brain/body disease (biology only)	Interaction of biological, psychological, and social factors
View of patient	Passive recipient of treatment	Active participant; context matters
Typical treatment	Medication, somatic therapies	Combined: meds + psychotherapy + social support
Strength	Drove drug development; reduces blame	Holistic, individualized, often better outcomes
Weakness	Ignores environment and experience	Harder to test/operationalize

## KEY

The diathesis-stress model is the biopsychosocial model's flagship mechanism. A diathesis is a biological or psychological predisposition (vulnerability); stress is an environmental trigger. A disorder appears only when a sufficient stressor acts on a vulnerable individual. Someone with high genetic risk for schizophrenia may stay healthy in a low-stress life but develop it after major stressors, explaining why genes alone are not destiny.

## TRAP

The MCAT will try to make you equate 'biopsychosocial' with 'biological.' They are NOT the same, the biopsychosocial model explicitly REJECTS pure biological reductionism. If a passage describes treatment that addresses family dynamics and social stigma alongside medication, that is biopsychosocial, not biomedical.

## Classifying Disorders: The DSM-5 System

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition (DSM-5, published 2013) is the standard classification system used in the United States. It is descriptive and largely atheoretical, it groups disorders by observable symptoms and required durations rather than by presumed cause, so a clinician of any theoretical orientation can use it. (The World Health Organization's ICD is the international counterpart you may see referenced.) The DSM provides explicit diagnostic criteria: a list of symptoms, a minimum number that must be present, a minimum duration, and a requirement that the symptoms cause clinically significant distress or impairment.

A crucial DSM-5 update the MCAT tests: it ELIMINATED the old multiaxial system (Axes I V) used in DSM-IV. It also reorganized several categories. Most importantly for the exam, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) were REMOVED from the anxiety disorders category and given their own chapters: 'Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders' and 'Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders,' respectively. Knowing this reclassification is a classic high yield discriminator.

Major DSM-5 Categories (High-Yield)

Category	Example Disorders	Defining Feature
Depressive disorders	MDD, persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia)	Sad/empty/irritable mood + somatic and cognitive changes
Bipolar and related	Bipolar I, Bipolar II, cyclothymia	Episodes of mania/hypomania (± depression)
Anxiety disorders	GAD, panic disorder, specific phobia, social anxiety, agoraphobia	Excessive fear/anxiety + avoidance
Obsessive-compulsive & related	OCD, body dysmorphic, hoarding	Obsessions and/or compulsions
Trauma- & stressor-related	PTSD, acute stress disorder, adjustment disorder	Onset after an identifiable stressor/trauma
Schizophrenia spectrum & psychotic	Schizophrenia, schizoaffective, brief psychotic	Psychosis: loss of contact with reality
Dissociative disorders	Dissociative identity, dissociative amnesia, depersonalization/derealization	Disruption of identity, memory, or consciousness

Personality disorders	Clusters A, B, C (10 total)	Enduring, inflexible, maladaptive personality patterns

TIP

The DSM is descriptive (symptom-based) and improves diagnostic reliability, but the MCAT may critique it for medicalizing normal variation, cultural bias, high comorbidity (people often meet criteria for several disorders at once), and the influence of pharmaceutical interests. Knowing both the utility AND the criticisms lets you answer reasoning questions about classification.

### FIGURE: DSM-5 DIAGNOSTIC DECISION FLOW

A funnel diagram. At the top, an observed behavior is filtered through the 4 Ds (deviance, distress, dysfunction, danger). If flagged, it enters DSM-5 evaluation, where it is matched against a specific category's symptom checklist (number of symptoms required), a duration threshold (e.g., 2 weeks for MDD, 6 months for GAD), and the impairment/distress requirement. ## Mood Disorders: Depressive and Bipolar

Mood (affective) disorders are split in DSM-5 into two families: depressive disorders (mood is persistently down) and bipolar disorders (mood swings between elevated and depressed poles). The single most important distinction the MCAT tests is that the presence of even ONE lifetime manic episode shifts a diagnosis out of the depressive category and into bipolar I, depressive episodes alone are never enough to diagnose bipolar disorder.

MAJOR DEPRESSIVE DISORDER (MDD) requires at least 5 of 9 symptoms present nearly every day for at least 2 weeks, and at least one of the five must be either (1) depressed mood or (2) anhedonia (markedly diminished interest or pleasure). The symptoms are captured by the mnemonic SIG E CAPS: Sleep changes (insomnia or hypersomnia), Interest loss (anhedonia), Guilt/worthlessness, Energy loss (fatigue), Concentration difficulty, Appetite/weight change, Psychomotor agitation or retardation, and Suicidal ideation. PERSISTENT DEPRESSIVE DISORDER (dysthymia) is a milder but chronic depressed mood lasting at least 2 years in adults (1 year in children/adolescents), longer duration, generally fewer or less severe symptoms than a full MDD episode.

A MANIC EPISODE is a distinct period of abnormally elevated, expansive, OR irritable mood PLUS increased energy or goal-directed activity, lasting at least 1 week (or any duration if hospitalization is required), with at least 3 additional symptoms (4 if mood is only irritable). The symptoms follow the mnemonic DIG FAST: Distractibility, Indiscretion/Impulsivity (risky pleasurable activity), Grandiosity, Flight of ideas, Activity increase, Sleep decrease (decreased NEED for sleep), Talkativeness (pressured speech). Mania causes marked impairment and may include psychotic features. HYPOMANIA has the same symptom set but lasts at least 4 days, causes NO marked impairment, requires no hospitalization, and never includes psychosis.

Bipolar Spectrum Comparison

Disorder	Mania	Hypomania	Major Depressive Episode
Bipolar I	Required (≥1 episode)	May occur	Common but NOT required
Bipolar II	Never (a manic episode would make it Bipolar I)	Required (≥1 episode)	Required (≥1 episode)
Cyclothymia	No	Hypomanic symptoms (subthreshold)	Depressive symptoms (subthreshold); ≥2 yrs

Biological correlates are heavily tested. Depression is associated with LOW activity of the monoamine neurotransmitters serotonin and norepinephrine (the monoamine hypothesis), which is why SSRIs and SNRIs are first-line treatments. Depression also correlates with elevated cortisol (HPA-axis dysregulation) and reduced hippocampal volume. Seasonal affective disorder (a specifier, 'with seasonal pattern') links to reduced light exposure and is treated with light therapy. Bipolar disorder is highly heritable and classically treated with the mood stabilizer lithium.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A 24-year-old reports that for the past 5 days he has felt 'on top of the world,' has slept only 3 hours a night without feeling tired, started three businesses, and has been talking so fast his friends can't follow. He continues to work and has not been hospitalized, and there are no hallucinations or delusions. He reports a past episode of 2 weeks of depressed mood with anhedonia. Which diagnosis is most consistent, and what single feature decides between the two main candidate episode types?

The current symptoms, elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, increased goal-directed activity, pressured speech, describe an elevated-mood episode. The decisive features are DURATION and IMPAIRMENT: the episode is 5 days (≥4 but <7 days), causes NO marked impairment, requires no hospitalization, and has no psychosis. That defines a HYPOMANIC episode, not a manic one (mania requires ≥1 week, or any duration if hospitalization is needed, and causes marked impairment). Because his worst lifetime mood elevation is hypomania AND he has had a major depressive episode, the diagnosis is BIPOLAR II. If the same elevated symptoms had instead lasted ≥7 days with marked impairment (or required hospitalization, or included psychosis), it would be a manic episode and the diagnosis would shift to BIPOLAR I, regardless of whether depression ever occurred. The single deciding feature between the episode types is the severity/duration threshold (mania vs hypomania).

## TRAP

A common MCAT trap: assuming 'decreased sleep' in mania means insomnia (an inability to sleep that causes fatigue). In mania it is a DECREASED NEED for sleep, the person feels rested and energetic on little sleep. In depression, sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia) is tied to fatigue and low energy. Same surface feature, opposite functional meaning.

## Anxiety, OCD, and Trauma-Related Disorders

Anxiety disorders share a core of excessive, persistent fear (a response to a present threat) or anxiety (anticipation of a future threat) that is out of proportion to the actual danger and leads to avoidance. GENERALIZED ANXIETY DISORDER (GAD) is chronic, free-floating, difficult-to-control worry about many things, present more days than not for at least 6 months, with physical symptoms (restlessness, fatigue, muscle tension, irritability, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance). PANIC DISORDER involves recurrent, unexpected panic attacks, sudden surges of intense fear peaking within minutes with palpitations, sweating, shortness of breath, and a feeling of impending doom, PLUS at least 1 month of persistent worry about having more attacks or a maladaptive change in behavior to avoid them.

PHOBIAS are marked, persistent, excessive fears of specific objects or situations. SPECIFIC PHOBIA targets a particular trigger (heights, spiders, needles). SOCIAL ANXIETY DISORDER (social phobia) is fear of social scrutiny or embarrassment. AGORAPHOBIA is fear of two or more situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable (crowds, open spaces, public transit, lines, enclosed places), often leading to housebound avoidance. The biological signature of anxiety disorders centers on the amygdala (the brain's fear/threat detector) and on the neurotransmitter GABA (the main inhibitory transmitter, low GABA activity → under-braked anxiety, which is why benzodiazepines, which potentiate GABA-A signaling, reduce acute anxiety). Serotonin is also implicated, so SSRIs are first-line for chronic anxiety.

## KEY

Distinguish a PANIC ATTACK from PANIC DISORDER. A panic attack is a discrete event (the surge of fear) that can occur in many conditions, it is a symptom, not a diagnosis. Panic DISORDER requires recurrent UNEXPECTED attacks plus ≥1 month of worry about future attacks or a behavior change to avoid them. A single attack triggered by a real stressor is not panic disorder.

OBSESSIVE-COMPULSIVE DISORDER (its own DSM-5 category) features OBSESSIONS, intrusive, unwanted, recurrent thoughts, urges, or images that cause anxiety (e.g., contamination fears), and/or COMPULSIONS, repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to neutralize the anxiety (e.g., hand-washing, checking, counting). The compulsion provides temporary relief, which negatively reinforces the cycle. Related disorders include body dysmorphic disorder and hoarding disorder. OCD is linked to abnormal activity in the orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and basal ganglia (caudate), and to serotonin dysfunction (SSRIs are first-line).

POST-TRAUMATIC STRESS DISORDER (in the trauma- and stressor-related category) follows exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. Its four symptom clusters are: (1) INTRUSION (flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories), (2) AVOIDANCE of trauma reminders, (3) negative alterations in COGNITION and MOOD (numbing, detachment, distorted blame, persistent negative emotions), and (4) AROUSAL/reactivity changes (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, irritability). Symptoms must persist more than 1 month. If the same picture lasts only 3 days to 1 month, it is ACUTE STRESS DISORDER. PTSD involves a hyperactive amygdala, a hypoactive prefrontal cortex (impaired top-down fear regulation), and reduced hippocampal volume.

## TRAP

High-yield trap: OCD and PTSD are NOT anxiety disorders in DSM-5. The reorganization moved OCD into 'Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders' and PTSD into 'Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders.' If an answer choice lists OCD or PTSD under 'anxiety disorders,' it is testing whether you know the DSM-5 reclassification.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A combat veteran, 3 months after returning home, experiences vivid nightmares of an explosion, avoids fireworks and crowds, feels emotionally numb toward his family, and startles violently at loud noises. He also worries constantly about money, work, and his children's safety on most days. A clinician must decide whether this is best captured by PTSD, GAD, or both. Reason through it.

First, identify the PTSD pattern. There is a qualifying trauma (combat, threatened death/serious injury) and all four clusters: INTRUSION (nightmares of the explosion), AVOIDANCE (fireworks, crowds), negative COGNITION/MOOD (emotional numbing toward family), and AROUSAL (exaggerated startle). Duration is 3 months (>1 month), so this is PTSD, not acute stress disorder. Separately, the chronic, hard-to-control worry about MULTIPLE everyday domains (money, work, children) on most days resembles GENERALIZED ANXIETY DISORDER, but GAD requires the worry to persist at least 6 months AND not be better explained by another disorder. Because comorbidity is common, the cleanest answer is: the trauma-linked symptoms are PTSD, and GAD can be diagnosed comorbidly ONLY IF the generalized worry independently meets the 6-month threshold and is not simply part of the PTSD arousal/negative-mood symptoms. The vignette gives 3 months since return, so a separate GAD diagnosis is not yet established. The key teaching point: PTSD is anchored to a specific trauma and shows the four clusters, whereas GAD is free-floating worry across many domains unrelated to a single event.

## Schizophrenia and Psychotic Disorders

Psychosis is a loss of contact with reality. SCHIZOPHRENIA is the prototypical psychotic disorder: diagnosis requires ≥2 of five core symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior, negative symptoms), with at least one being delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech, present for a significant portion of 1 month, and with continuous signs of disturbance for at least 6 months. Onset is typically late adolescence to early adulthood, somewhat earlier in men. Related disorders along the spectrum include schizophreniform disorder (1 6 months), brief psychotic disorder (<1 month, often post-stressor), and schizoaffective disorder (psychosis plus a major mood episode, with psychosis also occurring in the absence of the mood episode).

Schizophrenia symptoms are grouped into three types, and the MCAT cares deeply about the positive/negative distinction. POSITIVE symptoms are ADDITIONS to normal experience, they are 'present' but shouldn't be: hallucinations (false perceptions, most commonly auditory voices), delusions (fixed false beliefs, e.g., persecution, grandeur, reference, control), and disorganized speech (loose associations, word salad). NEGATIVE symptoms are SUBTRACTIONS, normal functions that are reduced or absent: flat/blunted affect, avolition (lack of motivation), alogia (poverty of speech), anhedonia, and asociality. COGNITIVE symptoms include impaired attention, working memory, and executive function. Negative and cognitive symptoms are harder to treat and predict worse functional outcomes.

Positive vs Negative vs Cognitive Symptoms

Symptom type	Meaning	Examples	Mnemonic anchor
Positive	Added abnormal behaviors (excess relative to normal function)	Hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior	'P for Present/Plus'
Negative	Loss of normal behaviors (deficit)	Flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia, asociality	The '5 A's'
Cognitive	Impaired thinking	Poor attention, working memory, executive dysfunction	Subtle, often early

The DOPAMINE HYPOTHESIS is the central biological explanation. It states that POSITIVE symptoms result from EXCESS dopamine activity in the MESOLIMBIC pathway. Evidence: classic (typical) antipsychotics are dopamine D2 receptor ANTAGONISTS and reduce positive symptoms, whereas drugs that increase dopamine (amphetamines, L-DOPA) can induce psychosis. A refinement holds that NEGATIVE symptoms relate to DEFICIENT dopamine in the MESOCORTICAL pathway (prefrontal cortex). Other findings: enlarged ventricles and reduced gray matter, glutamate (NMDA) dysfunction, and strong genetic loading (monozygotic-twin concordance roughly 40 50% vs ~1% population prevalence), a textbook diathesis-stress picture.

## KEY

Memorize the mapping: POSITIVE symptoms ↔ EXCESS dopamine in the MESOLIMBIC pathway; typical antipsychotics block D2 receptors to treat them. NEGATIVE/cognitive symptoms ↔ relatively LOW dopamine in the MESOCORTICAL pathway and are poorly controlled by typical antipsychotics. Atypical antipsychotics add serotonin (5-HT2A) antagonism and address negative symptoms somewhat better.

## TRAP

Do not confuse 'positive' with 'good' or 'negative' with 'bad.' Positive symptoms are not desirable, the words refer to ADDITION (positive) versus SUBTRACTION (negative) relative to normal functioning. Also, disorganized speech is a POSITIVE symptom, while alogia (poverty of speech) is a NEGATIVE symptom, both involve speech but sit on opposite sides of the divide.

### FIGURE: DOPAMINE PATHWAYS IN SCHIZOPHRENIA

A sagittal brain schematic showing two dopaminergic tracts. The MESOLIMBIC pathway runs from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the limbic system (e.g., nucleus accumbens); it is shown OVERACTIVE (hyperdopaminergic), producing positive symptoms like hallucinations and delusions. The MESOCORTICAL pathway runs from the VTA to the prefrontal cortex; it is shown UNDERACTIVE (hypodopaminergic), producing negative and cognitive symptoms. The figure teaches that a single neurotransmitter (dopamine) can be simultaneously too high in one circuit and too low in another, explaining why blanket D2 blockade helps positive symptoms but can worsen negative ones.

## Personality, Dissociative, and Somatic Disorders

PERSONALITY DISORDERS are enduring, pervasive, inflexible patterns of inner experience and behavior that deviate from cultural expectations, are stable over time, typically trace back to adolescence or early adulthood, and cause distress or impairment. They are often EGO-SYNTONIC (the person sees the traits as part of who they are rather than as a problem), which contrasts with the ego-dystonic nature of, say, OCD. DSM-5 groups the 10 personality disorders into three clusters by descriptive theme.

Personality Disorder Clusters

Cluster	Theme	Disorders	Quick descriptor
A	Odd / eccentric ('weird')	Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal	Distrustful; detached loner; eccentric/magical thinking
B	Dramatic / emotional / erratic ('wild')	Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic	Rule-breaking; unstable; attention-seeking; grandiose
C	Anxious / fearful ('worried')	Avoidant, Dependent, Obsessive-Compulsive (OCPD)	Fear of rejection; clingy; rigid perfectionism

## TRAP

OCPD (obsessive-compulsive PERSONALITY disorder, Cluster C) is NOT the same as OCD. OCPD is a pervasive, ego-syntonic pattern of perfectionism, orderliness, and control WITHOUT true obsessions or compulsions; the person sees their rigidity as appropriate and is rarely distressed by it. OCD involves intrusive, ego-dystonic obsessions and ritualistic compulsions the person recognizes as excessive and wants to stop. The MCAT exploits this overlap constantly.

DISSOCIATIVE DISORDERS involve a disruption of the normally integrated functions of consciousness, memory, identity, or perception, often as a defense against trauma. DISSOCIATIVE AMNESIA is an inability to recall important personal information, typically about a traumatic or stressful event, beyond ordinary forgetting (sometimes with dissociative fugue, sudden, unexpected travel with amnesia for one's identity). DISSOCIATIVE IDENTITY DISORDER (DID) involves two or more distinct personality states with gaps in memory; it is strongly associated with severe early childhood trauma. DEPERSONALIZATION/DEREALIZATION disorder is persistent or recurrent feelings of detachment from one's own body or self (depersonalization) or of one's surroundings feeling unreal (derealization), with reality testing intact.

SOMATIC SYMPTOM AND RELATED DISORDERS feature physical symptoms or health concerns that are disproportionate to, or unexplained by, a medical condition. In SOMATIC SYMPTOM DISORDER, one or more distressing bodily symptoms drive excessive thoughts, anxiety, and behavior about them. ILLNESS ANXIETY DISORDER (formerly hypochondriasis) is preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness despite minimal or no actual somatic symptoms. CONVERSION DISORDER (functional neurological symptom disorder) presents with neurological symptoms (paralysis, blindness, nonepileptic seizures) that are incompatible with recognized disease, often arising after psychological stress. The key MCAT point: these symptoms are NOT consciously faked. That distinguishes them from FACTITIOUS DISORDER (deliberately producing or feigning symptoms to assume the sick role, an internal motivation) and from MALINGERING (faking for external gain such as money or avoiding work, not a mental disorder).

WORKED EXAMPLE

A patient suddenly develops paralysis of the right arm after a severe argument with a family member. Extensive neurological testing finds no organic cause, and the deficit does not follow known anatomical nerve distributions. The patient is genuinely distressed and is not seeking any external reward. Which condition best fits, and how would you rule out the two main 'faking' alternatives?

The picture, a neurological deficit (paralysis) that is incompatible with recognized neurological disease, follows a psychological stressor, and is not under voluntary control, is CONVERSION DISORDER (functional neurological symptom disorder). To rule out the alternatives, apply two questions. (1) Is the symptom intentionally produced? Here, no, the patient is not consciously generating it, which excludes both factitious disorder and malingering (both require intentional production). (2) Is there an external incentive? No money, no escape from duty, no legal benefit, which specifically excludes MALINGERING (defined by external gain). FACTITIOUS DISORDER would involve deliberately producing or feigning symptoms to occupy the SICK ROLE (an internal psychological motivation, with no external reward), also excluded, because the symptom here is not voluntary. So the genuine, unintentional, stress-triggered, anatomically-implausible deficit points to conversion disorder.

TIP

Build a quick 'intent ladder' for unexplained symptoms: Conversion/somatic (NOT intentional, no external gain) → Factitious (intentional, INTERNAL gain = the sick role, no external reward) → Malingering (intentional, EXTERNAL gain = money/drugs/avoidance, and NOT a disorder). Two questions, Is it intentional? For what reward?, resolve almost any MCAT vignette in this family.

## Epidemiology, Risk Factors, and MCAT Application

The MCAT integrates psychology with sociology, so know the population-level patterns. Mental disorders are common, roughly 1 in 5 U.S. adults experiences a mental illness in a given year. Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent category overall; depression (major depressive disorder) is among the leading causes of disability worldwide. There are robust sex differences: depression and most anxiety disorders are about twice as common in women, while substance use disorders and antisocial personality disorder are more common in men. Schizophrenia affects roughly 1% of the population across cultures, with somewhat earlier onset and slightly higher prevalence in men.

Risk and protective factors span the biopsychosocial spectrum. Heritability is high for many disorders (especially bipolar disorder and schizophrenia); early-life adversity and trauma, low socioeconomic status, social isolation, and substance use raise risk, while strong social support, secure attachment, and effective coping protect. Two sociological concepts are frequently tested. The SOCIAL CAUSATION hypothesis argues that the stress of low socioeconomic status CAUSES (or contributes to) mental illness. The SOCIAL DRIFT (downward drift) hypothesis argues the reverse, that mental illness (especially schizophrenia) impairs functioning and causes a DRIFT into lower socioeconomic strata. The correlation between low SES and serious mental illness is consistent with both.

Neurotransmitter and Sex/Prevalence Cheat Table

Disorder	Key neurotransmitter/biology	Sex skew / prevalence note
Depression	↓ Serotonin, ↓ norepinephrine; ↑ cortisol	~2x more common in women
Bipolar disorder	Dysregulated monoamines; highly heritable (lithium Tx)	Roughly equal; high heritability
Anxiety / panic	↓ GABA, serotonin involvement; amygdala	~2x more common in women
OCD	Serotonin; orbitofrontal basal ganglia loop	Roughly equal
Schizophrenia	↑ dopamine (mesolimbic); glutamate/NMDA dysfunction	~1% prevalence; earlier/somewhat more common in men
Alzheimer's (for contrast)	↓ Acetylcholine	More common in women (partly due to longevity)

Finally, the MCAT treats STIGMA as a sociological barrier to treatment. LABELING THEORY holds that a psychiatric diagnosis can itself become a master status, others respond to the label rather than to the person, and the labeled individual may internalize it (a self-fulfilling prophecy), worsening outcomes. This connects to the medicalization of deviance: behaviors once seen as moral or legal problems are reframed as medical ones. Understanding these concepts lets you answer integrated psych/soc questions about why people delay care, how diagnosis affects identity, and how the biopsychosocial model's 'social' arm shapes both cause and treatment.

## KEY

Pair the social-causation vs social-drift hypotheses in memory: CAUSATION = poverty/stress CAUSES illness; DRIFT = illness CAUSES poverty (downward mobility). Schizophrenia is the classic example used to argue for drift, because its early onset disrupts education and employment before a person's adult SES is fully established.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A study finds that schizophrenia is roughly four times more prevalent in the lowest income quartile than the highest. One researcher concludes that economic hardship causes schizophrenia; another argues the data are equally consistent with the opposite causal direction. Name both hypotheses and explain why this correlational design cannot distinguish them.

The first researcher invokes the SOCIAL CAUSATION hypothesis: the chronic stress, instability, and adversity of low SES trigger or contribute to schizophrenia (a diathesis-stress logic, stress acting on an underlying vulnerability). The second invokes the SOCIAL DRIFT (downward drift) hypothesis: schizophrenia typically has onset in late adolescence or early adulthood and impairs cognition and functioning, so affected individuals fail to complete education or maintain employment and DRIFT downward into the lowest income quartile. The study is CROSS-SECTIONAL and CORRELATIONAL, it measures SES and diagnosis at a single time point, so it captures only an association, not temporal order or causal direction. Without longitudinal data tracking SES BEFORE onset (e.g., parental SES at the person's birth, or an income trajectory followed across the course of illness), you cannot tell whether low SES preceded the illness (supporting causation) or followed it (supporting drift). This is a textbook 'correlation ≠ causation, and directionality is ambiguous' reasoning problem.
